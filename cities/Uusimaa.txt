'''Uusimaa''' ({{IPA|fi|ˈuːsimɑː|lang}}; {{langx|sv|Nyland}}, {{IPA|sv-FI|ˈnyːlɑnd|lang}}; both lit. 'new land') is a [[Regions of Finland|region]] of [[Finland]]. It borders the regions of [[Southwest Finland]], [[Tavastia Proper|Tavastia Proper (Kanta-Häme)]], [[Päijänne Tavastia|Päijänne Tavastia (Päijät-Häme)]], and [[Kymenlaakso]]. Finland's capital and largest city, [[Helsinki]], along with the surrounding [[Helsinki metropolitan area|metropolitan area]], are both contained in the region, and Uusimaa is Finland's most populous region. The population of Uusimaa is 1,734,000.

While predominantly [[Finnish language|Finnish-speaking]], Uusimaa has the highest total number of [[Swedish-speaking population of Finland|native speakers]] of [[Swedish language|Swedish]] in Finland even at a much lower share than two other regions.<!-- Ostrobothnia has an overall population similar to Swedish-speakers in Uusimaa and about half of those are native Swedish-speakers and Åland has a much lower population. -->

== History ==

From the time of the [[Vikings]] in the 8th century, an eastern road ran along the [[Gulf of Finland]]. The first inhabitants were [[nomad]]s.<ref name="matkalla-maakunnassa">{{cite book |last1=Paavola |first1=Arja-Leena |last2=Selén |first2=Otto |last3=Pekkanen |first3=Risto |date=2009 |title=Matkalla maakunnissa - tietoa maakunnista ja nähtävyyksistä |trans-title=Traveling in the regions - information about the regions and attractions |url= |language=fi |location=Saarijärvi |publisher=AtlasArt |isbn=978-952-5671-19-3}}, pp. 5-7</ref> The place name of [[Nuuksio]] derives from the [[Sámi languages|Sami]] word {{Lang|smi|njukča}} which means '[[swan]]'''.''<ref>{{Cite book|last=Tarkiainen|first=Kari|title=Ruotsin itämaa|publisher=[[Svenska litteratursällskapet i Finland]]|year=2010|location=Helsinki|pages=120|language=fi}}</ref> Later [[Finns proper]] and [[Tavastians]] inhabited the area. Some place names have traces of Tavastian village names, like [[Konala]], which likely derives from the older Tavastian village name ''Konhola''.<ref>{{Cite book|last1=Ainiala|first1=Terhi|title=Nimistötutkimuksen perusteet|last2=Saarelma|first2=Minna|last3=Sjöblom|first3=Paula|publisher=[[Suomalaisen Kirjallisuuden Seura]]|year=2008|location=Helsinki|pages=66|language=fi}}</ref> [[Estonians]] inhabited the region to a smaller extent, specifically for seasonal fishing.<ref name=":0">{{Cite book|last=Tarkiainen|first=Kari|title=Ruotsin itämaa|publisher=[[Svenska litteratursällskapet i Finland]]|year=2010|location=Helsinki|pages=119–136|language=fi}}</ref>

Swedish colonisation of coastal Uusimaa started after the [[Second Swedish Crusade|second crusade to Finland]] in the 13th century.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.hel.fi/hel2/kaumuseo/rakennusinventoinnit/raportit/kyl_paikkainventointi.pdf|title=Helsingin keskiaikaiset ja uuden ajan alun kylänpaikat 2011, Inventointiraportti 2011. Museovirasto|last=V.-P. Suhonen and Janne Heinonen|date=2011}}</ref><ref>{{Cite book|title=Ruotsin itämaa|last=Tarkiainen|first=Kari|publisher=Svenska litteratussällskapet i Finland|year=2010|isbn=9789515832122|location=Porvoo|pages=122–125}}</ref> The colonisation was part of converting pagan areas to [[Catholic Church|Catholicism]]. Eastern Uusimaa had its first Christian Swedish colonialists earlier than the western part, which got its colonialists in one mass transfer of people to [[Porvoo]] in the 14th century. The colonisation was supported by the Swedish kingdom and the immigrants were provided with grain seeds and cattle. They also got a four-year tax exemption from the crown.<ref name=":0" /> All the Swedish place names of Uusimaa date back to this period.<ref>{{Cite book|last1=Ainiala|first1=Terhi|title=Nimistötutkimuksen perusteet|last2=Saarelma|first2=Minna|last3=Sjöblom|first3=Paula|publisher=[[Suomalaisen Kirjallisuuden Seura]]|year=2008|location=Helsinki|pages=68|language=fi}}</ref>

The names ''Uusimaa'' and ''Nyland'', meaning 'new land' in English, derived from the Swedish colonisation era. The Swedish-language name ''Nyland'' appears in documents from the 14th century. The Finnish-language name ''Uusimaa'' appears for the first time in 1548 as ''Wsimaa'' in [[Se Wsi Testamenti|the first translation]] of the [[New Testament]] to Finnish by [[Mikael Agricola]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.uudenmaanliitto.fi/index.phtml?l=sv&s=55|title=Nytt Land, Nylands historia|author=Uusimaa Regional Council|date=May 12, 2010|access-date=May 17, 2010|language=sv|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110716074117/http://www.uudenmaanliitto.fi/index.phtml?l=sv&s=55|archive-date=July 16, 2011|url-status=dead}}</ref> Much of Uusimaa is literally new – it has risen off the [[Baltic Sea]] due to [[post-glacial rebound]].

The Finnish provinces were ceded to [[Imperial Russia]] in the [[War of Finland]] in 1809. After this, Uusimaa became the [[Uusimaa (historical province)|Province of Uusimaa]] in the old {{lang|fi|[[Län|lääni]]}} system of the newly established [[Grand Duchy of Finland]]. From 1997 to 2010, Uusimaa was a part of the [[Southern Finland|Province of Southern Finland]]. In 1994 it was divided into the [[regions of Finland|regions]] of Uusimaa and [[Eastern Uusimaa]], but in 2011 the two regions were merged as Uusimaa.<ref>{{cite web |title=Valtioneuvosto päätti Uudenmaan ja Itä-Uudenmaan maakuntien yhdistämisestä |date=October 22, 2009 |publisher=[[Ministry of Finance (Finland)|Finnish Ministry of Finance]] |language=fi |url=http://www.valtioneuvosto.fi/ajankohtaista/tiedotteet/tiedote/fi.jsp?oid=274585 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110807084712/http://www.valtioneuvosto.fi/ajankohtaista/tiedotteet/tiedote/fi.jsp?oid=274585 |archive-date=August 7, 2011 }}</ref>

== Heraldry ==
The coat of arms of the province is {{cn-span|''Azure, a boat Or between two fesses wavy Argent''|date=April 2023}} (a golden boat which is a symbol for the coastal areas, and two silver wavy [[Fess|fesses]] which are the symbol for rivers.)

Uusimaa received its coat of arms at the end of the 16th century. There is an image of the coat of arms made in 1599. In 1997, the traditional coat of arms became the official coat of arms of the region.

== Economy ==
The gross domestic product (GDP) of the region was €91.2 billion in 2018, accounting for 38.9% of Finnish economic output. GDP per capita adjusted for purchasing power was €43,500 or 144% of the EU27 average in the same year. The GDP per employee was 120% of the EU average.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://ec.europa.eu/eurostat/documents/2995521/10474907/1-05032020-AP-EN.pdf/81807e19-e4c8-2e53-c98a-933f5bf30f58|title=Regional GDP per capita ranged from 30% to 263% of the EU average in 2018|website=Eurostat}}</ref> The employment rate is 76% as of 2022, second highest in Finland. There were 836,000 workplaces in Uusimaa in 2021, over 35% of the workplaces in Finland. The most common sectors were health and social services, wholesale and retail trade as well as professional, scientific and technical activities.<ref>{{cite web |title=Työllisyystilastot |url=https://uudenmaanliitto.fi/tietoa-uudestamaasta/tilastot/tyollisyys/ |website=Uudenmaan liitto |access-date=5 December 2023 |language=Finnish}}</ref>

Uusimaa is a bilingual region, with municipalities both bilingual in [[Finnish language|Finnish]] and [[Finland Swedish|Swedish]], and monolingual in Finnish. Uusimaa's coastal areas tend to be Swedish-speaking. The traditional regional dialects of Swedish ({{Lang|sv|nyländska}}) are currently{{When|date=May 2020}} mostly spoken in [[Eastern Uusimaa]], while in the rest of the Uusimaa Swedish dialect has become more standardised.

The Finnish-speaking population started to grow when the capital of the [[Grand Duchy of Finland]] was moved from [[Turku]] to [[Helsinki]] by the Emperor of Russia [[Alexander I of Russia|Alexander I]] in 1812, and the region attracted settlers from other parts of Finland. [[Helsinki slang|Helsinki's slang]] first evolved in the late 19th century. 7.6% of the population of the region speaks the Swedish language natively.

Due to immigration, many foreign languages{{notetag|A language other than Finnish, Swedish or one of the Sami languages.}} are spoken in Uusimaa. 18% speak a foreign language as their mother tongue, the highest proportion in Finland and 58% of all foreign-language speakers in Finland. The figure was 1.1% in 1990, 3.9% in 2000, 8.0% in 2010 and 14.7% in 2020. Meanwhile, the proportion of Finnish and Swedish speakers has decreased from 87.6% and 11.3% in 1990 to 75.9% and 7.5% in 2021 respectively. On a municipal level, the highest shares of foreign speakers are in [[Vantaa]] (26.9%), [[Espoo]] (23.6%), [[Helsinki]] (19.6%) and [[Kerava]] (16.2%). The lowest share is in [[Pukkila]] (3.4%). 

The most spoken foreign languages are [[Russian language|Russian]] (2.8%), [[Estonian language|Estonian]] (1.9%), [[Arabic]] (1.4%), [[Somali language|Somali]] (1.2%) and [[English language|English]] (1.2%). Other languages include [[Albanian language|Albanian]], [[Mandarin Chinese|Chinese]], [[Persian language|Persian]], [[Kurdish languages|Kurdish]], [[Vietnamese language|Vietnamese]], [[Spanish language|Spanish]], [[Turkish language|Turkish]], [[Thai language|Thai]], [[Tagalog language|Tagalog]], [[German language|German]], [[Nepali language|Nepali]], [[Bengali language|Bengali]], [[French language|French]], [[Romanian language|Romanian]], [[Urdu]], [[Hindi]], [[Portuguese language|Portuguese]], [[Ukrainian language|Ukrainian]], [[Italian language|Italian]], [[Polish language|Polish]], [[Tamil language|Tamil]], [[Bulgarian language|Bulgarian]], [[Hungarian language|Hungarian]], [[Swahili language|Swahili]], [[Amharic]], [[Serbo-Croatian]], [[Latvian language|Latvian]], [[Japanese language|Japanese]], [[Dutch language|Dutch]], [[Sinhala language|Sinhalese]], [[Tigrinya language|Tigrinya]], [[Uzbek language|Uzbek]], [[Greek language|Greek]], [[Punjabi language|Punjabi]], [[Pashto language|Pashto]] and [[Telugu language|Telugu]], all with over 1,000 speakers.<ref>{{cite web |title=Language according to age and sex by region, 1990-2021 |url=https://pxnet2.stat.fi/PXWeb/pxweb/en/StatFin/StatFin__vrm__vaerak/statfin_vaerak_pxt_11rl.px/ |website=Statistics Finland |publisher=Statistics Finland |access-date=19 May 2022}}</ref>

== Health ==
In late March 2020, the region of Uusimaa went into lockdown to be isolated from the rest of [[COVID-19 pandemic in Finland|Finland]] due to the global [[COVID-19 pandemic in Europe|COVID-19 pandemic]] (2020/21).<ref>{{Cite web|title=Checkpoints ready: Parliament approves government plans to isolate Uusimaa|url=https://newsnowfinland.fi/domestic/checkpoints-ready-parliament-approves-government-plans-to-isolate-uusimaa|url-status=dead|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20200409202300/https://newsnowfinland.fi/domestic/checkpoints-ready-parliament-approves-government-plans-to-isolate-uusimaa|archive-date=2020-04-09|website=News Now Finland}}</ref>

== Regional council ==
The regional council is the main governing body for the region and focuses primarily on urban planning. Like all regional councils, it is mandated by law.{{cn|date=June 2023}}
