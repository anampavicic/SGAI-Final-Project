'''Kikwit''' is the largest [[Cities of the Democratic Republic of the Congo|city]] of [[Kwilu Province]], lying on the [[Kwilu River]] in the southwestern part of the [[Democratic Republic of the Congo]]. Kikwit is also known in the region under the nickname "The Mother". The population is approximately 458,000 (2017). An important [[commerce|commercial centre]], it is home to a [[stadium]] and is known for its [[traditional dance]]s, in particular the [[Bapende]] dancers whose geographic origin centers on the village of [[Gungu]]. Bapende dancers often wear traditional costumes comprising colorful masks and attire made from [[raffia]].  
[[Image:PendeMunganji.jpg|thumb|200px|The Munganji dancer is central to [[Pende people|Pende]] dance ceremonies. The full-body suit is woven from raffia thread.]]

In 1995 the city saw a serious outbreak of the deadly [[Ebola]] virus.<ref>{{cite web | url=https://www.cdc.gov/mmwr/preview/mmwrhtml/00037078.htm | title=Outbreak of Ebola Viral Hemorrhagic Fever -- Zaire, 1995 | publisher=Centers for Disease Control | date=19 May 1995 | access-date=7 April 2014}}</ref>

Singer [[King Kester Emeneya]] was born in Kikwit in 1956. In April 2014, a tribute concert held in Kikwit to honor King Kester Emeneya ended in disaster, and at least thirteen people in the stadium died in a stampede following a power failure.<ref>{{Cite news

==Transportation==
Kikwit is home to [[Kikwit Airport]], operating domestic flights to [[Kinshasa]]. Kikwit is also connected to Kinshasa by road and river transport.
