The '''City of Brussels'''{{efn|name=fn1|{{langx|fr|Ville de Bruxelles}} {{IPA|fr|vil də bʁysɛl|}} or alternatively ''Bruxelles-Ville'' {{IPA|fr|bʁysɛl vil|}}; {{langx|nl|Stad Brussel}} {{IPA|nl|stɑd ˈbrʏsəl|}} or ''Brussel-Stad'' {{IPA|nl|ˌbrʏsəl ˈstɑt|}}}} is the largest [[List of municipalities of the Brussels-Capital Region|municipality]] and historical [[City centre|centre]] of the [[Brussels|Brussels-Capital Region]], as well as the capital of the [[Flemish Region]] (from which it is [[List of capitals outside the territories they serve|separate]]) and [[Belgium]].<ref>{{cite book|title=The Belgian Constitution|date=May 2014|publisher=Belgian House of Representatives|location=Brussels, Belgium|page=63|url=http://www.const-court.be/en/basic_text/belgian_constitution.pdf|access-date=10 September 2015|archive-date=10 August 2015|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20150810142522/http://www.const-court.be/en/basic_text/belgian_constitution.pdf|url-status=dead}}</ref> The City of Brussels is also the administrative centre of the [[European Union]], as it hosts a number of principal [[Institutions of the European Union|EU institutions]] in its [[Brussels and the European Union#European Quarter|European Quarter]].{{sfn|Demey|2007}}<ref name="12010E/PRO/06">{{cite web |date=30 March 2010 |title=Protocol (No 6) on the location of the seats of the institutions and of certain bodies, offices, agencies and departments of the European Union, Consolidated version of the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union, OJ C 83, 30.3.2010, p. 265–265 |url=http://eur-lex.europa.eu/JOHtml.do?uri=OJ:C:2010:083:SOM:EN:HTML |access-date=3 August 2010 |publisher=EUR-Lex |archive-date=8 July 2012 |archive-url=https://archive.today/20120708132633/http://eur-lex.europa.eu/JOHtml.do?uri=OJ:C:2010:083:SOM:EN:HTML |url-status=live }}</ref>{{efn|name=fn2|Brussels is not [[de jure|formally declared]] capital of the EU, though its position is spelled out in the [[Treaty of Amsterdam]].}}

Besides the central historic town located within the [[Pentagon (Brussels)|Pentagon]], the City of Brussels covers some of the city's immediate outskirts within the greater Brussels-Capital Region, namely the former municipalities of [[Haren, Belgium|Haren]], [[Laeken]], and [[Neder-Over-Heembeek]] to the north, as well as the [[Avenue Louise|Avenue Louise/Louizalaan]] and the [[Bois de la Cambre|Bois de la Cambre/Ter Kamerenbos]] park to the south-east, where it borders municipalities in [[Flanders]].

{{As of|2023|1|1}}, the City of Brussels had a population of 194,291 inhabitants. The total area is {{convert|33.09|km2|abbr=on}} which gives a [[population density]] of {{convert|5704|PD/km2|abbr=on}}. As of 2007, there were 75,998 registered non-Belgians in the City.<ref>{{cite web | url=https://dofi.ibz.be/fr | title=Immigration Office &#124; IBZ }}</ref> In common with all of Brussels' municipalities, it is legally [[Multilingualism|bilingual]] (French–Dutch).

==Territorial history==
[[File:Brussel.1610.a.jpg|thumb|left|Engraving of Brussels from {{Circa|1610}}]]

Historically, the City of Brussels was simply defined, being the area within the [[second walls of Brussels]], the modern-day [[Small Ring, Brussels|Small Ring]] (Brussels' inner ring road). As the city grew, the surrounding villages grew as well, eventually growing into a contiguous city, though the local governments retained control of their respective areas.

The construction of the [[Avenue Louise|Avenue Louise/Louizalaan]] was commissioned in 1847 as a monumental avenue bordered by [[Aesculus hippocastanum|chestnut trees]] that would allow easy access to the popular recreational area of the [[Bois de la Cambre|Bois de la Cambre/Ter Kamerenbos]]. However, fierce resistance to the project was put up by the town of [[Ixelles]]—then, as now, a separate municipality (local authority) from the City of Brussels—through whose territory the avenue was to run. After years of fruitless negotiations, Brussels finally [[annexation|annexed]] the narrow band of land needed for the avenue, in addition to the Bois de la Cambre itself, in 1864. That decision accounts for the unusual shape of today's City of Brussels and for the separation of Ixelles into two separate areas.

Unlike most of the municipalities in Belgium, the ones now located in the Brussels-Capital Region were not merged with others during mergers occurring in 1964, 1970, and 1975.<ref name="roots">{{cite web|url=http://belgium.rootsweb.ancestry.com/bel/_places/bel_places.html|title=Municipalities (1795-now)|last=Picavet|first=Georges|date=29 April 2003|publisher=Georges Picavet|access-date=5 April 2009}}</ref> However, a few neighbouring municipalities have been merged into the City of Brussels, including [[Haren, Belgium|Haren]], [[Laeken]] and [[Neder-Over-Heembeek]] in 1921.<ref name="Brussels">{{cite web|url=http://belgium.rootsweb.ancestry.com/bel/2bru/index.html|title=Brussels Capital-Region|date=4 June 2005 |publisher=Georges Picavet |access-date=5 April 2009}}</ref> These comprise the northern bulge in the municipality. To the south-east is the above-mentioned strip of land along the Avenue Louise that was annexed from Ixelles. Part of the {{Lang|fr|[[Université libre de Bruxelles]]|italic=no}} (ULB)'s Solbosch campus is also part of the City of Brussels, partially accounting for the bulge in the south-eastern end.

====Central Quarter====
The origins of Brussels can be traced back to the heart of [[Saint-Géry Island|Saint-Géry/Sint-Goriks Island]], formed by the river [[Senne (river)|Senne]], on which a first [[keep]] was built around 979. Nowadays, the neighbourhood around the {{lang|fr|Halles Saint-Géry|italic=no}}/{{lang|nl|Sint-Gorikshallen|italic=no}}, a former covered market, is one of the capital's trendiest districts.<ref>{{Cite web |title=Halles Saint-Géry – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/Bruxelles_Pentagone/Place_Saint-Gery/1/32982 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref> In this Central Quarter ({{langx|fr|Quartier du Centre|link=no}}, {{langx|nl|Centrumwijk|link=no}}), there are some vestiges of the 13th-century [[Fortifications of Brussels#First walls|first walls of Brussels]], which encompassed the area between the first port on the Senne, the old [[Romanesque architecture|Romanesque]] church (later replaced by the [[Brabantine Gothic]] [[Cathedral of St. Michael and St. Gudula]]),<ref>{{Cite web |title=Cathédrale Saints-Michel-et-Gudule – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/Bruxelles_Pentagone/Parvis_Sainte-Gudule/A001/30595 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref> and the former [[Palace of Coudenberg|ducal palace of Coudenberg]] in today's Royal Quarter. At the centre of this triangle are the [[Grand-Place|Grand-Place/Grote Markt]] (Brussels' main square);<ref>{{Cite web |title=Grand-Place – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/streets/10002053 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref> the Îlot Sacré district, which takes its name from its resistance to demolition projects, itself crossed by the [[Royal Saint-Hubert Galleries]];<ref>{{Cite web |title=Galeries Royales Saint-Hubert – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/Bruxelles_Pentagone/Rue_du_Marche_aux_Herbes/90/33789 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref> the {{lang|fr|Saint-Jacques|italic=no}}/{{lang|nl|Sint-Jacobs|italic=no}} district, which welcomed pilgrims on their way to [[Santiago de Compostela]]; as well as the former [[Brussels Stock Exchange]] building, erected on the site of a former [[Franciscan]] [[convent]] whose remains have been unearthed.{{sfn|Eggericx|1997|p=26–28}}<ref>{{Cite web |title=La Bourse – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/Bruxelles_Pentagone/Place_de_la_Bourse/A001/31241 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref>

====Sablon/Zavel Quarter====
{{Main|Sablon, Brussels}}

From the [[Place Royale (Brussels)|Place Royale/Koningsplein]], the {{lang|fr|Rue de la Régence|italic=no}}/{{lang|nl|Regentschapsstraat|italic=no}} crosses the [[Sablon (Brussels)|Sablon/Zavel]] Quarter ({{langx|fr|Quartier des Sablons|link=no}}, {{langx|nl|Zavelwijk|link=no}}),<ref>{{Cite web |title=Rue de la Régence – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/streets/10001090 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref> made of the larger {{lang|fr|Grand Sablon|italic=no}}/{{lang|nl|Grote Zavel|italic=no}} ("Large Sablon") square in the north-west<ref>{{Cite web |title=Place du Grand Sablon – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/streets/10001039 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref> and the smaller {{lang|fr|Petit Sablon|italic=no}}/{{lang|nl|Kleine Zavel|italic=no}} ("Small Sablon") square and garden in the south-east,<ref>{{Cite web |title=Place du Petit Sablon – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/streets/10001078 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref> divided by the [[Church of Our Blessed Lady of the Sablon]].<ref>{{Cite web |title=Eglise Notre-Dame des Victoires au Sablon – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/Bruxelles_Pentagone/Rue_de_la_Regence/A001/30493 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref> It is a swanky district, where an antiques market is held, and in which antique and art dealers, as well as other luxury shops, have their businesses. The Sablon is also home to the [[Egmont Palace]]<ref>{{Cite web |title=Palais d'Egmont / Palais d'Arenberg et le parc – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/Bruxelles_Pentagone/Place_du_Petit_Sablon/8/30423 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref> and the [[Royal Conservatory of Brussels]].<ref>{{Cite web |title=Conservatoire Royal de Musique et ancienne résidence du directeur – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/Bruxelles_Pentagone/Rue_de_la_Regence/30/30488 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref>

====Midi–Lemonnier or Stalingrad Quarter====
It was in the heart of the Midi–Lemonnier Quarter ({{langx|fr|Quartier Midi–Lemonnier|link=no}}, {{langx|nl|Lemmonier–Zuidwijk|link=no}}), where the [[Place Rouppe|Place Rouppe/Rouppeplein]] is today, that Brussels' first South Station, the terminus of the South Line, was built in 1839. It was known as Bogards' railway station for the eponymous convent whose site it was built on, and to which the {{lang|fr|Rue des Bogards|italic=no}}/{{lang|nl|Bogaardenstraat|italic=no}} is now the only reference.<ref name=":3">{{Cite web |title=Place Rouppe – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/streets/10002107 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |title=Rue des Bogards – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/streets/10002011 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref> The former presence of a station at this location also explains the unusual width of the current {{lang|fr|Avenue de Stalingrad|italic=no}}/{{lang|nl|Stalingradlaan|italic=no}}, which goes from the square to the [[Small Ring, Brussels|Small Ring]], cleared of its train tracks since the inauguration of [[Brussels-South railway station|Brussels-South Station]], built outside the Pentagon in 1869.<ref name=":3" /><ref>{{Cite web |title=Avenue de Stalingrad – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/Bruxelles_Pentagone/Avenue_de_Stalingrad/10002118 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref> Because of this, the neighbourhood is sometimes called the Stalingrad Quarter ({{langx|fr|Quartier Stalingrad|link=no}}, {{langx|nl|Stalingradwijk|link=no}}). At the same time, following the [[covering of the Senne]], the district saw the construction of ''[[Haussmann's renovation of Paris|Haussmann]]-esque'' grand [[Central Boulevards of Brussels|central boulevards]], including the [[Boulevard Maurice Lemonnier|Boulevard Maurice Lemonnier/Maurice Lemonnierlaan]],<ref>{{Cite web |title=Boulevard Maurice Lemonnier – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/streets/10002077 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref> bordered by the [[Place Fontainas|Place Fontainas/Fontainasplein]]<ref>{{Cite web |title=Place Fontainas – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/Region_de_Bruxelles-Capitale/Place_Fontainas/10002049 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref> and the [[Place Anneessens|Place Anneessens/Anneessensplein]] (former location of the ''Old Market''),<ref>{{Cite web |title=Place Anneessens – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/Bruxelles_Pentagone/Place_Anneessens/10003005 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref> as well as by the Midi Palace.<ref>{{Cite web |title=Palais du Midi – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/buildings/32777 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref> Each Sunday morning, the Midi district hosts the second largest market in Europe.

====Senne/Zenne or Dansaert Quarter====
The damp and marshy land around the present-day {{lang|fr|Rue de la Senne|italic=no}}/{{lang|nl|Zennestraat|italic=no}} and {{lang|fr|Rue des Fabriques|italic=no}}/{{lang|nl|Fabriekstraat|italic=no}} was occupied by craftsmen since the [[Middle Ages]].<ref>{{Cite web |title=Rue de la Senne – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/streets/10003154 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |title=Rue des Fabriques – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/streets/10003062 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref> An arm of the [[Senne (river)|river]] crossed the defences of the [[Fortifications of Brussels#Second walls|second walls]] at the level of the Ninove Gate via the {{lang|fr|Petite Écluse|italic=no}}/{{lang|nl|Kleine Sluis|italic=no}} ("Small Lock"), which served as a maritime gate, an end of which remaining there until the 1960s. Later, small industries and many artisan breweries (now disappeared) established themselves in the area, as evidenced by the names of the {{lang|fr|Rue du Houblon|italic=no}}/{{lang|nl|Hopstraat|italic=no}} ("Hops Street")<ref>{{Cite web |title=Rue du Houblon – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/Bruxelles_Pentagone/Rue_du_Houblon/10003081 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref> and the {{lang|fr|Rue du Vieux Marché aux Grains|italic=no}}/{{lang|nl|Oude Graanmarktstraat|italic=no}} ("Old Grain Market Street").<ref>{{Cite web |title=Rue du Vieux Marché aux Grains – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/Bruxelles_Pentagone/Rue_du_Vieux_Marche_aux_Grains/10003172 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref> The [[Brussels Shot Tower|Shot Tower]] ({{lang|fr|Tour à Plomb}}), which was used to manufacture [[Shot (pellet)|lead shot]] for hunting,<ref>{{Cite web |title=Fonderie de la tour de plomb – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/buildings/32459 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref> and the {{lang|fr|Rue de la Poudrière|italic=no}}/{{lang|nl|Kruitmolenstraat|italic=no}} ("Gunpowder Street"),<ref>{{Cite web |title=Rue de la Poudrière – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/Bruxelles_Pentagone/Rue_de_la_Poudriere/10003136 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref> also testify to the neighbourhood's former activities. Long neglected following the relocation of businesses outside the city centre, the Senne/Zenne Quarter ({{langx|fr|Quartier de la Senne|link=no}}, {{langx|nl|Zennewijk|link=no}}) has in recent years been the object of renewed interest and is undergoing [[gentrification]] due to the many disused industrial premises being converted into [[loft]]s. The area around the {{lang|fr|Rue Antoine Dansaert|italic=no}}/{{lang|nl|Antoine Dansaertstraat|italic=no}} has become a trendy district and is attracting a younger, more well-off, and mostly Dutch-speaking population.<ref>{{Cite web |title=Rue Antoine Dansaert – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/streets/10003008 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref> This new situation, which has resulted in rising rents, is not without problems for the neighbourhood's less fortunate inhabitants.

====Quays or Maritime Quarter====

Boats coming from the river [[Scheldt]] penetrated through the former Shore Gate ({{langx|fr|Porte du Rivage|link=no}}, {{langx|nl|Oeverpoort|link=no}}), on the site of the present-day {{lang|fr|Place ====Marais–Jacqmain Quarter====
Few of the buildings in the Marais–Jacqmain Quarter ({{langx|fr|Quartier Marais–Jacqmain|link=no}}, {{langx|nl|Jacqmain–Broekwijk|link=no}}) have escaped 20th-century demolition, from the {{lang|fr|Boulevard Pachéco|italic=no}}/{{lang|nl|Pachecolaan|italic=no}}<ref>{{Cite web |title=Boulevard Pachéco – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/streets/10004083 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref> to the [[Rue Neuve (Brussels)|Rue Neuve/Nieuwstraat]].<ref name=":4">{{Cite web |title=Rue Neuve – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/streets/10004076 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref> They have given way to the State Administrative Centre, press printers, banking facilities, and commercial galleries. The current trend is to restore the neighbourhood's social mix by redeveloping former office buildings into housing. Despite the district's long-time grim aspect, the centuries-old [[Meyboom]] tradition has been maintained, and the former [[Art Nouveau]] ''Magasins Waucquez'' by [[Victor Horta]] have been preserved to house, since 1993, the [[Belgian Comic Strip Center]].<ref>{{Cite web |title=Anciens magasins Waucquez / Centre Belge de la bande dessinnée – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/Bruxelles_Pentagone/Rue_des_Sables/20/33798 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref> Another preserved islet is the 18th-century [[Neoclassical architecture|neoclassical]] [[Place des Martyrs, Brussels|Place des Martyrs/Martelaarsplein]], which has gradually been renovated.<ref>{{Cite web |title=Place des Martyrs: ensemble architectural – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/Bruxelles_Pentagone/Place_des_Martyrs/1/33663 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref> The victims of the [[Belgian Revolution|Belgian Revolution of 1830]] are buried there in an open crypt with a [[Monument to the Martyrs of the 1830 Revolution|memorial]]. Nearby is the Rue Neuve, one of Belgium's main shopping streets, with more than {{convert|1|km|abbr=on}} of stores on both its sides;<ref name=":4" /> the [[Boulevard Adolphe Max|Boulevard Adolphe Max/Adolphe Maxlaan]], a traditional 19th-century artery;<ref>{{Cite web |title=Boulevard Adolphe Max – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/streets/10004001 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref> and the [[Boulevard Émile Jacqmain|Boulevard Émile Jacqmain/Émile Jacqmainlaan]] (where the [[Théâtre national Wallonie-Bruxelles]] has been installed since 2004),<ref>{{Cite web |title=Boulevard Emile Jacqmain – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/streets/10003059 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref> close to the [[Place de Brouckère|Place de Brouckère/De Brouckèreplein]]. The latter, a very busy square, is dominated at its southern end by two block-style towers, but for the rest, it has totally ([[Hotel Métropole, Brussels|Hotel Métropole]] and its neighbour the Hotel Atlanta) or partially ([[UGC (cinema operator)|UGC cinema]]) retained its old facades.<ref>{{Cite web |title=Place De Brouckère – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/streets/10004036 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |title=Hôtel Métropole – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/buildings/32380 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |title=Hôtel Atlanta – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/buildings/33217#&gid=null&pid=6 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref>

====Freedom Quarter====
{{Main|Freedom Quarter}}

The [[Freedom Quarter]] ({{langx|fr|Quartier des Libertés|link=no}}, {{langx|nl|Vrijheidswijk|link=no}}) is situated between the [[Belgian Federal Parliament|Belgian Parliament]] and the [[Rue Royale, Brussels|Rue Royale/Koningsstraat]], not far from the crossroads with the Small Ring. Its focal point is the [[Congress Column]] (erected in memory of the [[National Congress of Belgium|National Congress]] of 1830–31, the founder of democratic liberties in Belgium), under which also lies the [[tomb of the Unknown Soldier]] with an [[eternal flame]].<ref name=":5">{{Cite web |title=Colonne du Congrès – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/Bruxelles_Pentagone/Place_du_Congres/A001/37870 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref> Not far from there is the [[Corinthia Grand Hotel Astoria|Hotel Astoria]], dating from 1911, which is currently being renovated and enlarged, to be reopened in the coming years.<ref>{{Cite web |title=Hôtel Astoria – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/Bruxelles_Pentagone/Rue_Royale/100/30553 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref> In the 19th century, the district was known as {{lang|fr|Notre-Dame-aux-Neiges|italic=no}}/{{lang|nl|Onze-Lieve-Vrouw-ter-Sneeuw|italic=no}} and was predominantly inhabited by working-class people. The authorities' desire to clean up the squalid parts of the city led to the expulsion of the population and the neighbourhood's complete destruction. A new [[Bourgeois of Brussels|bourgeois]] district was developed during the last quarter of the century. The choice was made to commemorate [[Belgian Revolution|Belgian independence]]: the {{lang|fr|Place de la Liberté|italic=no}}/{{lang|nl|Vrijheidsplein|italic=no}} ("Liberty Square"),<ref>{{Cite web |title=Place de la Liberté – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/streets/10004060 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref> the {{lang|fr|Place des Barricades|italic=no}}/{{lang|nl|Barricadenplein|italic=no}} ("Barricades' Square"),<ref>{{Cite web |title=Place des Barricades – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/Bruxelles_Pentagone/Place_des_Barricades/10004007 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref> the {{lang|fr|Rue de la Révolution|italic=no}}/{{lang|nl|Revolutiestraat|italic=no}} ("Revolution Street"),<ref>{{Cite web |title=Rue de la Révolution – Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/fr/streets/10004097 |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref> the {{lang|fr|Rue du Congrès|italic=no}}/{{lang|nl|Congresstraat|italic=no}} ("Congress Street"), etc.<ref>{{Cite web |title=Inventaire du patrimoine architectural |url=https://monument.heritage.brussels/index.php?quick_search=rue+du+congr%C3%A8s&section=buildings&lg=fr |access-date=2023-10-26 |website=monument.heritage.brussels |language=fr}}</ref> The four streets leading off the Place de la Liberté bear the names of the four constitutional liberties, symbolised by four [[allegory|allegorical]] [[bronze]] female sculptures surrounding the Congress Column: ''[[Freedom of the press|Freedom of the Press]]'', ''[[Freedom of religion|Worship]]'', ''[[Freedom of association|Association]]'' and ''[[Freedom of education|Education]]''.<ref name=":5" /> This [[Eclecticism in architecture|eclectic]] urban complex is one of the best preserved in the Pentagon.

===Eastern Quarters===

====European Quarter and Leopold Quarter====
{{Main|Leopold Quarter|Brussels and the European Union}}

The [[Brussels and the European Union#European Quarter|European Quarter]] ({{langx|fr|Quartier Européen|link=no}}, {{langx|nl|Europese Wijk|link=no}}) is located to the east of the Pentagon, around the [[Place du Luxembourg|Place du Luxembourg/Luxemburgplein]] and the [[Robert Schuman Roundabout]], and includes the smaller [[Leopold Quarter]] ({{langx|fr|Quartier Léopold|link=no}}, {{langx|nl|Leopoldswijk|link=no}}). The [[Espace Léopold|European Parliament]] was built near the Place du Luxembourg, on the site of the former Leopold Quarter railway station, and of which only the central building overlooking the square remains, having been replaced by the underground [[Brussels-Luxembourg railway station]]. The [[European Commission]], housed in the [[Berlaymont building]], is located on the Schuman Roundabout, not far from the [[Cinquantenaire|Cinquantenaire Park]]. Across the street stands the [[Justus Lipsius building]] and the [[Europa building]] (part of the ''Residence Palace''), serving as the seat of the [[European Council]] and the [[Council of the European Union|Council of the EU]]. In [[Leopold Park]], the [[House of European History]] (HEH) initiates visitors to the social history of the European continent. There is a visitor centre in the main European Parliament building, known as the ''Parliamentarium'', and another smaller one in the Justus Lipsius building for the European Council. It is accessible on certain days, by appointment. Many of the attractions in the European Quarter are free to visit.

====Squares Quarter====
The Squares Quarter ({{langx|fr|Quartier des Squares|link=no}}, {{langx|nl|Squareswijk|link=no}}) is the northern spur of the European Quarter, located between [[Saint-Josse-ten-Noode]] and the Leopold Quarter. The district is bounded by the {{lang|fr|Chaussée de Louvain|italic=no}}/{{lang|nl|Leuvense Steenweg|italic=no}} to the north, the {{lang|fr|Rue du Noyer|italic=no}}/{{lang|nl|Notelaarsstraat|italic=no}} and the {{lang|fr|Avenue de Cortenbergh|italic=no}}/{{lang|nl|Kortenberglaan|italic=no}} to the east, the {{lang|fr|Rue Joseph II|italic=no}}/{{lang|nl|Jozef II-straat|italic=no}} and the {{lang|fr|Rue Stevin|italic=no}}/{{lang|nl|Stevinstraat|italic=no}} to the south, as well as the [[Small Ring, Brussels|Small Ring]] to the west. The toponym refers to the many squares in the area, in particular the {{lang|fr|Square Ambiorix|italic=no}}/{{lang|nl|Ambiorixsquare|italic=no}}, the {{lang|fr|Square Marguerite|italic=no}}/{{lang|nl|Margaretasquare|italic=no}}, the {{lang|fr|Square Marie-Louise|italic=no}}/{{lang|nl|Maria-Louizasquare|italic=no}} and the {{lang|fr|Avenue Palmerston|italic=no}}/{{lang|nl|Palmerstonlaan|italic=no}}. The area between the Small Ring and the Square Marie-Louise is sometimes considered to be part of the Leopold Quarter.

===Northern Quarters===

====Laeken====
{{Main|Laeken}}

[[Laeken]] is a former municipality in the north of the Brussels-Capital Region, annexed by the City of Brussels in 1921. It is home to, among others, the Royal Domain of Laeken, the [[Palace of Laeken]], the [[Royal Greenhouses of Laeken]], the [[Church of Our Lady of Laeken]] (whose crypt contains the tombs of the [[Monarchy of Belgium|Belgian royal family]]) and [[Laeken Cemetery]], known for its wealth of monuments and sculptures. On the territory of Laeken also lies the [[Heysel Plateau|Heysel/Heizel Plateau]], the site of the [[World's fair|World's Fairs]] of [[Brussels International Exposition (1935)|1935]] and [[Expo 58|1958]], and which includes the [[King Baudouin Stadium]], Bruparck (with the [[Atomium]], [[Mini-Europe]] [[miniature park]] and [[Kinepolis Brussels|Kinepolis]] cinema), the Centenary Palace, home to the Brussels Exhibition Centre ([[Brussels Expo]]), and the [[Port of Brussels]], next to which the ''Monument to Work'' by [[Constantin Meunier]] was erected. On its southern part, it holds the former [[Tour & Taxis]] former industrial site, which was annexed by the City of Brussels in 1897, twenty-four years before the rest of the municipality.

====Mutsaard====
Sometimes also known as the Pagoda district, the [[Japanese Tower of Brussels|Japanese Tower]] district or De Wand district, Mutsaard (also spelled Mutsaert), is an old hamlet and a historic district located between Laeken and Neder-over-Heembeek and centred around the {{lang|fr|Place du Mutsaert|italic=no}}/{{lang|nl|Mutsaertplaats or Mutsaardplein|italic=no}}. The district was part of the former municipality of Laeken (postcode: 1020), but also a piece of Neder-over-Heembeek, annexed by Laeken in 1897. It is separated from the rest of Laeken by the Royal Domain and is the site of the [[Museums of the Far East]]. The district also extends a little into the neighbouring Flemish municipalities of [[Vilvoorde]] and [[Grimbergen]].

====Neder-Over-Heembeek====
{{Main|Neder-Over-Heembeek}}

[[Neder-Over-Heembeek]] is a former municipality incorporated into the City of Brussels in 1921, at the same time as Laeken and Haren. It has the distinction of having the oldest place name in the Brussels-Capital Region, as it was mentioned in an [[Decree|ordinance]] as early as the 7th century.{{Citation needed|date=March 2018}} This is where the Queen Astrid Military Hospital, which is the National Burns and Poisons Centre, as well as recruitment services of the [[Belgian Armed Forces]] are located.

====Haren====
{{Main|Haren, Belgium}}

Like Laeken and Neder-Over-Heembeek, the former municipality of [[Haren, Belgium|Haren]] was annexed by the municipality (City) of Brussels in 1921, which allowed the extension of [[Schaarbeek railway station|Schaerbeek railway station]] north of its territory. But it was above all the presence, south-west of the town, of an airfield, created by the Germans during the [[World War I|First World War]], and where the former Belgian national airline [[Sabena]] was born, that precipitated the annexation of Haren. For almost fifty years, Haren has been home to [[NATO headquarters|NATO's headquarters]]. It is also the location of many other administrations and companies, such as the headquarters of [[Eurocontrol]].

==Demographics==

===Foreign population===
The City of Brussels has a large immigrant population, with both the EU and non-European migrant communities outnumbering the native Belgians. Akin to neighbouring [[Ixelles]], [[Etterbeek]] and [[Schaerbeek]], the City of Brussels also has a large [[Islam in Belgium|Muslim]] population, mainly of [[Moroccans in Belgium|North African]] origin.

{{As of|2023}}, taking into account the nationality of birth of the parents, 55.84% of the City of Brussels's population is of non-European origin (predominantly Moroccan, Indian and Congolese), 27.21% is of European origin other than Belgian (mainly French, Romanian, Italian, Spanish, and Polish), while 16.94% is solely of native Belgian ancestry.<ref>{{Cite web |title=Origin {{!}} Statbel |url=https://statbel.fgov.be/en/themes/population/structure-population/origin#figures |access-date=2023-07-04 |website=statbel.fgov.be}}</ref> Among all major migrant groups from outside the EU, a majority of the permanent residents have acquired Belgian nationality.<ref>{{cite web |title=2.738.486 inwoners van vreemde afkomst in België op 01/01/2012 |trans-title=2,738,486 inhabitants of foreign origin in Belgium on 01/01/2012 |url=http://www.npdata.be/BuG/155-Vreemde-afkomst/Vreemde-afkomst.htm |access-date=12 March 2013 |website=Npdata.be |language=nl |archive-date=22 October 2019 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20191022180710/http://www.npdata.be/BuG/155-Vreemde-afkomst/Vreemde-afkomst.htm |url-status=live}}</ref>

===Environmental policy===
Brussels is ranked sixth in the index of cities which are becoming greener fastest as for the year 2022, even though in the past it had a reputation of a "traffic-choked city of high rises and concrete". The authorities released a plan composed of seven steps on how to make the city even more [[Sustainable city|sustainable]]. Those include introducing "a use-based, [[Circular economy|circular]] and [[low-carbon economy]]" and making the city "proactive". The city should become a "[[15-minute city|10-minute city]]" meaning "making the facilities essential to urban life accessible to every inhabitant in less than 10 minutes". [[Urban green space|Green spaces]] should be expanded. The plan includes participation of the population in decision-making and high life level for all.<ref>{{cite news |last1=Ann Hughes |first1=Rebecca |title=10-minute city: How Brussels plans to become a pedestrian-friendly green hub |url=https://www.euronews.com/green/2023/12/22/10-minute-city-how-brussels-plans-to-become-a-pedestrian-friendly-green-hub |access-date=7 January 2024 |agency=Euronews |date=22 December 2023}}</ref>

===Food===
Brussels is well known for its food. [[Brussels sprout]]s were named after the city.<ref>{{Cite web|title=Real Food Encyclopedia {{!}} Brussels Sprouts|url=https://foodprint.org/real-food/brussels-sprouts/|access-date=9 February 2021|website=FoodPrint|language=en-US}}</ref> Like most of Belgium, ''[[moules-frites]]'', [[Belgian waffle|waffle]]s (gaufres), [[Belgian chocolate|chocolate]], [[French fries]], and [[Beer in Belgium|beer]] are common there.<ref>{{Cite web|last=2venturists|date=10 June 2018|title=What to Eat in Brussels Belgium|url=https://www.venturists.net/what-to-eat-in-brussels-belgium/|access-date=8 February 2021|website=Venturists|language=en-US}}</ref> It is home to one 2-starred and four 1-starred [[Michelin Guide|Michelin]] restaurants.<ref>{{Cite web|title=Brussels 2 Stars MICHELIN MICHELIN Restaurants – the MICHELIN Guide Belgium|url=https://guide.michelin.com/en/be/bruxelles-capitale/bruxelles/restaurants/2-stars-michelin/1-star-michelin|access-date=8 February 2021|website=MICHELIN Guide|language=en-US}}</ref>
