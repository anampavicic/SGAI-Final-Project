'''Udmurtia''',{{refn|group=note|{{IPAc-en|ʊ|d|ˈ|m|ʊər|t|i|ə}}; {{langx|ru|Удмуртия|Udmurtiya, {{IPA-all|ʊˈdmurtʲɪjə}}}}; {{langx|udm|Удмуртия|Udmurtija}}}} officially the '''Udmurt Republic''',{{refn|group=note|{{langx|ru|Удмуртская Республика|Udmurtskaya Respublika}}, {{langx|udm|Удмурт Республика/Элькун|Udmurt Respublika/Eľkun}}}} is a [[republics of Russia|republic]] of [[Russia]] located in [[Eastern Europe]]. It is administratively part of the [[Volga Federal District]]. Its [[capital city|capital]] is the [[types of inhabited localities in Russia|city]] of [[Izhevsk]].

It was established as the Udmurt (until 1931 — Votskaya) [[Autonomous administrative division|Autonomous Region]] on November 4, 1920.<ref>{{Cite web |title=Государственная автоматизированная система Российской Федерации «Правосудие» (17 марта 2010) |url=http://vs.udm.sudrf.ru/modules.php?id=17&name=info_court}}</ref>

==Name==
The name ''Udmurt'' comes from {{lang|mis|odo-mort}} ('meadow people'), where the first part represents the [[Permic]] root {{lang|mis|od}} or {{lang|mis|odo}} ('meadow, glade, turf, greenery'). This is supported by a document dated 1557, in which the [[Udmurt people|Udmurts]]<ref>{{cite web | url=https://dic.academic.ru/dic.nsf/vasmer/49476/уд | title=уд &#124; это... Что такое уд? }}</ref> are referred to as {{transliteration|ru|lugovye lyudi}} ('meadow people'), alongside the traditional Russian name {{transliteration|ru|otyaki}}.<ref>A.G. Ivanov, "Udmurty – 'Lugovye lyudi'", ''Linguistica Uralica'' Vol. 27, No. 3 (1991), pp. 188–92.</ref> 

The second part {{lang|mis|murt}} means 'person' (cf. [[Komi language|Komi]] {{transl|kv|mort}}, [[Mari language|Mari]] {{transl|chm|mari}}). It is probably an early borrowing from a [[Scythian languages|Scythian]] language: {{lang|mis|mertä}} or {{lang|mis|martiya}} ('person, man'; 
Sanskrit: Manus or Manushya), which is thought to have been borrowed from the [[Sanskrit language|Indo-Aryan]] term {{lang|inc|maryá-}} ('man, mortal, one who is bound to die'. cf. [[Old Indic]] {{transliteration|sa|márya}} ('young warrior') and {{transliteration|sa|marut}} ('chariot warrior'), both connected specifically with horses and chariots.<ref>Christopher I. Beckwith. Empires of the Silk Road: A History of Central Eurasia from the Bronze Age to the Present. Princeton: Princeton University Press. 2009. Page 397.</ref>  The [[Indo-Europeanists]] [[Tamaz V. Gamkrelidze|T. Gamkrelidze]] and [[Vyacheslav Vsevolodovich Ivanov|V. Ivanov]] associate this word with horse-riding Altaic tribes in the [[w:Bronze Age|Bronze Age]].<ref>R. Matasović (2009): Etymological Dictionary of Proto-Celtic, p. 257.</ref><ref>T. Gamkrelidze & V. Ivanov (1995): Indo-European and the Indo-Europeans, p. 472-473.</ref>

On the other hand, in the Russian tradition, the name 'meadow people' refers to the inhabitants of the left bank of river in particular. {{clarify|date=November 2022}} Recently, the most relevant is the version of [[Vladimir Napolskikh|V. V. Napolskikh]] and S. K. Belykh. They suppose that ethnonym was borrowed either from [[Indo-Iranian languages|Indo-Iranian]] {{lang|iir|*anta}} 'outside, close, last, edge, limit, boundary' or Turkic-Altaic {{lang|mis|*anda}}/{{lang|mis|*ant}} 'oath (in fidelity), comrade, friend'.<ref>{{Cite web |title=Этноним удмурт: исчерпаны ли альтернативы? |url=http://www.udmurt.info/library/belykh/udmetn.htm |access-date=2024-09-07 |website=www.udmurt.info}}</ref>

==History==
[[File:Udmurt03.png|thumb|right|Map of the Udmurt Republic.]]
{{History of Udmurtia}}

On November 4, 1920, the Votyak Autonomous Oblast was formed.<ref name="1987USSR">''Administrative-Territorial Structure of the Union Republics. 1987.'', p.&nbsp;57</ref> On January 1, 1932, it was renamed [[Udmurt Autonomous Oblast]],<ref>{{Cite web |last=Новости |first=Р. И. А. |date= October 31, 2019|title=Удмуртская Республика |url=https://ria.ru/20191031/1560441690.html |access-date= |website=РИА Новости |language=ru}}</ref> which was then reorganized into the [[Udmurt ASSR]] on December 28, 1934.<ref name="1987USSR" /> During [[World War II]], many industrial factories were evacuated from the [[Ukrainian SSR]] and western borderlands to Udmurtia.

On October 11, 1991, the Supreme Council of the Udmurt ASSR adopted a law according to which the Udmurt ASSR acquired a new name — the Udmurt Republic<ref>{{Cite web |title=Время перемен: Верховный Совет Удмуртии в эпоху девяностых |url=https://udmurt.media/rubrics/obshchestvo/93166-vremya_peremen_verkhovnyy_sovet_udmurtii_v_epokhu_devyanostykh_/?ysclid=lyo465ow7r288034001}}</ref>

==Geography==
The republic is located to the west of the [[Ural Mountains]] and borders [[Kirov Oblast|Kirov]], [[Perm Krai|Perm]], [[Bashkortostan]], and [[Tatarstan]].<ref>{{cite book|last=Robert W. Orttung|display-authors=etal |title=The Republics and Regions of the Russian Federation: A Guide to Politics, Policies and Leaders |chapter-url=https://books.google.com/books?id=VroABgoe4-EC&q=udmurtia&pg=PA589|year=2000|publisher=[[EastWest Institute]] |page=586|chapter=Republic of Udmurtia|isbn=9780765605597 }}</ref>

Udmurtia is a republic in the Russian Federation, located in Central Russia between the branches of the rivers [[Kama (river)|Kama]] and its right tributary the [[Vyatka (river)|Vyatka]].

The city of Izhevsk is the administrative, industrial and cultural center of Udmurtia. Geographically, it is located not far from Moscow, the capital and largest city of the Russian Federation. The city has a well-developed transport system (including air, land, and water).

Udmurtia borders Kirov Oblast to the west and north, Perm Oblast to the east, and the Bashkortostan and Tatarstan Republics to the south.

===Climate===
The republic has a moderate [[continental climate]], with warm summers and cold, snowy winters. Annual [[precipitation (meteorology)|precipitation]] averages 400–600 mm.{{Citation needed|date=March 2021}}

==Demographics==
Population: {{ru-census|p2021=1,452,914|p2010=1,521,420|p2002=1,570,316|p1989=1,609,003}}

Although as of 2007 the population was declining, the decline was stabilizing and was more pronounced in urban areas. Out of the 19,667 births reported in 2007, 12,631 were in urban areas (11.86 per 1,000) and 7,036 were in rural areas (14.88 per 1,000). Birth rates for rural areas are 25% higher than that of urban areas. Of the total of 21,727 deaths, 14,366 were reported in urban areas (13.49 per 1,000) and 7,361 were in rural areas (15.56 per 1,000). Natural decline of the population was measured at −0.16% for urban areas and an insignificant −0.07% for rural areas (the average for Russia was −0.33% in 2007).<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.gks.ru|title=Главная::Федеральная служба государственной статистики}}</ref>

===Ethnic groups===
According to the [[Russian Census (2021)|2021 Census]],<ref name="census2021">{{cite web|title=Национальный состав населения|url=https://rosstat.gov.ru/storage/mediabank/Tom5_tab1_VPN-2020.xlsx|publisher=[[Federal State Statistics Service (Russia)|Federal State Statistics Service]]|accessdate=30 December 2022}}</ref> [[Russians]] make up 67.7% of the republic's population, while the ethnic [[Udmurt people|Udmurts]] make up only 24.1%. Other groups include [[Tatars]] (5.5%), [[Mari people|Mari]] (0.5%), [[Ukrainians]] (0.3%), and a host of smaller groups, each accounting for less than 0.5% of the republic's total population.

According to a 2012 survey,<ref name="2012ArenaAtlas"/> 33.1% of the population of Udmurtia adheres to the [[Russian Orthodox Church]], 5% are [[nondenominational Christianity|unaffiliated]] generic [[Christians]], 2% are Eastern Orthodox Christian believers without belonging to any church or members of other [[Eastern Orthodox church]]es, 4% are [[Muslim]]s, 2% of the population adheres to the [[Slavic native faith]] (Rodnovery) or to [[Udmurt Vos]] (Udmurt native faith), 1% adheres to forms of [[Protestantism]], and 1% of the population are [[Old Believers]]. In addition, 29% of the population declares to be "[[spiritual but not religious]]," 19% is [[atheism|atheist]], and 3.9% follows other religions or did not give an answer to the question.<ref name="2012ArenaAtlas" />

The local Russian Orthodox Church is the Metropolitanate of Udmurtia, comprising the Eparchy of Izhevsk (founded 1927) under Bishop and Metropolitan Viktorin (Kostenkov) (2015), the Eparchy of Glazov (founded 1889) under Bishop Viktor (Sergeyev) and the Eparchy of Sarapul (founded 1868) under Bishop Anthony (Prostikhin) (2015).

==Culture==
{{See|Udmurt cuisine|Music in Udmurtia}}
Udmurt folklore is understood both in a broad sense ({{transliteration|udm|kalyk oner}}, {{transliteration|udm|kalyk todon-valan}}, {{transliteration|udm|kalyk viz}} - folk knowledge, folk wisdom), and in a narrower one ({{transliteration|udm|kalyk kylos}}, {{transliteration|udm|kalyk kylburet}} - folk poetry, oral poetry). In everyday life, folklore is not divided into genres, it is perceived in unity with material culture, with religious, legal and ethical aspects. Popular terms-definitions have incorporated the ritual action ({{transliteration|udm|syam}}, {{transliteration|udm|nerge}}, {{transliteration|udm|yilol}}, {{transliteration|udm|kiston}}, {{transliteration|udm|kuyaskon}}, {{transliteration|udm|syuan}}, {{transliteration|udm|madiskon}}), symbolically figurative and magically forming words ({{transliteration|udm|madkyl}}, {{transliteration|udm|vyzhykyl}}, {{transliteration|udm|tunkyl}}, {{transliteration|udm|kylbur}}), musical and choreographic behavior ({{transliteration|udm|krez}}, {{transliteration|udm|gur}}, {{transliteration|udm|shudon-serekyan}}, {{transliteration|udm|thatchan}}, {{transliteration|udm|ecton}}).<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.tangyra.ru/etno/info/folklore.html|title=Удмуртский фольклор - ТАНГЫРА}}</ref>
