'''W√ºrzburg''' ({{IPA|de|Ààv è Åtsb ä Åk|lang|De-W√ºrzburg.ogg}}; [[Main-Franconian]]: {{lang|vmf|W√∂rtzburch}}) is, after [[Nuremberg]] and [[F√ºrth]], the [[Franconia#Towns and cities|third-largest]] city in [[Franconia]] located in the north of [[Bavaria]]. W√ºrzburg is the administrative seat of the [[Regierungsbezirk]] [[Lower Franconia]]. It spans the banks of the [[Main (river)|Main river]].

W√ºrzburg is situated approximately 110&nbsp;km west-northwest of [[Nuremberg]] and 120&nbsp;km east-southeast of [[Frankfurt|Frankfurt am Main]]. The population as of 2019 is approximately 130,000 residents.<ref>{{Cite web |last=Wuerzburg |first=Stadt |title=W√ºrzburg Online - Bev√∂lkerung |url=https://www.wuerzburg.de/buerger/statistikstadtforschung/bevoelkerung/31501.Bevoelkerung.html |access-date=2018-10-07 |publisher =www.wuerzburg.de}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |title=Census 2022 |url=https://www.zensus2022.de/DE/Home/_inhalt.html |access-date=2021-11-02 |website=Statistisches Bundesamt |language=de}}</ref>

W√ºrzburg is famous for its partly rebuilt and reconstructed old town<ref name="auto">{{Cite web|url=https://geoportal.bayern.de/denkmalatlas/searchResult.html?koid=59353&objtyp=bau&top=1|title=DenkmalAtlas 2.0|website=geoportal.bayern.de}}</ref> and its [[W√ºrzburg Residence|W√ºrzburger Residenz]], a [[List of World Heritage Sites in Germany|UNESCO World Heritage Site]].

The regional dialect is [[East Franconian German]].

===Early and medieval history===
[[File:W√ºrzburg Siegel.jpg|thumb|Impression of the city seal of 1319]]
[[File:Nuremberg chronicles - HERBIPOLIS.png|thumb|Woodcut depicting W√ºrzburg from the [[Nuremberg Chronicle]] (1493)]]
[[File:Dankaerts-Historis-9309.tif|thumb|Panorama of W√ºrzburg with castle Marienberg. [[Matth√§us Merian]] in Cornelis Danckerts, "Historis", 1642.]]

A [[Bronze Age Europe|Bronze Age]] ([[Urnfield culture]]) [[refuge castle]], the Celtic [[Segodunum]],<ref name="koch131">Koch, John T. (2020). [https://www.wales.ac.uk/Resources/Documents/Centre/2020/Celto-Germanic2020.pdf CELTO-GERMANIC Later Prehistory and Post-Proto-Indo-European vocabulary in the North and West] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20211125063756/https://www.wales.ac.uk/Resources/Documents/Centre/2020/Celto-Germanic2020.pdf |date=2021-11-25 }}, p. 131</ref> and later a [[Roman Empire|Roman]] fort, stood on the hill known as the Leistenberg,<ref name="EB1911">{{Cite EB1911|wstitle=W√ºrzburg|volume=28|page=860}}</ref> the site of the present [[Fortress Marienberg]]. The former [[Celts|Celtic]] territory was settled by [[Alamanni]] in the 4th or 5th century later by the [[Franks]] in the 6th to 7th. W√ºrzburg was the seat of a [[Merovingian]] duke from about 650. It was Christianized in 686 by [[Irish missionaries]] [[Saint Kilian|Kilian]], [[St. Colman (martyr)|Kolonat]] and [[Saint Totnan|Totnan]]. The city is mentioned in a donation by Duke [[Hedan II]] to bishop [[Willibrord]], dated 1 May 704, ''in castellum Virteburch''. The [[Ravenna Cosmography]] lists the city as ''Uburzis'' at about the same time.<ref>Norbert Wagner, 'Uburzis-Wirziburg "W√ºrzburg"'</ref> The name is presumably of [[Celtic toponyms|Celtic]] origin, but based on a [[folk etymology|folk etymological]] connection to the German word ''[[:wikt:W√ºrze|W√ºrze]]'' "herb, spice", the name was [[Medieval Latin|Latinized]] as ''Herbipolis'' in the medieval period.<ref>Heinz Willner, ''Der Name W√ºrzburg'', Frankenland 1/1999.</ref><ref>{{cite web |title=Sacred Magic of Abramelin: The First Book: The Eighth Chapter |url=http://www.sacred-texts.com/grim/abr/abr014.htm#fn_58 |website=www.sacred-texts.com |access-date=8 July 2021}}</ref>

The first diocese was founded by [[Saint Boniface]] in 742 when he appointed the first [[Bishopric of W√ºrzburg|bishop of W√ºrzburg]], [[Burchard of W√ºrzburg|Saint Burkhard]]. The [[Bishops in the Catholic Church|bishops]] eventually created a secular fiefdom, that extended to Eastern [[duchy of Franconia|Franconia]] in the 12th century. The city was the site of several [[Imperial Diet (Holy Roman Empire)|Imperial Diets]], including the one of 1180, at which [[Henry the Lion]], duke of Saxony and Bavaria, was banned for three years from the Empire<ref name="EB1911"/> and his [[Kingdom of Bavaria|duchy]] Bavaria was handed over to [[Otto I of Wittelsbach, Duke of Bavaria|Otto of Wittelsbach]]. Massacres of Jews took place in 1147 and 1298.

The first church on the site of the present [[W√ºrzburg Cathedral]] was built as early as 788 and consecrated that same year by [[Charlemagne]]; the current building was constructed from 1040 to 1225 in [[Romanesque architecture|Romanesque]] style. The [[University of W√ºrzburg]] was founded in 1402 and re-founded in 1582 by [[Julius Echter von Mespelbrunn]]. The citizens of the city revolted several times against the prince-bishop.

In 1397, King [[Wenceslaus IV of Bohemia]] visited the city and promised its people the status of a [[free Imperial City]]. However, the German ruling princes forced him to withdraw these promises. In 1400, the bishop's troops decisively defeated the citizenry in the {{ill|Battle of Bergtheim|de|Schlacht von Bergtheim}}, and the city fell under his control permanently until the dissolution of the fiefdom.<ref name="Dumont"/>{{rp|41}} During the [[German Peasants' War]], a local town council member, [[Tilman Riemenschneider]] refused to obey an order by [[Konrad von Th√ºngen]], the [[Prince-Bishop of W√ºrzburg]] to fight the revolting peasants.<ref name="Booklet">{{cite book |title=Tilman Riemenschneider ‚Äì Werke seiner Bl√ºtezeit (German) |publisher=Freunde Mainfr√§nkischer Kunst und Geschichte e.V. W√ºrzburg |year=2004}}</ref>{{rp|23}} This resulted in 8,000 peasants killed on 4 June 1525 just outside W√ºrzburg. Riemenschneider and the entire town council was incarcerated and tortured in [[Marienberg Fortress]].<ref name="Booklet" />{{rp|24}}

=== Modern history ===
The [[W√ºrzburg witch trials]], which occurred between 1626 and 1631, are one of the largest peace-time mass trials. In W√ºrzburg, under Bishop [[Philipp Adolf von Ehrenberg|Philip Adolf]], an estimated 600 to 900 alleged witches were burnt.<ref>Wolfgang Behringer, ''Witchcraft in Bavaria: Popular Magik, Religious Zealotry, and Reason of State in Early Modern Europe'', (Cambridge University Press, 1997).</ref> In 1631, Swedish King [[Gustavus Adolphus of Sweden|Gustaf Adolf]] invaded and ended the witch burnings.

In 1720, the foundations of the [[W√ºrzburg Residence]] were laid. In 1796, the [[Battle of W√ºrzburg]] between [[Austrian monarchy|Habsburg Austria]] and the [[First French Republic]] took place. The city passed to the [[Electorate of Bavaria]] in 1803 but, two years later, in the course of the [[Napoleonic Wars]], it became the seat of the Electorate of W√ºrzburg (until September 1806), the later [[Grand Duchy of W√ºrzburg]].

In 1814, the town became part of the [[Kingdom of Bavaria]] and [[Roman Catholic Diocese of W√ºrzburg|a new bishopric]] was created seven years later, as the former one had been [[German mediatization|secularized in 1803]] (see also [[Reichsdeputationshauptschluss]]).
In 1817, [[Friedrich Koenig]] and [[Andreas Friedrich Bauer|Andreas Bauer]] founded ''Schnellpressenfabrik Koenig & Bauer'' (the world's first steam-driven [[printing press]] manufacturer).

The [[Hep-Hep riots]] from August to October 1819 were [[pogrom]]s against [[Ashkenazi Jews]], beginning in the Kingdom of Bavaria, during the period of Jewish emancipation in the [[German Confederation]]. The antisemitic communal violence began on August 2, 1819, in W√ºrzburg and soon reached the outer regions of the German Confederation. Many Jews were killed, and much Jewish property was destroyed.

In 1848, Catholic bishops held the [[W√ºrzburg Bishops' Conference]], a forerunner of later German and Austrian conferences. By distinction, the ''W√ºrzburg Conference'' is a name given to the meeting of representatives of the smaller German states in 1859 to devise some means of mutual support. The conference, however, had no result. W√ºrzburg was bombarded and taken by the [[Prussia]]ns in 1866 when it ceased to be a fortress.<ref name="EB1911"/>

In the early 1930s, around 2,000 Jews lived in W√ºrzburg, which was also a rabbinic centre. The [[Nazi Party]] in [[Enabling Act of 1933|1933 achieved total control]]. During the ''[[Kristallnacht]]'' pogroms in 1938, many Jewish houses and shops were raided, looted, or destroyed.<ref name=kn>{{cite web|url=https://www.yadvashem.org/yv/en/exhibitions/communities/wurzburg/kristallnacht.asp|title=W√ºrzburg During the Holocaust. Kristallnacht|website=Yad Vashem|access-date=14 June 2020}}</ref> The contents of two [[synagogues]] were stolen or destroyed.<ref name=kn/> Many Jews were imprisoned and tortured by the [[Gestapo]].<ref name=kn/> Between November 1941 and June 1943, Jews from the city were sent to the [[Nazi concentration camps]] in Eastern Europe.<ref>[http://www.yadvashem.org/yv/en/exhibitions/communities/wurzburg/index.asp?WT.mc_id=wiki The Story of the Jewish Community in W√ºrzburg] an online exhibition by [[Yad Vashem]]</ref>

From April 1943 to March 1945 a subcamp of the [[Flossenb√ºrg concentration camp]] was located in the city, with dozens of prisoners, mostly from Poland and the [[Soviet Union]].<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.gedenkstaette-flossenbuerg.de/en/history/satellite-camps/wuerzburg|title=W√ºrzburg Subcamp|website=KZ-Gedenkst√§tte Flossenb√ºrg|access-date=14 June 2020}}</ref>

====World War II bombing====
{{main|Bombing of W√ºrzburg in World War II}}
On 16 March 1945, about 90% of the city was destroyed in 17 minutes by firebombing from 225 British [[Avro Lancaster|Lancaster bombers]] during a [[World War II]] air raid. W√ºrzburg became a target for its role as a traffic hub and to break the spirit of the population.<ref name="Dumont">{{cite book|last=Dettelbacher|first=Werner|title=Franken - Kunst, Geschichte und Landschaft (German)|publisher=Dumont Verlag|year=1974|isbn=3-7701-0746-2}}</ref>{{rp|19}}

All of the city's churches, cathedrals, and other monuments were heavily damaged or destroyed. The city centre, which mostly dated from medieval times, was destroyed in a [[firestorm]] in which 5,000 people perished.{{citation needed|date=August 2022}}

Over the next 20 years, the buildings of historical importance were painstakingly and accurately reconstructed. The citizens who rebuilt the city immediately after the end of the war were mostly women ‚Äì ''[[Tr√ºmmerfrauen]]'' ("rubble women") ‚Äì because the men were either dead or still [[prisoners of war]]. On a relative scale, W√ºrzburg was destroyed to a larger extent than was [[Bombing of Dresden in World War II|Dresden in a firebombing]] the previous month.{{citation needed|date=August 2022}} Today the whole of the old town is sited as a cultural heritage site after decades of rebuilding and reconstructions.<ref name="auto"/>

====Battle of W√ºrzburg====
{{main|Battle of W√ºrzburg (1945)}}
On 3 April 1945, W√ºrzburg was occupied by the U.S. [[12th Armored Division (United States)|12th Armored Division]] and the U.S. [[42nd Infantry Division (United States)|42nd Infantry Division]] in a series of frontal assaults masked by smokescreens. The battle continued until the last German resistance was defeated on 5 April 1945.<ref>Stanton, Shelby, ''World War II Order of Battle: An Encyclopedic Reference to U.S. Army Ground Forces from Battalion through Division, 1939‚Äì1946'' (Revised Edition, 2006), Stackpole Books, p. 65, 129.</ref><ref>[http://www.ibiblio.org/hyperwar/AAF/III/AAF-III-21.html Seite 777], see also [http://www.ibiblio.org/hyperwar/AAF/III/AAF-III-21.html Chapter XVIII]</ref>

==Geography==
[[File:Wuerzburg mit Festung Marienberg Luftansicht 20160813.jpg|thumb|W√ºrzburg with [[Marienberg Fortress|Fortress Marienberg]] and [[Main (river)|Main]] river]]
[[File:2019-06-07 Wuerzburg panorama from Festung.jpg|thumb|Panoramic view of city center from the [[Marienberg Fortress|fortress]] with [[Main (river)|Main]] river]]
W√ºrzburg spans the banks of the [[Main (river)|river Main]] in the region of Lower Franconia in the north of the state of Bavaria, Germany. The heart of the town is on the locally eastern ([[bank (geography)|right]]) bank. The town is enclosed by the ''Landkreis W√ºrzburg'' but is not a part of it.

W√ºrzburg covers an area of 87.6 square kilometres and lies at an altitude of around 177 metres.<ref>{{Cite web|last=Wuerzburg|first=Stadt|title=Rathaus {{!}} W√ºrzburg in Zahlen - Stadtgebiet, Fl√§chennutzung, Klima|url=https://www.wuerzburg.de/buerger/statistikstadtforschung/stadtgebietflaechennutzungklima/32319.Stadtgebiet-Flaechennutzung-Klima.html|access-date=2020-09-27|website=www.wuerzburg.de}}</ref>

Of the total municipal area, in 2007, building area accounted for 30%, followed by agricultural land (27.9%), forestry/wood (15.5%), green spaces (12.7%), traffic (5.4%), water (1.2%) and others (7.3%).<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.wuerzburg.de/media/www.wuerzburg.de/org/med_5493/402237_flaechenaufteilung_stadtgebiet.pdf |title=Data  |website=www.wuerzburg.de |access-date=2020-09-27}}</ref>

The centre of W√ºrzburg is surrounded by hills. To the west lies the 266-meter Marienberg and the Nikolausberg (359 m) to the south of it. The Main flows through W√ºrzburg from the southeast to the northwest.

==Economy==
W√ºrzburg is mainly known as an administrative centre. Its largest employers are the [[University of W√ºrzburg|Julius-Maximilians-University]] and the municipality. The largest private employers are [[Brose Fahrzeugteile]] followed by [[Koenig & Bauer]], a maker of printing machines. W√ºrzburg is also the capital of the German wine region [[Franconia (wine region)|Franconia]] which is famous for its mineral-rich dry white wines, especially from the [[Silvaner]] grape. [[W√ºrzburger Hofbr√§u]] brewery also locally produces a well-known pilsner beer.

===Military===
Following World War II, W√ºrzburg was occupied by the U.S. Army's [[U.S. 1st Infantry Division|1st]] and [[U.S. 3d Infantry Division|3rd]] infantry divisions, as well as an Army hospital staff and various other U.S. military units. The last U.S. troops were withdrawn in 2008, concluding more than 60 years of U.S. presence in W√ºrzburg.

==Arts and culture==
Notable artists who lived in W√ºrzburg include poet [[Walther von der Vogelweide]] (12th and 13th centuries), philosopher [[Albertus Magnus]] and painter [[Matthias Gr√ºnewald]]. Sculptor [[Tilman Riemenschneider]] (1460‚Äì1531) served as mayor and participated in the [[German Peasants' War]]. [[Richard Wagner]] obtained the position of chorusmaster at the city‚Äôs theater in 1833 through his brother, Albert, and finished his first opera, [[Die Feen]] (The Fairies), there that year (see Wikipedia article on Richard Wagner).

Some of the city's "100 churches" survived intact. In style they range from [[Romanesque architecture|Romanesque]] ([[W√ºrzburg Cathedral]]), [[Gothic architecture|Gothic]] (''[[Marienkapelle, W√ºrzburg|Marienkapelle]]''), [[Renaissance architecture|Renaissance]] (''{{Interlanguage link|Neubaukirche (W√ºrzburg)|de|3=Neubaukirche (W√ºrzburg)|lt=Neubaukirche}}''), [[Baroque architecture|Baroque]] (''{{Interlanguage link|Kollegiatstift Haug|de|3=Kollegiatstift Haug|lt=Stift Haug Kirche}}'') to modern (St. Andreas).

Major festivals include the ''Africa Festival'' in May, the ''[[Mozart Festival W√ºrzburg|Mozart Festival]]'' in June/July, and the ''Kiliani Volksfest'' in mid-July.

*''[[W√ºrzburg Residence|W√ºrzburger Residenz]]'': A [[UNESCO]] [[World Heritage Site]], the vast compound near the center of the town was commissioned by two prince-bishops, the brothers [[Johann Philipp Franz von Sch√∂nborn|Johann Philipp Franz]] and [[Friedrich Karl von Sch√∂nborn]]. Several architects, including [[Johann Lukas von Hildebrandt]] and [[Maximilian von Welsch]], supervised the construction between 1720 and 1744, in imitation of the [[Palace of Versailles]],<ref name="EB1911"/> but it is mainly associated with the name of [[Balthasar Neumann]], the creator of its famous Baroque staircase. The palace suffered severe damage in the British bombing of March 1945 but has been completely rebuilt. The main attractions are:
**''Hofkirche'': The church interior is richly decorated with paintings, sculptures and stucco ornaments. The altars were painted by [[Giovanni Battista Tiepolo]].
**''Treppenhaus'': Here Giovanni Battista Tiepolo created the largest fresco in the world, which adorns the vault over the staircase designed by [[Balthasar Neumann]]. 
**''Kaisersaal'': The "Imperial Hall", the centerpiece of the palace, testifies to the close relationship between W√ºrzburg and the [[Holy Roman Empire]].
*''[[Marienberg Fortress|Festung Marienberg]]'' is a fortress on ''Marienberg'', the hill to the west of the city centre, overlooking the whole town area and the surrounding hills. Most current structures date to the Renaissance and Baroque periods, but the chapel's foundations go back to the 8th century.
[[File:San Kilian.jpg|thumb|The statue of [[Saint Kilian|St Kilian]], with the [[Marienberg Fortress]] on the right]]
*''{{Interlanguage link|Alte Mainbr√ºcke|de}}'' (Old Main Bridge) was built in 1473‚Äì1543 to replace the destroyed Romanesque bridge dated 1133. In two phases, beginning in 1730, the bridge was adorned with twelve 4.5-meter statues of saints and historically important figures like [[John of Nepomuk]], [[Mary, mother of Jesus|Mary]] and [[Saint Joseph]], [[Charlemagne]] and [[Pepin the Short]]. Explosives damaged the bridge in the final days of World War II. US troops threw the original Pepin into the river to make way for an anti-aircraft gun.<ref name="Dumont"/>{{rp|32}}
* The ''Rathaus'' or city hall of W√ºrzburg differs from those of most [[Imperial Cities]] in that it was not a sumptuous edifice purpose-built in Renaissance style. Rather, the motley collection of buildings and wings reflects that after 1426 the city was permanently under the bishop's control, who did not allow a representative new building. The ''Rathaus'' consists of parts dating from 1339 (chapel), 1453 (tower with the town's first public clock), 1544 (southwest oriel), and 1659/60 (''Roter Bau''). In 1822 the three-winged structure of the neighboring ''Karmeliterkloster'' (monastery of the [[Carmelites]]) was added to the city hall. The "Renaissance" row on Karmeliterstrasse was built only in 1898.<ref name="Dumont"/>{{rp|41}}
*Among W√ºrzburg's many notable churches are the ''[[K√§ppele]]'', a small Baroque/Rococo chapel by Balthasar Neumann, perched on a hill facing the fortress, and the ''Dom'' ([[W√ºrzburg Cathedral]]). The Baroque ''Sch√∂nbornkapelle'', a side-chapel of the cathedral, has interior decoration of (artificial) human bones and skulls. Also in the cathedral are two of [[Tilman Riemenschneider]]'s most famous works, the tombstones of [[Rudolf von Scherenberg|Rudolf II von Scherenberg]] (1466‚Äì1495) and [[Lorenz von Bibra]] (1495‚Äì1519). At the entrance to the ''[[Marienkapelle, W√ºrzburg|Marienkapelle]]'' (on the market square; built between 1377 and 1441) stand replicas of the statues of Adam and Eve by Riemenschneider.<ref name="EB1911"/> The ''[[Neum√ºnster, W√ºrzburg|Neum√ºnster]]'' is a Romanesque (11th century) minster church with a Baroque fa√ßade and dome. Its crypt (''Kiliansgruft'') houses the relics of Kilian, Totnan, and Kolonat.<ref name="EB1911"/> There are also two stone sarcophagi from the 8th century, the tombs of the first and second Bishop of W√ºrzburg, Burkard and Megingaud. The latter's tomb features the oldest post-Roman [[monumental inscription]] in Franconia.<ref name="Dumont"/>{{rp|45}} Next to the ''Neum√ºnster'' is the ''{{Interlanguage link|Lusamg√§rtchen|de}}''. It contains a memorial from 1930 to [[Walther von der Vogelweide]], who very likely was buried here in 1230.<ref name="Dumont"/>{{rp|47}} Only the church remains of the town's oldest abbey, [[St. Burchard's Abbey, W√ºrzburg|St Burchard's Abbey]] founded around 750. It was transformed into a [[collegiate church]] in 1464 and dissolved in 1803. Among the Baroque churches in the city centre is ''Stift Haug'' (1670‚Äì1691), ''{{Interlanguage link|St. Michael (W√ºrzburg)|de|3=St. Michael (W√ºrzburg)|lt=St. Michael}}'', ''{{Interlanguage link|St. Stephan (W√ºrzburg)|de|3=St. Stephan (W√ºrzburg)|lt=St. Stephan}}'' and ''St. Peter''. The church of St Burkhard was built between 1033 and 1042 in the Romanesque style and was restored in 1168. The [[International Gothic|Late Gothic]] choir dates from 1494 to 1497.<ref name="EB1911"/>
*The ''[[Stiftung Juliusspital W√ºrzburg|Juliusspital]]'' is a Baroque hospital with a courtyard and a church originally established by prince-bishop [[Julius Echter]] in 1576. The 160 m long northern wing was added by Italian architect {{Interlanguage link|Antonio Petrini|it}} in 1700‚Äì1704. Beneath it lies the similarly sized wine cellar, which (together with those of the W√ºrzburg Residence and the ''[[B√ºrgerspital zum Heiligen Geist|B√ºrgerspital]]'') offers a chance to taste the local ''[[Franconia (wine region)|Frankenwein]]'' in a unique environment. The Juliusspital is the second largest winery in Germany, growing wine on {{convert|1.68|km2|0|abbr=off}}.<ref name="Dumont"/>{{rp|58‚Äì9}}
*The ''{{Interlanguage link|Haus zum Falken|de}}'' on Marktplatz, next to the ''Marienkapelle'', with its ornate stucco fa√ßade, is an achievement of the W√ºrzburg Rococo period. In the past, it served as an inn, and today it houses a public library and the tourist information office.<ref name="Dumont"/>{{rp|62}}
*The ''Stift Haug'' (formally the ''Stiftskirche St. Johannis im Haug'', dedicated to [[John the Baptist]] and [[John the Evangelist]]) was built in the years 1670‚Äì1691 as the first Baroque church in Franconia. Works of art include a [[Crucifixion of Jesus|crucifixion]] by [[Tintoretto]] loaned by the [[Bavarian State Painting Collections]].<ref name="Dumont"/>{{rp|59‚Äì60}}
*The ''[[W√ºrzburger Stein]]'' vineyard just outside the city is one of Germany's oldest and largest vineyards.

==Education and research==
W√ºrzburg has several internationally recognized institutions in science and research:

=== University ===
[[File:Wuerzburg old university 2001.jpg|thumb|200px|''Alte Universit√§t'', the old [[Renaissance architecture|Renaissance]] building of W√ºrzburg University]]
The [[University of W√ºrzburg]] (official name ''Julius-Maximilians-Universit√§t W√ºrzburg'') was founded in 1402 and is one of the oldest universities in Germany.

Academic disciplines are [[astronomy]], [[biology]], [[Catholic theology]], [[chemistry]], [[computer science]], [[culture]], [[economics]], [[Pedagogy|educational]] and [[social sciences]], [[geography]], [[history]], [[languages]] and [[linguistics]], [[law]], [[literature]], [[mathematics]], [[medicine]] ([[human medicine]], [[dentistry]] and [[biomedicine]]), [[pharmacy]], [[philosophy]], [[physics]], [[political science]], [[psychology]] and [[sociology]].

Today, the ten faculties are spread throughout the city. The university currently enrolls approximately 29,000 students, out of which more than 1,000 come from other countries.

*[[Wilhelm R√∂ntgen]]'s original laboratory, where he discovered [[X-ray]]s in 1895, is at the University of W√ºrzburg.
*The university awarded [[Alexander Graham Bell]] an honorary [[Ph.D.]] for his pioneering scientific work.
*The [[Botanischer Garten der Universit√§t W√ºrzburg]] is the university's [[botanical garden]].

=== University of Applied Science ===
[[File:Fh wuerzburg hauptgebaeude.jpg|thumb|[[University of Applied Sciences W√ºrzburg-Schweinfurt]] main building in the city centre]] 
The [[University of Applied Sciences W√ºrzburg-Schweinfurt]] was founded in 1971 as an [[institute of technology]] with departments in W√ºrzburg and [[Schweinfurt]]. Academic disciplines are [[architecture]], [[business economics]], [[business informatics]], [[civil engineering]], [[computational engineering]], [[computer science]], [[electrical engineering]], [[engineering management]], [[geodesy]], [[graphic design]], [[logistics]], [[mechanical engineering]], [[mass media|media]], [[nursing theory]], [[plastics engineering]], and [[social work]]. With nearly 8,000 students, it is the second-largest university of applied science in Franconia.

=== Conservatory ===
The Conservatory of W√ºrzburg is an institution with a long tradition as well as an impressive success story of more than 200 years. It was founded in 1797 as ''Collegium musicum academicum'' and is Germany's oldest conservatory. Nowadays, it is known as [[Hochschule f√ºr Musik W√ºrzburg|University of Music W√ºrzburg]]. After the commutation from the conservatory to the university of music in the early 1970s, science and research were added to complement music education.
