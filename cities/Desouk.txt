'''Desouk''' ({{langx|ar|دسوق}}, {{IPA|arz|dɪˈsuːʔ}} {{Audio|Dosūq (Desouk) in Arabic.ogg|pronunciation}}) is a [[List of cities in Egypt|city]] in northern [[Egypt]]. Located 80&nbsp;km east of [[Alexandria]], in the [[Kafr El Sheikh Governorate]] and had a population of 137,660 inhabitants as of 2011.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://desouq.org/ta2sim-edari.html|title=Presidency of Desouk City, Administrative division & Population (2011)|publisher=Epcodes.com|date=2009-12-31|access-date=2010-12-29|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20101113054109/http://desouq.org/ta2sim-edari.html|archive-date=13 November 2010|url-status=dead}}</ref> It is bordered to the west by the [[Beheira Governorate]].

Desouk dates back to at least c. 3200 BC and was part of the ancient city of [[Buto]] before the unification of Upper and Lower Egypt. From 1250 to 1517, the city of Desouk was part of the Gharbia province. From 1798 to 1801, it was part of the now-defunct Rosetta province.

== Etymology ==
The city's name could be derived from {{Langx|egy|tꜣ-sbk|lit=land of [[Sobek]]}}, attested on a statuette from [[Sais, Egypt|Sais]] dating to the [[Third Intermediate Period of Egypt|Third Intermediate Period]], through {{Langx|cop|*ⲧⲉⲥⲟⲩⲭⲓ}}, or from {{Langx|egy|tꜣ-ı͗ꜣ.t-sbk|lit=}}, attested in Greek as ''Thasoukhios'' ({{Langx|grc|Θασουχιος}}) and ''Tasoukis'' ({{Langx|grc|Τασουκις}}). The cult of Sobek had presence to the west of Disuq, on the other side of the [[Nile]].

Other proposal derives it from the rare Arabic verb ''dasaqa'' "to overflow (about a basin)" and its nominal form ''daysaq'' "bassin full of water" <ref>El-Gwely, Faris. “التلوث الصوتي بمدينة دسوق (دراسة جغرافية).” University of Sadat City, 2019.</ref> which in turn has its origin in {{Langx|grc|δίσκος|lit=disk, salver}}, but it is considered implausible. Another improbable etymology is a Copto-Arabic word combining the Coptic feminine definite article ''ti-'' ({{Langx|cop|ϯ-}}) with {{Langx|ar|سوق|lit=market|translit=suq}}.<ref>{{Cite book |last=Engsheden |first=Âke |title=Ancient Place-Names in the Governorate of Kafr el-Sheikh |date=15 August 2023 |publisher=Peeters |isbn=9789042941755 |pages=133–134}}</ref>

==Overview==
[[File:Desouk-Name in English.jpg|thumb|150px|left|"Desouk"]]
Desouk is a member of the Organization of Islamic Capitals and Cities<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.oicc.org/oiccenglish/membership.htm|title=Organization of Islamic Capitals and Cities, Membership|publisher=oicc.org|access-date=2010-12-29|archive-date=7 January 2011|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110107101627/http://www.oicc.org/oiccenglish/membership.htm|url-status=dead}}</ref> due to the location of important Islamic shrines in the city, such as the tomb of Egyptian [[Sufism|Sufi]] Saint [[Ibrahim El Desouki]], which is located in the main mosque in the center of Desouk.

Desouk lies on the Nile, on the eastern banks of the Rosetta branch, where there are only two bridges entering the city.

Many important Egyptians hail from Desouk: Youssef El-Mansy, [[Ahmed Zewail]],<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.sis.gov.eg/VR/figures/english/html/a1.htm|title=Egyptian figures, Ahmed H. Zewail|publisher=sis.gov.eg|access-date=2010-12-28|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20101016003736/http://www.sis.gov.eg/VR/figures/english/html/a1.htm|archive-date=16 October 2010|url-status=dead}}</ref> Mohammed Roshdy, [[Evelyn Ashamallah]], and Abdel-Salam Mohammed Nasar, a politician in the city.

== Tourism ==
Desouk is renowned for the presence of [[Ibrahim El Desouki Mosque]], which attracts over a million visitors annually on average. The mosque is the final resting place of the last Sufi pole, Imam Ibrahim El Desouki, established around the year 1277. It currently covers an area of 6400 square meters, and the Ladies' Mosque occupies 600 square meters, making it one of the largest mosques in the Islamic world in terms of area. It houses a university Islamic library as well. In Desouk, an annual celebration is held for the birth of Ibrahim El-Desouki in October, lasting a week with strict security measures. There are numerous Pharaonic artifacts in the ancient city of [[Buto]], which served as the political capital of the [[Lower Egypt]]. Its construction dates back to the [[Prehistoric Egypt|Predynastic period]], and it is located northeast of Desouk, about 12 km away.<ref>[http://www.costaricatourguide.org/hd/index.php?t=Disuq Costa Rica Tour Guide - Desouk] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160409021833/http://www.costaricatourguide.org/hd/?t=disuq |date=9 أبريل 2016}}</ref> Buto was a significant religious center where every king or prince, upon ascending to rule, was required to go to Buto to legitimize their rule by the priests. This was done for worship and to draw closer to the goddess [[Wadjet]], the Lady of the City, a central figure in the Egyptian myth of [[Isis]] and [[Osiris]].<ref>منتصر ثابت، إيزيس ونفتيس دراما البعث والخلود، دار الهلال، القاهرة، 2010.</ref>

The area is characterized by several artifacts, some dating back to the Predynastic period, such as the Wadjet Temple, [[Horus]] Statue, and the double statue of the goddess [[Sekhmet]] and King [[Ramesses II]], among many other artifacts.<ref>وزارة الإسكان والمرافق والتنميّة العمرانيّة، مشروع تحديث المخطط العام لمدينة دسوق (حتى عام 2017) - التقرير العام 2006، صـ: 19، الهيئة العامة للتخطيط العمراني، دسوق، 2006.</ref> Locally, the region is known as "Tall Al-Fara'in," named after the founding pharaohs. It spans an area of 175 acres (0.7 km²).<ref>وزارة الإسكان والمرافق والتنميّة العمرانيّة، مشروع تحديث المخطط العام لمدينة دسوق (حتى عام 2017) - التقرير العام 2006، صـ: 18، الهيئة العامة للتخطيط العمراني، دسوق، 2006.</ref><ref>[http://dostor.org/%D9%85%D8%AF%D9%86/10/june/23/20206 الدستور، الخميس 26 يونيو 2010] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20100629181226/http://dostor.org/مدن/10/june/23/20206|date=29 يونيو 2010}}</ref>
