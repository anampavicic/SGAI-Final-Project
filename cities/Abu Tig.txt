'''Abu Tig''' ({{langx|ar|أبو تيج}}; {{langx|cop|Ⲧⲁⲡⲟⲑⲏⲕⲏ}}<ref>{{Cite book|title=Das christlich-koptische Agypten in arabischer Zeit (Teil 1 A-C)|last=Timm|first=Stefan|pages=57}}</ref> ''Tapothēkē {{IPA-cop|təpoˈtʰeːⲕə|pronounced}})'' is a city in the [[Asyut Governorate]] of [[Egypt]]. Located on the west bank of the [[Nile]], it has a population of 70,969 inhabitants (2006).

Abu Tig is the third largest city in Asyut Governorate in Egypt after [[Asyut]] itself and [[Dairut]]. Abu-Tig is very famous for herbs and botanicals all over Egypt. In the Ancient era, Abu-Tig was and still considered as the central hub for fresh and natural fruits and vegetables as well as herbs and spices. It is not only a commercial city but is famous for El Farghal Mosque as well. El Farghal Mosque, with its twin minarets, is the largest mosque in Upper Egypt. Many people come to Abu Tig every year in July to visit El Farghal Mosque and celebrate at the El Farghal fair.<ref name="El Farghal mosque">{{cite web|title=About Asyut City area and location|url=http://www.aun.edu.eg/assiut.html|website=aun edu|publisher=Asyut University}}</ref> During holidays and feasts, people from all over Upper Egypt come to Abu Tig to visit its zoo, called "Nasser Zoo".

==History==
Abu Tig is an ancient city distinguished by its ancient-times archaeology, including an obelisk with a depiction of Queen and Pharaoh [[Hatshepsut]].<ref name="Dean2017">{{cite book|author=Rebecca Angharad Dean|title=Warfare and Weaponry in Dynastic Egypt|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=r2jBDgAAQBAJ&pg=PP127|date=31 March 2017|publisher=Pen and Sword|isbn=978-1-4738-6205-0|pages=127–|url-status = live|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20171126034727/https://books.google.com/books?id=r2jBDgAAQBAJ&pg=PP127|archive-date=26 November 2017}}</ref> [[Henri Gauthier|Gauthier]] adds that its [[Ancient Egypt|ancient Egyptian]] name was "Pa-shna" which translates to "The store" where the Greeks translated the name to "Apotheke" ({{langx|grc|Ἀποθήκή}}) and hence came the modern name of the city, it was a place of worship of [[Hathor]].<ref>[[#Dictionnaire des Noms Géographiques Contenus dans les Textes Hiéroglyphiques Vol. 5|Gauthier (1928)]], p. 135</ref> Palaces and churches in Abu Tig date back to ancient Coptic,<ref name="Meinardus2006">{{cite book|author=Otto F. A. Meinardus|title=Christians In Egypt: Orthodox, Catholic, and Protestant Communities - Past and Present|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=LFbbAgAAQBAJ&pg=PT47|date=1 September 2006|publisher=American University in Cairo Press|isbn=978-1-61797-262-1|pages=47–|url-status = live|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20171126034727/https://books.google.com/books?id=LFbbAgAAQBAJ&pg=PT47|archive-date=26 November 2017}}</ref><ref name="Hanna1972">{{cite book|author=Sami A. Hanna|title=AZIZ SURYAL ATIYA|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=q9YUAAAAIAAJ&pg=PA322|date=1 January 1972|publisher=Brill Archive|isbn=90-04-03406-4|pages=322–|url-status = live|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20171126034728/https://books.google.com/books?id=q9YUAAAAIAAJ&pg=PA322|archive-date=26 November 2017}}</ref> and Islamic eras. During the [[Napoleonic Era]], tourists looking for fine belly-dancing could find belly-dancers in Abu Tig. A school for training belly-dancers existed there until public belly-dancing was banned.<ref name="Fraser2014">{{cite book|author=Kathleen W. Fraser|title=Before They Were Belly Dancers: European Accounts of Female Entertainers in Egypt, 1760–1870|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=6luXBQAAQBAJ&pg=PA53|date=25 November 2014|publisher=McFarland|isbn=978-1-4766-1916-3|pages=53–|url-status = live|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20171126034728/https://books.google.com/books?id=6luXBQAAQBAJ&pg=PA53|archive-date=26 November 2017}}</ref>  In the early 19th century slaves from Sudan were castrated near Abu Tig, mainly by Coptic priests.<ref name="Ayad2016">{{cite book|author=Mariam Ayad|title=Studies in Coptic Culture: Transmission and Interaction|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=3oUeDQAAQBAJ&pg=PA132|date=2 May 2016|publisher=American University in Cairo Press|isbn=978-1-61797-766-4|pages=132–|url-status = live|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20171126034727/https://books.google.com/books?id=3oUeDQAAQBAJ&pg=PA132|archive-date=26 November 2017}}</ref> Abu Tig has a number of bars that cater to tourists, including divers.<ref name="Fodor's2011">{{cite book|author=Fodor's|title=Fodor's Egypt|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=BAJhNElwjOUC&pg=PA377|year=2011|publisher=Fodor's Travel Publications|isbn=978-1-4000-0519-2|pages=377–|url-status = live|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20171126034727/https://books.google.com/books?id=BAJhNElwjOUC&pg=PA377|archive-date=2017-11-26}}</ref>

==Geography==
Abu Tig has many subordinate villages like: Baqor, Nazlat Baqor, El Felio, El Nekhila, Bani Semei, El Zayara, El Zawia, Dekran, El Zarabi, Dweina, Nazlat Dweina, Abu El Hareth, Abu Khors and El Balayza.
