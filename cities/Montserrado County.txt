'''Montserrado County''' is a [[Counties of Liberia|county]] in the northwestern portion of the [[West Africa]]n nation of [[Liberia]] containing its national capital, [[Monrovia]]. One of 15 counties that comprise the [[Administrative division|first-level of administrative division]] in the nation, it has 17 sub political [[Districts of Liberia|districts]]. As of the 2022 Census, it had a population of 1,920,914, making it the most populous county in Liberia.<ref>{{Cite web |title=Liberia: Counties, Major Cities, Towns & Urban Areas - Population Statistics, Maps, Charts, Weather and Web Information |url=https://www.citypopulation.de/en/liberia/ |access-date=2024-01-16 |website=www.citypopulation.de}}</ref><ref name="census2008">{{cite web|url=http://www.lisgis.net/pg_img/NPHC%202008%20Final%20Report.pdf|title=2008 Population and Housing Census Final Results|year=2008|publisher=Liberia Institute of Statistics and Geo-Information Services (LISGIS)|access-date=2017-08-17}}</ref> The area of the county measures {{convert|738.5|mi2}}, the smallest in the country.<ref name="Atlas">{{cite web|title=2008 Census Atlas|url=http://www.lisgis.net/pg_img/2008%20Census%20Atlas.pdf|website=lisgis.net/pg_img/|publisher=LIBERIA INSTITUTE OF STATISTICS AND GEO-INFORMATION SERVICES (LISGIS)|access-date=17 August 2017}}</ref> [[Bensonville]] serves as the capital.

Created in 1847 at the foundation of the Liberian republic, the county is the oldest in Liberia. Previously, it had been the site of the colony of Kentucky in Liberia. Montserrado’s County Superintendent is Nyenekon Beauty Snoh-Barcon.<ref name="census2008"/><ref name=cda>{{cite news|url=http://www.emansion.gov.lr/doc/MontserradoCDA.pdf|title=Montserrado County Development Agenda|year=2008|publisher=Republic of Liberia|access-date=2008-10-14}}</ref> The county is bordered by [[Bomi County]] to the west, [[Bong County]] to the north, and [[Margibi County]] to the east. The southern part of Montserrado lies on the [[Atlantic Ocean|Atlantic Coast]].

==Geography==
Located on the coast in the northwestern third of Liberia, Montserrado County is bordered by three counties.<ref name=cda/> The [[Atlantic Ocean]] makes up the county’s southern border, while [[Bomi County]] lies on the western border.<ref name=cda/> [[Bong County]] is to the north and [[Margibi County]] to the east.<ref name=cda/> The land is mainly alluvial soils, primarily clay, washed seaward from the streams and rivers of the interior valleys.<ref name=cda/> In the lowlands on the coast grow palm trees, mangrove woods, and savanna grasslands with tropical forest covering the interior hills and valleys.<ref name=cda/> Rivers include the [[Saint Paul River|St. Paul]], [[Mesurado River|Mesurado]], [[Du River|Du]], and [[Po River (Liberia)|Po]].<ref name=cda/>

The climate is tropical with dry and wet seasons.<ref name=cda/> Annual precipitation measures approximately {{convert|75|in|cm}}.<ref name=cda/> From May to November is the rainy season, followed by the dry season from December through April.<ref name=cda/> During the dry season winds from the [[Sahara Desert]] called the [[Harmattan]] create wild temperature fluctuations from December to the beginning of March.<ref name=cda/>

===Districts===
[[Careysburg District]] and [[Todee District]] are the two statutorily created districts in the county.<ref name=cda/> Commonwealth District, [[Greater Monrovia District]] and [[St. Paul River District]] are also recognized, but are not officially recognized as administrative districts.<ref name=cda/> There are 21 townships, seven cities, one borough, and two chiefdoms contained within these districts. Administration varies by subunit with a governor running the borough, mayors in charge of cities, commissioners administering townships, and superintendents controlling districts.<ref name=cda/>

Commonwealth's population is 11,876.<ref name="census2008"/>

Careysburg’s population is 29,712.<ref name="census2008"/> The main ethnic groups are Kpelle, Bassa, Mano, Kissi, Loma, and Gola.<ref name=cda/> Both the White Plains Water Treatment Plant and the [[Mount Coffee Hydropower Project]] are in the district.<ref name=cda/>

Todee District’s population is 33,998 with farming the primary economic activity.<ref name="census2008"/> The district is run by chiefdoms and clan systems, as the county contains a large number of native African communities.<ref name=cda/>

The most populous district in the county and the nation, Greater Monrovia District, is home to 970,824 people.<ref name="census2008"/> Employment is mainly informal, small-scale trade and government employment through the national government or foreign governments.<ref name=cda/> The population has members of each of Liberia’s 16 main tribes.<ref name=cda/>

St. Paul River District is the second most populous district in the county with 71,831 residents.<ref name="census2008"/> Residents are primarily Christians and a member of the Bassa, Dey, or Kpelle tribes.<ref name=cda/> Farming and fishing are the main economic activities along with some small-scale trading.<ref name=cda/> The district has one paved road.<ref name=cda/>

==Demographics==
Montserrado is the smallest county by size, but largest by population comprising approximately 33% of Liberia’s total population.<ref name="census2008"/> The population density is {{convert|599.7|PD/sqkm}}, the highest in Liberia.<ref name="census2008"/> The county had a population of 491,078 at the 1984 Census.<ref name="census2008"/> The average household size was 4.7 people, a decrease from 5.4 at the 1984 Census.<ref name=cda/> The county capital of [[Bensonville]] has a population of 4,089.<ref name="census2008"/>

Males outnumber females in the county with 585,833 to 558,973.<ref name="census2008"/> Christians compose an estimated 68.2% of the population, with Muslims totaling 31.8%.<ref name=cda/> All of Liberia’s 16 main tribal groups are represented in the ethnic make up of the county.<ref name=health/> [[Kpelle language|Kpelle speaking]] groups represent 52% of the population while [[Bassa language|Bassa speakers]] comprise 21%, followed by Lorma with 6%, Kru with 4%, and all others with 3% or less each.<ref name=cda/>

In the county 10% of the population was considered food insecure, with 35% listed as highly vulnerable, 43% as moderately vulnerable, and 13% were listed as food secure regarding access to sustenance as of October 2006.<ref name=cfsns2006>[https://web.archive.org/web/20110722150119/http://www.moa.gov.lr/doc/Liberia%20CFSNS_October%202006.pdf Comprehensive Food Security and Nutrition Survey (CFSNS).] Republic of Liberia. Monrovia, October 2006. Retrieved on October 17, 2008.</ref> Residents lived mainly in owner occupied dwellings, which made up 61% of households. Seventeen percent rented their homes with an average cost of 341 LD, and 22% lived rent free in their home.<ref name=cfsns2006/>

==Government==
Each county in Liberia is headed by a superintendent appointed by the [[President of Liberia|Liberian President]].<ref>[http://www.britannica.com/EBchecked/topic/339254/Liberia Liberia.] (2008). In Encyclopædia Britannica. Retrieved October 17, 2008, from Encyclopædia Britannica Online.</ref> Montserrado’s county superintendent is Beauty Barcon.<ref>Liberia Government. [http://allafrica.com/stories/200808210017.html Liberia: Town Hall Meetings Continue in Bentol City.] allAfrica.com. 20 August 2008.</ref> Other executive office include commissioners for districts and townships, line ministries, and an assistant superintendent that focuses on development.<ref name=cda/>  The county is divided into five districts, seven cities, one borough, twenty-one townships, seven clans, and two chiefdoms.<ref name=cda/> A district superintendent heads each of the two statutory districts.<ref name=cda/>

Judicially, the county has eight total courts between probate, justice of the peace, and magisterial courts.<ref name=cda/> The [[Law enforcement in Liberia|Liberian National Police]] have 844 officers spread across 33 stations in the county, as of October 2007.<ref name=cda/> Additionally, the National Police Training Academy is in Montserrado County in [[Paynesville, Liberia|Paynesville City]].<ref>{{cite news|title=Nine officials commissioned|date=October 11, 2008|work=The Analyst}}</ref> Other national government employees assigned to the county include tax collectors, revenue agents, and customs officials.<ref name=cda/>

===Flag===
[[Image:Flag of Montserrado County.svg|thumb|County flag]]
The county flag contains green, brown, blue, and red. Half is red and half is blue, split along a diagonal line running from the lower left-hand corner to the upper right-hand corner.<ref name=cda/> Blue comprises the top half and symbolizes that Montserrado was the first county.<ref name=cda/> Red is on the bottom and represents the bloodshed from wars between tribal members and the African-American settlers.<ref name=cda/> In the center lies a circle with trees which celebrate agriculture and the soil.<ref name=cda/> There is a small [[Flag of Liberia|Liberian]] flag in the upper left corner.

==Economy==
The county contains the most and the largest markets in the entire country.<ref name="lmr"/> One of these is the Red Light market in Paynesville where a variety of consumer goods are sold out of [[wheelbarrow]]s and old [[intermodal container]]s.<ref name="wapost">{{cite news|title=Liberia's Streets, Spirits Brighten; Four Years After War's End, Battered W. African Nation Begins a Slow Reawakening|last=Timberg|first=Craig|date=March 12, 2008|newspaper=[[The Washington Post]]|pages=A8}}</ref> Markets include daily markets, primarily for food, and weekly markets.<ref name="lmr"/> The Red Light market and the Duala markets serve as distribution markets for incoming goods from the rural parts of Liberia.<ref name="lmr"/>

Due to a lack of [[informal sector|formal employment]], many engage in informal businesses and trading.<ref name="lmr"/>  As of October 2006, employment in the county is primarily self-employment with only 17% of households having members that were salaried employees.<ref name=cfsns2006/> The largest income generation activity was through petty trade or small business, with 46% of households engaged in these activities.<ref name=cfsns2006/> This was followed by 25% for making charcoal, 19% for temporary employment, and 18% for [[palm oil]]/nut production and sales.<ref name=cfsns2006/> The national government is the county's single largest employer.<ref name=cda/>

[[Image:Monrovia Street.jpg|thumb|left|Cityscape of Monrovia]]
Agriculture is a small part of the economy, with the main crops consisting of cassava (90% of all crops), rice (16%), other vegetables (18%), corn (16%), sweet potatoes or eddoes (8%), plantain or bananas (8%), and pulses (1%).<ref name=cda/> Livestock is mainly pigs, chickens, and ducks.<ref name=cda/> Commercial crops grown include cocoa, coconuts, sugarcane, pineapple, kola nuts, palm oil, and rubber.<ref name=cda/> Some palm oil is produced on government-owned farms in Mt. Coffee and Fendell.<ref name=cda/>

Rubber plantations are located in the Todee and Careysburg districts.<ref name=cda/> These include the Morris American Rubber Company in Todee which employs 600 and the Liberia Resources Corporation in Careysburg with 300 employees.<ref name=cda/> Overall rubber production accounts for eight percent of household income in Montserrado County.<ref name=cda/> Small scale mineral extraction occurs for gold and diamonds. Other resource extraction activities include logging and fishing.<ref name=cda/>

The [[Central Bank of Liberia]] is located in Monrovia, with other commercial banks also operating in Montserrado County, including the [[International Bank (Liberia)|International Bank]].<ref name=cda/> The main port is the [[Freeport of Monrovia]], the country's busiest port.<ref name="phones">{{cite news|title=Liberia; Over 200,000 Acquire GSM Phones|last=Liberian Observer|date=June 5, 2006|work=Africa News}}</ref> The port has one wharf and four piers, though one pier cannot be used due to a sunken ship.<ref name="phones"/> Air travel available at [[Spriggs Payne Airport]] in Monrovia and [[Roberts International Airport]] in Robertsfield.<ref name="phones"/> Roberts is the only international airport in Liberia, and Payne along with Roberts have the only paved runways in the nation.<ref name="phones"/> Limited public transportation is available in the capital via the [[Monrovia Transit Authority]]. Passenger and freight rail service is available from the private company Geoservices between Monrovia and the Bong mines.<ref name="lmr"/>

==Education==
The county had a total of 947 students enrolled in school in 1910.<ref>Starr, p. 162.</ref>
In 1948, the missionaries from the Pentecostal faith opened a school in Mein Clan in Todee District.<ref name=cda/> Additional primary schools were built in the 1970s, while in 1976 the University of Liberia opened the Fendall branch campus in Louisiana.<ref name=cda/> Bentol City added a high school in 1978.<ref name=cda/> The [[University of Liberia]]’s main campus is located in Monrovia and includes the country’s only law school in the [[Louis Arthur Grimes School of Law]].<ref>Jallah, David A. B. [http://www.ialsnet.org/meetings/enriching/JallahDavid.pdf “Notes, Presented by Professor and Dean of the Louis Arthur Grimes School of Law, University of Liberia, David A. B. Jallah to the International Association of Law Schools Conference Learning From Each Other: Enriching the Law School Curriculum in an Interrelated World Held at Soochow University Kenneth Wang School of Law, Suzhou, China, October 17&ndash;19, 2007.”] International Association of Law Schools. Retrieved on September 1, 2008.</ref>

Enrollment in primary schools in the county totaled 314,409 students, which was 35% of the total number for the nation as a whole.<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.poledakar.org/IMG/Liberia_EFA-FTI.pdf|title=Liberian Primary Education Recovery Program|date=March 30, 2007|publisher=Liberia Ministry of Education|pages=13|access-date=2008-10-21}} {{Dead link|date=September 2010|bot=H3llBot}}</ref> Students attended a total of 1,096 schools in the county.<ref name=cda/> Thirty-three percent of county residents had no formal education, while 27% had attended some elementary school.<ref name=cfsns2006/> Seven percent completed elementary school, 19% had some high school education, and 11% completed high school.<ref name=cfsns2006/> One percent of residents completed college, attended college, or have received a vocational education.<ref name=cfsns2006/> Enrollment for school-age children is 70%, with 76% for males and 65% for females.<ref name=cfsns2006/>

==Culture==
The [[Liberian National Museum]] is located in Monrovia, while the city also hosts two football stadiums, the [[National Complex (Liberia)|National Complex]] and [[Antoinette Tubman Stadium]].<ref>{{cite news|title=Rename soccer stadiums after Wannie Bo-Toe and George Oppong Weah|last=Sungbeh|first=Tewroh-Wehtoe|date=August 13, 2008|work=The News}}</ref> Media include [[STAR radio]], [[The Daily Talk]] news boards, and [[The Analyst (Liberia)|''The Analyst'']] newspaper.<ref name=pul>"Liberia: Press Union names Star Radio as radio station of year", BBC Monitoring Africa, July 30, 2008. PoliticalSupplied by BBC Worldwide Monitoring.</ref>

The majority of the county’s population is Christian.<ref name=cda/> with the [[Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Monrovia]] serving as the head of the Catholic Church.

==Infrastructure==
The Liberia Water and Sewer Corporation provides water to some parts of the county.<ref name=cda/> Prior to the civil wars it used water from the [[Mount Coffee Hydropower Project]] to supply water and some sewage disposal.<ref name=cda/> There are approximately 13,000 wells in the county and 518 hand pumps.<ref name=cda/> In health care, the county has eight [[hospital]]s, nine health centers, and approximately 93 medical clinics functioning as of 2008.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://liberiamohsw.org/County_Health_Plans_files/Montserrado%20County%20Template%202007-08.xls|title=Montserrado County Template 2007-08|last=Man|first=Yue|publisher=Liberia Ministry of Health and Social Welfare|access-date=2008-10-21}} {{Dead link|date=September 2010|bot=H3llBot}}</ref> Montserrado County has limited access to electricity, with the majority of power being generated by privately owned generators.<ref name="phones"/><ref>{{cite news|title=Liberia; U.S. to Debate New 'Seeds' for Development |date=July 30, 2008|work=Africa News|publisher=allAfrica.com}}</ref> The county is not served by any public communications, though cellular communications are available through four private companies.<ref name="phones"/> [[Ashmun Street]] is the main street in Monrovia, while the [[People’s Bridge]] crosses the [[Mesurado River]].

==History==
[[Image:Monrovia Plan Map 1830.jpg|thumb|right|Early plan for Monrovia]]
On December 11, 1821, officials from the United States arrived aboard the vessel ''Alligator'' under the command of Captain Robert F. Stockton at Mesurado Bay.<ref name=starr>{{cite book|last=Starr|first=Frederick|title=Liberia: Description, History, Problems|location=Chicago|year=1913|url=https://archive.org/details/bub_gb_KjwUAAAAIAAJ|quote=history Liberia monrovia.|page=[https://archive.org/details/bub_gb_KjwUAAAAIAAJ/page/n76 57]&ndash;58}}</ref> Stockton and Dr. Eli Ayers negotiated to acquire the land in and around the bay from the native chiefs for a settlement by free people of color before sailing to Sierra Leone to pick up these colonists.<ref name=starr/>

On January 7, 1822, free people of color arrived and settled on Providence Island on the [[Mesurado River]] under the auspices of the [[American Colonization Society]] (ACS), and by April they had moved to the mainland. A [[Kentucky]] state affiliate of the ACS was formed in 1828, and members raised money to transport Kentucky blacks—[[freeborn]] volunteers as well as slaves set free on the stipulation that they leave the [[United States]]—to Africa.<ref name=ket>[https://www.ket.org/program/kentucky-in-africa/ "Kentucky in Africa" (special edition of ''Kentucky Life'')], [[Kentucky Educational Television]] (Aug. 15, 2005).</ref> The Kentucky society bought a {{convert|40|sqmi|km2|adj=on}} site along the [[Saint Paul River]] and named it [[Kentucky in Africa]].<ref name=ket/> [[Clay-Ashland]] was the colony's main town.<ref name=ket/>

Within the next two decades, approximately 3,200 free people of color arrived in the Montserrado County area from [[Trinidad and Tobago]], [[Saint Vincent and the Grenadines]], [[Saint Kitts and Nevis]], [[Grenada]], [[Saint Lucia]], and [[Jamaica]]. As opportunities increased, these people moved through [[Grand Cape Mount]], [[Bomi County]], [[Montserrado]], and [[Margibi County]] to seek employment and prosperity.<ref name=starr/> Many of the communities in the county are named for the pioneers who settled the area and their former homes in America.<ref name=cda/>

''The Liberian Herald'' began printing in 1830 in Monrovia and was the first newspaper in Liberia.<ref name=starr2>Starr, p. 71&ndash;73.</ref> In 1832, the Dey-Golah War erupted between the colonists and the native tribes.<ref name=starr2/> There was one battle, with the colonists from Monrovia defeating the Dey and Golah (Gola) combined group.<ref name=starr2/> In 1847, the colony declared its independence and Montserrado was the first of the Republic of Liberia's counties to sign the Declaration of Independence on July 26, 1847.<ref name=cda/> At the time of creation, the county was composed of three administrative districts in Bomi, Gibi, and Marshall with Monrovia as the county capital.<ref name=cda/><ref name=health>{{cite web|url=http://liberiamohsw.org/County_Health_Plans_files/Montserrado%20County%20Health%20Plan%20Narrative%20Revised.doc|title=Montserrado County Health Plan 2007/2008|date=5 October 2007|publisher=Ministry of Health & Social Welfare: Republic of Liberia|access-date=2008-10-21}} {{Dead link|date=September 2010|bot=H3llBot}}</ref> In 1885, the first municipal water supply system was completed in Monrovia.<ref>[http://www.lr.undp.org/State%20of%20the%20environment%20report%20final.pdf First State of the Environment Report for Liberia – 2006.] {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20090411035426/http://www.lr.undp.org/State%20of%20the%20environment%20report%20final.pdf |date=2009-04-11 }} United Nations Development Programme. 2006, p. 39. Retrieved on October 19, 2008.</ref>

The first County Inspector was appointed in 1949 by [[William Tubman|President Tubman]], followed by [[William R. Tolbert, Jr.|President Tolbert’s]] appointment of the first County Superintendent in 1973.<ref name=cda/> A railroad was constructed in the mid-1960s from Monrovia's port to Todee District by the Bong Mining Company.<ref name="lmr">{{cite web|url=http://home.wfp.org/stellent/groups/public/documents/ena/wfp188456.pdf|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110728174545/http://home.wfp.org/stellent/groups/public/documents/ena/wfp188456.pdf|url-status=dead|archive-date=2011-07-28|title=Liberia Market Review|date=July 2007|publisher=Republic of Liberia|access-date=2008-10-20}}</ref> The [[Mount Coffee Hydropower Project]]'s first phase was completed in 1966.<ref>[http://www.lr.undp.org/State%20of%20the%20environment%20report%20final.pdf First State of the Environment Report for Liberia – 2006.] {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20090411035426/http://www.lr.undp.org/State%20of%20the%20environment%20report%20final.pdf |date=2009-04-11 }} United Nations Development Programme. 2006, pp. 44&ndash;45. Retrieved on October 19, 2008.</ref> Rubber and palm oil plantations were started in the 1960s and 1970s, while a factory for producing clothing was built in 1979 in Bentol City.<ref name=cda/>

In 1974, the county capital was moved by President Tolbert from Monrovia to his hometown of Bensonville.<ref name=health/> In 1976, an Assistant Superintendent was added to focus on development in Montserrado County.<ref name=cda/> Bomi Territory in the western portion of the county became Bomi County in 1983.<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.emansion.gov.lr/doc/BomiCDA.pdf|title=Bomi County Development Agenda|year=2008|publisher=Republic of Liberia|access-date=2008-10-21}}</ref> In 1984, Marshall and Gibi territories were combined and became neighboring Margibi County to the east.<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.emansion.gov.lr/doc/MargibiCDA.pdf|title=Margibi County Development Agenda|year=2008|publisher=Republic of Liberia|access-date=2008-10-21}}</ref>

[[Virginia, Liberia|Virginia]] saw the construction of the [[Unity Conference Center]] and Hotel Africa in 1979.<ref>{{cite news|title=Liberia; Looters Take Charge of Hotel Africa|date=February 26, 2008|work=The Inquirer|publisher=Africa News}}</ref> During the civil strife of the 1980s into the early 2000s much of the county’s infrastructure was looted and left unmaintained.<ref name=cda/> In July 2008, parts of the county received the worst flooding in its recorded history.<ref>UN Integrated Regional Information Networks. [http://en.ismico.org/content/view/698/15/ Liberia: Govt warns Monrovia floods worst in city's history.] {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20090108083025/http://en.ismico.org/content/view/698/15/ |date=2009-01-08 }} Missionari della Consolata. 23 July 2008.</ref>
