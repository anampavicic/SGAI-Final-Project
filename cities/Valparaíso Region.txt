The '''Valparaíso Region''' ({{langx|es|Región de Valparaíso|links=no}}, {{IPA|es|balpaɾaˈiso|pron}}) is one of [[Chile]]'s 16 [[Regions of Chile|first order administrative divisions]].<ref group=FN>Valparaíso Region, 2006</ref> With the country's second-highest population of 1,790,219 {{As of|2017|lc=y}}, and fourth-smallest area of {{cvt|16396.1|sqkm|0}}, the region is Chile's second most densely populated after the [[Santiago Metropolitan Region]] to the southeast.<ref name="CINVER-VS"/> The region also includes Chile's remote islands of the [[Pacific Ocean]], including [[Rapa Nui]] and the [[Juan Fernandez Islands]].

Its capital is the port city of [[Valparaíso]]; other important cities include [[Viña del Mar]], [[Quillota]], [[San Felipe, Chile|San Felipe]], [[Quilpué]], [[Villa Alemana]], and [[San Antonio, Chile|San Antonio]].

== Administration ==
As a region, Valparaíso is a first-level administrative division. Since 2021, the region is governed by the governor, who is elected by popular vote. The current governor is [[Rodrigo Mundaca]] ([[Broad Front (Chilean political coalition)|Broad Front]]).

==Geography and natural features==
[[File:Cerro Tres Hermanos.jpg|thumb|left|210px|[[Nevado Juncal]].]]
The region is on the same latitude as the [[Santiago Metropolitan Region]]. Its capital is [[Valparaíso]], which is the site for the [[National Congress of Chile]] and an important commercial port. Also in this region is the top resort city of [[Viña del Mar, Chile|Viña del Mar]]. Additionally, the Pacific islands of [[Easter Island]], [[Isla Salas y Gómez]], the [[Juan Fernández Islands]] and the [[Desventuradas Islands]] fall under the Valparaíso Region's administration.

The Valparaíso Region is part of the very restricted range of the [[endangered]] [[Chilean Wine Palm|Chilean wine palm]] (''Jubaea chilensis''); in prehistoric times, this [[endemism|endemic]] Chilean tree had a significantly larger range.<ref group=FN>C. Michael Hogan, 2008</ref>

==Demographics==
The Valparaíso Region is populated by 1.71 million inhabitants. The population density reached 94.1 inhabitants/km<sup>2</sup>. 91.6% of the population lives in urban areas and only 8.4% of the population lives in rural areas.{{citation needed|date=August 2019}}

The most populous municipalities in the region are Valparaíso, with 308,000 inhabitants and [[Viña del Mar]], with 287,000 inhabitants, which together with [[Villa Alemana]], [[Quilpué]] and [[Concón]] form the [[Gran Valparaíso|Greater Valparaíso]], a continuum of 1.75 million people. There are also [[Quillota]], with about 201,000 inhabitants and [[San Antonio, Chile|San Antonio]] with more than 200,000 inhabitants with estimates at 250,000 to be the region's second-largest city. {{Citation needed|date=September 2010}}{{When|date=August 2019}}

Valparaíso developed as a trans-oceanic rest stop for fishing ships, sea cruise-liners, and international naval ships. Therefore, a large proportion of residents have a variety of national origins, ethnic groups, and cultures.<ref>{{Cite web |url=http://www.memoriachilena.gob.cl/602/w3-article-7670.html |title=Valparaíso (1820–1920) – Memoria Chilena |access-date=4 April 2020 |archive-date=30 April 2020 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20200430100215/http://www.memoriachilena.gob.cl/602/w3-article-7670.html |url-status=live }}</ref> The [[Spanish immigration to Chile|16th-century colonial population was founded by male settlers]] from the Spanish regions of [[Andalusia]], [[Asturia]] and [[Kingdom of León|León]], and the large [[Basque people|Basque contingent]] has given rise to a substantial [[Basque Chilean]] population. Large numbers came from other countries of Latin America from Mexico to Uruguay, esp. came during colonial rule in the 17th century. And in the late 18th–early 19th centuries came a small wave of [[Galician people|Galician]] settlers from the Spanish region of [[Galicia (Spain)|Galicia]].

It is thought{{By whom|date=September 2010}} the majority of Valparaíso's people have some [[Immigration to Chile|non-Spanish European background]], such as: British and Irish, Australians and New Zealanders, North Americans (U.S. or Canadian), Croats and Bosnians, Dutch and Belgians, French, Germans, Greeks, Italians, Portuguese and Scandinavians. Also, there are more or less [[Jewish assimilation|assimilated groups]] of [[History of the Jews in Chile|Chilean Jews (mostly Ashkenazi)]], as well as [[Christian emigration|Christians from the Ottoman Empire]], primarily [[Arab Chileans|Syro-Lebanese]] and a large [[Palestinian community in Chile|Palestinian community]] in the town of [[La Calera, Chile|La Calera]].

In racial terms, the majority of Valparaíso's inhabitants are [[castizos]], meaning that their paternal origins are overwhelmingly from white whalers, settlers and traders of various European nationalities, including colonial Spanish settlers, while their maternal origins usually stems from unions between [[Criollo people|colonial Spanish men]] and local indigenous women, including those of [[Mapuche]], [[Inca]], [[Aymara people|Aymara]] and [[North American Indian]] descent (transplanted [[Cherokee]]s are reported to come in the late 19th century, though it could well be a myth). Smaller numbers of [[East Asian people|East Asians]], mostly [[Chinese people in Chile|Chinese]], [[Japanese Chileans|Japanese]] or [[Koreans in Chile|Korean]], minuscule numbers of [[Afro-Chilean]]s, as well as a component of [[Polynesians]] [[Blackbirding|whose ancestors were kidnapped]] from [[Easter Island]] and the [[Marquesas Islands]], further added to the region's [[Hispanicized]] [[melting pot]].

==Economics and industry==
The Valparaíso Region is a host of agricultural lands, wine producers, and industrial activity such as copper mining and cement. Chile's largest oil refinery is located in [[Concón]] (on the mouth of the [[Aconcagua River]] and about {{cvt|20|km|mi|sp=us}} north of Valparaíso) and there are two important copper ore refineries: the state-owned [[Ventanas, Chile|Ventanas]] (on the coast and north of Concón) and the private works in [[Chagres, Chile|Chagres]], about {{cvt|55|mi|km|sp=us}} inland.

The region also is a hub for chemicals and gas storage near the port of [[Quintero]]. In the interior valleys, there is a booming export industry, mainly around the avocado (palta), chirimoyas and flowers. The most striking recent development has been the cultivation of hillsides using high-tech drip feed irrigation. This has allowed otherwise dry and unproductive land to bear high yields.
