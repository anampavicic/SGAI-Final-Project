'''Zinder Region''' is one of the seven [[regions of Niger]]; the capital of the region is [[Zinder]]. The region covers 145,430&nbsp;km². It is the most populous province of Niger.

==History==
Numerous Palaeolithic and Neolithic remains, as well as cave paintings, have been found in the [[Termit Massif]].<ref name="Bradt"/>

Zinder was the centre of the [[Sultanate of Damagaram]], a powerful sultanate which dominated much of the surrounding region from the mid-18th century until the French conquest in the 1890s.<ref>Abdourahmane Idrissa & Samuel Decalo, "Damagaram, Sultanate of", in ''Historical Dictionary of Niger'', pp. 160-161</ref> Zinder was initially the capital of the Niger territory, however this was moved to [[Niamey]] in 1926 and thereafter Zinder declined in importance, though it remains an important regional centre.<ref name="Bradt"/>

==Geography==
[[File:Termit2.jpg|thumb|left|The Termit Massif in northern Zinder]]
Zinder Region is located in the southeast of Niger and covers 145,430&nbsp;km². It borders [[Agadez Region]] to the north, [[Diffa Region]] to the east, [[Nigeria]] to the south (specifically, the states of [[Yobe State|Yobe]], [[Jigawa State|Jigawa]] and [[Katsina State|Katsina]]), and [[Maradi Region]] to the west. The landscape is primarily [[Sahelian]] in the south, merging into the [[Sahara]] desert in the north of the region. The terrain is predominantly flat, except for the [[Koutous Hills]], which lie north of [[Kelle, Niger|Kelle]], and the [[Termit Massif]] in the far north of the region.<ref name="Bradt">Geels, Jolijn, (2006) ''Bradt Travel Guide - Niger'', pgs. 213-26</ref>

===Administrative subdivisions===
[[Image:Zinder Arrondissements.png|thumb|right|250px|Departments of Zinder]]
Zinder was divided into 5 [[Departments of Niger|Department]]s:
* [[Goure Department]]
* [[Magaria Department]]
* [[Matameye Department]]
* [[Mirriah Department]]
* [[Tanout Department]]

Of the 27 administrative stations (postes administratifs) of Niger which were set out in a law dated 1 August 2011 to become departments and for which the appointment of prefects on 29 February 2012 completed the conversion, the following 5 are in Zinder region.<ref>{{cite web | url =http://www.statoids.com/yne.html | title =Departments of Niger | publisher = Statoids | access-date = 21 August 2020 }}</ref>
* [[Belbédji Department]]
* [[Damagaram Takaya Department]]
* [[Dungass Department]]
* [[Takeita Department|Takiéta Department]]
* [[Tesker|Tesker Department]]

So, Zinder now has 10 departments and the city of Zinder.<ref>{{cite web | url =https://citypopulation.de/php/niger-zinder.php | title =Niger Zinder Region - Departments | publisher = City Population | access-date = 21 August 2020 }}</ref>

==Demographics==
[[File:Nomadic woman Niger.jpg|thumb|Nomads in Zinder region]]

As of 2012 the population of the Region was 3,539,764.<ref name="Annuaire statistique du Niger">[http://www.stat-niger.org/statistique/file/Annuaires_Statistiques/Annuaire_ins_2011/population.pdf Annuaire statistique du Niger]</ref> The main ethnolinguistic groups are various [[Arab]] groups, [[Fulani people|Fulani]], [[Hausa people|Hausa]], [[Kanuri people|Kanuri]], [[Dazaga]] [[Toubou]] and [[Tuareg]] groups such as the [[Air Tamajeq language|Tayart Tamajeq]].<ref name="Languages of Niger">{{cite web | url = https://www.ethnologue.com/country/ne/languages| title = Languages of Niger | publisher = [[Ethnologue]] | access-date = 24 October 2019}}</ref> The [[Tagdal]] language, thought to be a mixed [[Songhai languages|Songhay]]-Tuareg language, is also spoken.<ref name="Languages of Niger"/>{{Historical populations|1977|1,002,222|1985|1,411,061|2001|2,080,250|2012|3,539,764|align=none|footnote=source:<ref>[http://www.citypopulation.de/en/niger/cities/ Niger: Administrative Division population statistics]</ref>}}

== Economy ==
The region was the poorest in Niger and had the highest incidence of [[extreme poverty]] (60% of the population) according to [[World Bank]] data from 2018.<ref>{{Cite web |title=Geospatial Poverty Portal: Interactive Maps |url=https://pipmaps.worldbank.org/en/data/datatopics/poverty-portal/poverty-interactivemap |access-date=2024-01-22 |website=World Bank |language=en}}</ref> The region was also hit hard by the [[2005–2006 Niger food crisis]].
