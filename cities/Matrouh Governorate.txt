'''Matrouh Governorate''' ({{langx|ar|محافظة مطروح}} {{transl|ar|''Muḥāfaẓat Maṭrūḥ''}}) is one of the [[governorates of Egypt]]. Located in the north-western part of the country, it borders [[Libya]]. Its capital is [[Mersa Matruh]].

==Overview==
The interior of the Matrouh Governorate is part of Egypt's [[Western Desert (Egypt)|Western Desert]], including the [[Siwa Oasis]], in antiquity known for its shrine to [[Amun]]. In the center of the Governorate is the [[Qattara Depression]], descending to 133 metres below sea level.

Marsa Matrouh is the ancient {{langx|grc-x-koine|Παραιτόνιον}} ''Paraitónion'', [[Latin]] [[wikt:Paraetonium|''Paraetonium'']]. It was the westernmost city of the [[Ptolemaic Kingdom]] in the [[Hellenistic period]]. The city of [[Apis (city)|Apis]], some 18&nbsp;km to the west of Paraetonium, marked the boundary to Libycus [[nome (Egypt)|nome]], and the [[Halfaya Pass]] (at [[Sallum]]) marked the boundary to [[Marmarica]] proper.

Matrouh Governorate contains many historical sites related to [[World War II]]. The latter include [[el Alamein]], which comprises cemeteries of fallen soldiers from [[Axis powers|Axis]] and [[Allies of World War II|Allied]] forces. An estimated 16 million mines,<ref>[https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1Y2-akaMNdU خارج القاهرة: ربع مليون فدان آلغام بمطروح ] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160330015834/https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1Y2-akaMNdU# |date=2016-03-30 }} - ONTV - 13 Jan 2012 {{in lang|ar}}</ref> planted by the Europeans during the world wars and called "[[devil's gardens]]", still hinder the development of most of the governorate, and are constantly being removed.

==Population==
According to population estimates, in 2015 the majority of residents in the governorate lived in urban areas, with an [[urbanization]] rate of 70.6%. Out of an estimated 447,846 people residing in the governorate, 316,005 people lived in urban areas as opposed to only 131,841 in rural areas.<ref name="pop">{{cite web|title=Population Estimates By Sex & Governorate 1/1/2015|url=http://www.msrintranet.capmas.gov.eg/pdf/EgyptinFigures2015/EgyptinFigures/Tables/PDF/1-%20%D8%A7%D9%84%D8%B3%D9%83%D8%A7%D9%86/pop.pdf|website=CAPMAS|access-date=23 October 2016|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20151019093240/http://www.msrintranet.capmas.gov.eg/pdf/EgyptinFigures2015/EgyptinFigures/Tables/PDF/1-%20%D8%A7%D9%84%D8%B3%D9%83%D8%A7%D9%86/pop.pdf|archive-date=2015-10-19|url-status=dead}}</ref>
