'''Occitania'''<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.tourism-occitania.co.uk/home/discover/occitania-in-a-nutshell/the-best-of-occitania|title=Presentation of the Occitania / Pyrenees-Mediterranean region|website=www.tourism-occitania.co.uk}}</ref> ({{langx|fr|Occitanie}} {{IPA|fr|ɔksitani||LL-Q150 (fra)-WikiLucas00-Occitanie.wav}}; {{langx|oc|Occitània}} {{IPA|oc|utsiˈtanjɔ|}}; {{langx|ca|Occitània}} {{IPA|ca|uksiˈtaniə|}}) is the southernmost [[Regions of France|administrative region]] of [[metropolitan France]] excluding [[Corsica]], created on 1 January 2016 from the former regions of [[Languedoc-Roussillon]] and [[Midi-Pyrénées]]. The [[Conseil d'État (France)|Council of State]] approved Occitania as the new name of the region on 28 September 2016, coming into effect on 30 September 2016.<ref name="Decree confirming new region name">{{cite French decree|number or usual name=n° 2016-1264|date in French=28 septembre 2016|full name=portant fixation du nom et du chef-lieu de la région Occitanie|article=|articles=|language=French|lower case=|url=https://www.legifrance.gouv.fr/affichTexte.do?cidTexte=JORFTEXT000033161437&dateTexte=&categorieLien=id}}</ref>

The modern administrative region is named after the larger cultural and historical region of [[Occitania]], which corresponds with the southern third of France. The region of Occitania as it is today covers a territory similar to that ruled by the [[Count of Toulouse|Counts of Toulouse]] in the 12th and 13th centuries. The banner of arms of the Counts of Toulouse, known colloquially as the [[Occitan cross]], is used by the modern region and is also a popular cultural symbol. In 2021, Occitania had a population of 6,022,176.

==Toponymy==
[[File:France 1154-en.svg|thumb|left|The County of Toulouse in 1154 (shown in blue)]]
Enacted in 2014, the territorial reform of French regions had been subject to debate for many years.<ref>{{cite news |url=http://www.lemonde.fr/carte-departements/ |title=Résultats élections Régionales 2015 |date=14 December 2015 |agency=[[Agence France-Presse]] |work=[[Le Monde]] |language=fr |access-date=14 December 2015 }}</ref> The reform law used as the new region's provisional name, the [[Double-barrelled name|hyphenated]] names of its predecessors: [[Languedoc-Roussillon]] and [[Midi-Pyrénées]], in alphabetical order. As for most of the merged regions, a permanent name was then proposed by the new [[Regional council (France)|regional council]] to replace that provisional name.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://france3-regions.blog.francetvinfo.fr/le-blog-de-viure-al-pais-france3/2016/06/04/nom-region-lrmp-quelques-jours-pour-se-mobiliser-des-decennies-pour-durer.html |title=Nom Région LRMP : Quelques jours pour Se mobiliser, des décennies pour durer |work=Le Blog De L’occitan / Lo Blòg Occitan |publisher=[[France 3]] |date=14 May 2016 |access-date=10 August 2016}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://france3-regions.blog.francetvinfo.fr/midi-pyrenees-politiques-france3/2016/06/17/nom-region-lrmp-occitanie-cest-valide-carole-delga-soccupe-des-catalans.html |title=Nom Région LRMP : Occitanie c'est validé, Carole Delga s'occupe des Catalans |website=Le Blog Politique |publisher=[[France 3]] |date=17 June 2016 |access-date=10 August 2016}}</ref><ref name="reform law">{{cite French law|number or usual name=n° 2015-29|date in French=16 janvier 2015|full name=Relative à la délimitation des régions, aux élections régionales et départementales et modifiant le calendrier électoral |language=fr |url=http://www.legifrance.gouv.fr/affichTexte.do;jsessionid=9FECBA9D9314D1D2C093CF793C886ED5.tpdila21v_1?idSectionTA=JORFSCTA000030109623&cidTexte=JORFTEXT000030109622&dateTexte=29990101}}</ref> On 24 June 2016, the Regional Council of Languedoc-Roussillon-Midi-Pyrénées adopted the name Occitania after lengthy public consultation.<ref name="regionlrmp1">{{cite web|url=http://www.regionlrmp.fr/le-nom-de-ma-region |title=Le nom de ma région : Occitanie - La nouvelle Région - Région Occitanie / Pyrénées-Méditerranée |website=Regionlrmp.fr |date=9 March 2016 |access-date=10 August 2016}}</ref> The provisional name of the region was withdrawn on 30 September 2016, when the new name took effect.<ref name="Decree confirming new region name"/>

Occitania, the new name, derives from the historical appellation of the broader region, and refers to the historical use throughout that territory of the [[Occitan language]] and its various dialects, which are so named for the word {{lang|oc|òc}}, the Occitan word for {{lang|fr|oui}} or "yes". The circa 450,000 French Catalans living in the region (or [[Northern Catalonia|Catalans of the North]], as they mostly call themselves) expressed dismay at the regional assembly resolution, regarding the new name as ignoring their presence.<ref>{{cite web |url=https://www.nytimes.com/2016/09/09/world/europe/occitanie-france-catalans.html |title="Don't Erase Us": French Catalans Fear Losing More Than a Region's Name |last=Minder |first=Raphael |date= 8 September 2016 |website=www.nytimes.com |publisher=[[The New York Times Company]] |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20170208212519/https://www.nytimes.com/2016/09/09/world/europe/occitanie-france-catalans.html |archive-date=8 February 2017 |url-status=live |access-date=6 November 2017 |quote=We are the Catalans of the North and we want to continue to exist as such.}}</ref> On 10 September 2016, some 10,000 people (7,800 according to the police) demonstrated in [[Perpignan]], demanding that the merged region name contain the words {{lang|fr|Pays catalan}} (literal translation: "Catalan country").<ref>{{cite web |url=https://www.yahoo.com/news/thousands-hold-pro-catalan-rally-southern-france-171817192.html |title=Thousands hold pro Catalan rally in southern France |date= 10 September 2016 |website= www.yahoo.com
 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160911213010/https://www.yahoo.com/news/thousands-hold-pro-catalan-rally-southern-france-171817192.html |archive-date= 11 September 2016 |url-status=live |access-date= 6 November 2017 |quote=Organisers said as many as 10,000 people gathered -- police put the figure at some 7,800 people -- to demand their newly-merged region contain the words "{{lang|fr|Pays catalan|italics=no}}" (Catalan land).}}</ref>

==Geography==
Occitania is the second-largest region in mainland France, covering an area of {{convert|72724|km2|abbr=on}}; it has a population of 5,845,102 (2017).<ref name="Population">{{cite web |url=https://www.insee.fr/fr/statistiques/1405599?geo=REG-76 |title=Comparateur de territoire: Région d'Occitanie (76) |work=[[Insee]] |access-date=11 September 2020 }}</ref> It has a [[Mediterranean Sea|Mediterranean]] coast on the southeast and is neighboured by [[Provence-Alpes-Côte d'Azur]] on the east, [[Auvergne-Rhône-Alpes]] on the northeast and [[Nouvelle-Aquitaine]] on the west and northwest, as well as foreign borders of [[Andorra]] ([[Canillo]], [[Encamp]], [[La Massana]], [[Ordino]]) and [[Spain]] ([[Aragon]] and [[Catalonia]]) on the south.

==Economy==
The [[gross domestic product]] (GDP) of the region was 171.2 billion euros in 2018, accounting for 7.3% of French economic output. GDP per capita adjusted for purchasing power was 26,000 euros or 86% of the EU27 average in the same year. The GDP per employee was 102% of the [[European Union|EU]] average.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://ec.europa.eu/eurostat/documents/2995521/10474907/1-05032020-AP-EN.pdf/81807e19-e4c8-2e53-c98a-933f5bf30f58|title=Regional GDP per capita ranged from 30% to 263% of the EU average in 2018|website=Eurostat}}</ref>

==Politics==
Following the creation of the region in 2016, [[Carole Delga]], a member of the [[Socialist Party (France)|Socialist Party]], was elected to the [[List of current presidents of the regional councils of France and the Corsican Assembly|presidency]] of the [[Regional Council of Occitania]]. The {{lang|fr|Hôtel de région}}, where the Regional Council of Occitania meets, is situated in [[Toulouse]].

The new administrative region includes provinces and territories of diverse cultural and historical origin: [[Languedoc]] (High and Low Languedoc), [[Països Catalans]] ([[Roussillon]], [[Cerdanya]], [[Vallespir]], [[Conflent]], [[Capcir]]), the [[County of Foix]], and the eastern parts of what was formerly [[Gascony]] ([[Armagnac (province)|Armagnac]], [[Comminges]], [[Couserans]], [[County of Bigorre|Bigorre]], [[Condom, Gers|Condomois]], [[Nébouzan]], [[Rivière-Verdun]]), and [[Guyenne|Guiana]] ([[Quercy|Carcin]], [[Rouergue|Roergue]]). During the [[Ancien Régime]], most of these territories lay within the jurisdiction of the [[Parlement of Toulouse]], founded in 1443.

====Languedoc====
[[File:Blason Languedoc.svg|thumb|right|upright|The blazon of the counts of Toulouse and Languedoc: the [[Occitan cross]] gold on gules.]]

Most of the territory that came to be called [[Languedoc]] (the region where {{lang|fr|[[Occitan language|langue d'oc]]}} is spoken; in [[Occitan language|Occitan]] {{lang|ich|Lengadòc}}, {{IPA-oc|ˌleŋɡɔˈðɔ(k)|pron}}) became attached to the [[Kingdom of France]] in the 13th century, following the [[Albigensian Crusade]] (1208–1229). This crusade aimed to put an end to what the Church considered the [[Catharism|Cathar heresy]], and enabled the [[Capetian dynasty]] to extend its influence south of the [[Loire]]. As part of this process, the former principalities of [[Trencavel]] (the Viscounty of [[Albi]], [[Carcassonne|Carcassona]], [[Béziers|Besièrs]], [[Agde]] and [[Nîmes|Nimes]]) were integrated into the [[Crown lands of France|Royal French Domain]] in 1224. The [[Counts of Toulouse]] followed them in 1271. The remaining feudal enclaves were absorbed progressively up to the beginning of the 16th century; the [[County of Gévaudan]] in 1258, the [[County of Melgueil]] ([[Mauguio|Mauguiò]]) in 1293, the [[Lordship of Montpellier]] in 1349 and the [[Viscounts of Narbonne]] in 1507.

The territory falling within the jurisdiction of the [[Estates of Languedoc]], which convened for the first time in 1346, shrank progressively, becoming known during the [[Ancien Régime]] as the [[Provinces of France|province of Languedoc]].

The year 1359 marked a turning point in the history of the province.<ref>{{cite book|language=en|author=Rainer Babedl, Jean-Marie Moeglin|title="Identité régionale et conscience nationale en France et en Allemagne du Moyen Âge à l'époque moderne". actes du colloque organisé par l'université Paris XII – Val-de-Marne, l'Institut universitaire de France et l'Institut historique allemand à l'université Paris XII et à la fondation Singer-Polignac les 6 octobre 1993, 7 octobre 1993 et 8 octobre 1993|place=Sigmaringen|publisher=Thorbecke|date=1997 |edition=first|isbn=978-3-7995-7340-5|url=http://www.thorbecke.de/identite-regionale-et-conscience-nationale-en-france-et-en-allemagne-du-moyen-age-a-lepoque-moderne-p-298.html|access-date=15 March 2016}}.</ref>  The three [[bailiwick]]s ({{lang|fr|sénéchaussées}}) of [[Beaucaire, Gard|Bèucaire]], [[Carcassonne|Carcassona]] and [[Toulouse|Tolosa]] had the status of {{lang|fr|bonnes villes}} (towns granted privileges and protection by the king of France in return for providing a contingent of men at arms). Towards the end of the 14th century, the term "country of the three seneschalties" ({{lang|fr|pays des trois sénéchaussées}}), later to become known as Languedoc, designated the two bailiwicks of Bèucaire-Nimes and [[Carcassonne|Carcassona]], and the eastern part of [[Toulouse|Tolosa]] (Toulouse), retained under the [[Treaty of Brétigny]]. At that time, the [[County of Foix]], which belonged to the seneschal of Carcassona until 1333 before passing to Toulouse, ceased to belong to Languedoc. In 1542, the province was divided into two {{lang|fr|[[généralité]]s}}: Toulouse for Haut-Languedoc, and Montpellier for Bas-Languedoc. This lasted until the French Revolution in 1789. From the 17th century onward, there was only one intendance for the whole of Languedoc, with its seat in Montpellier.

====Haute-Guyenne====
[[File:Blason de l'Aquitaine et de la Guyenne.svg|thumb|upright|Coat of arms of the duchies of Aquitaine and Guyenne, taken again by the province of Guyenne, of gules with a leopard of gold]]

The [[Provinces of France|former provinces]] of [[Gascony]] (in [[Gascon language|Gascon]] {{lang|oc|Gasconha}}, {{IPA-oc|ɡasˈkuɲɔ}}), and [[Guyenne]]; in [[Occitan language|Occitan]] {{lang|oc|Guiana}} {{IPA-oc|ˈɡjanɔ|}}) were historically part of the Great South-West of France ({{lang|fr|Grand Sud-Ouest français}}), and derived from the medieval [[duchy|duchies]] of [[Duchy of Gascony|Vasconia]], [[Duchy of Aquitaine|Aquitaine]] and then [[Guyenne]]. Today, only the eastern regions of the two provinces are part of Occitania. These areas correspond essentially to the territories acquired by the kings of [[England]], dukes of Guyenne, under the [[treaty of Brétigny]] of 1360, and which then remained under the jurisdiction of the provincial appellate court of Toulouse ([[Parlement of Toulouse]]) after the creation of the Parlement of Bordeaux in 1462. From that time, they were generally grouped under the name of [[Guyenne|Haute-Guyenne]], by opposition with [[Guyenne|Basse-Guyenne]], which was dependent on the Parlement of Bordeaux.

[[File:Blason province fr Gascogne.svg|thumb|left|upright|Coat of arms of the former province of [[Gascony|Gascogne]], quartered azure a lion of silver and gules a sheaf of wheat azure bound gold, was created for [[Louis XIV of France|Louis XIV]]: it refers to the lion of [[County of Armagnac|counts of Armagnac]]]]

The territory of the [[Provinces of France|former province]] of [[Guyenne]] (Guiana) that lies within the region corresponds with [[Quercy]] (the current department of [[Lot (department)|Lot]] and the north of [[Tarn-et-Garonne|Tarn e Garona]]), and with [[Rouergue|Roergue]] ([[Aveyron|Avairon]]). These two counties are thus part of the [[Occitan language|Occitan]] linguistic area, in its [[Languedocien dialect|Languedocien]] dialectal variant. They were possessions of the [[Count of Toulouse|Counts of Toulouse]] from the 9th century, at various stages a minor branch or the main branch. Like the other possessions of the Counts of Toulouse, they were integrated for the first time with the [[Crown lands of France|French royal domain]] in 1271, and then yielded to the kings of England under the 1360 [[Treaty of Brétigny]]. The province of [[Quercy]] was definitely reunited with the Crown in 1472; [[Rouergue|Roergue]], possession of the counts of Armagnac, did not follow until 1607. Both came within the [[Provinces of France|province]] of Guyenne, under military rule from 1561. Together, they became the {{lang|fr|[[généralité]]}} of [[Montauban]] from 1635, which then became the province of Haute-Guyenne in 1779. Today, the name Guyenne no longer has administrative or political currency, although the [[Agen|Haut-Agenais]] used the term {{lang|oc|Pais de Guiana}} in a campaign which evoked its historical identity to promote the [[Lot-et-Garonne]] and part of the valley of [[Dropt|Dròt]]. This reference is now superseded in the promotion of tourism by the designation {{lang|oc|Pais del Dròt}}. The appellations Quercy and Roergue, in contrast, retain a strong identity.

Gascony is the [[Provinces of France|former province]] located on the territory of the current [[Departments of France|departments]] of [[Gers]] ([[Armagnac (province)|Armanhac]] and [[Condom, Gers|Condomois]]), [[Hautes-Pyrénées]] ([[County of Bigorre|Bigorre]]), [[Landes (department)|Lanas]] in the neighboring region, and parts of other departments of the two [[Regions of France|regions]] of [[Aquitaine|Aquitània]] and [[Midi-Pyrénées]] ([[Comminges|Comenge]], [[Nébouzan|Nebosan]] and [[Rivière-Verdun]] mainly in the south and west of [[Haute-Garonne]], and [[Couserans]] in the western part of [[Ariège (department)|Ariège]]). Successively called [[Gallia Aquitania|Aquitania]], [[Novempopulania]], [[Vasconia]] (territory of the [[Vascones]]) and Gascony in the 13th century, the territory was geographically diverse, situated between the [[Atlantic Ocean]], [[Garonne|Garona]] and the [[Pyrenees]]. Claiming a [[Identity (social science)|cultural identity]] based on evolving occupation, from the [[Aquitani]] peoples of [[Proto-Basque]] language to a [[Gascon language|Gascon]] population sharing a [[Occitano-Romance languages|latinized Occitan dialect]], it constitutes the current Gascon [[Sprachbund|linguistic area]]. Between the 15th and the beginning of the 16th centuries, it was progressively integrated into the [[Crown lands of France|Royal French domain]]. [[Comminges|Comenge]] yielded to the kings of France in 1443, extinguishing the local dynasty. [[Armagnac (province)|Armagnac]], [[County of Bigorre|Bigorre]] and [[Nébouzan|Nebosan]] followed, with the [[county of Foix]], in 1607.

From the time of the [[French Revolution]], the county was fully incorporated into the department of [[Ariège (department)|Ariège]]. The Occitan dialect traditionally spoken there is [[Languedocien dialect|Languedocien]].

====Roussillon====
[[File:Blason département fr Pyrénées-Orientales.svg|thumb|right|upright|Roussillon's coat of arms refers to the [[coat of arms of the Crown of Aragon]]]]
The [[Provinces of France|Ancien Régime province]] of [[Roussillon]], which had formerly been integrated with the [[Catalan counties]], the [[Kingdom of Majorca]], and the [[Principality of Catalonia]] within the [[Crown of Aragon]], was attached to the Crown of France under the [[Treaty of the Pyrenees]], signed on 7 November 1659. Prior to this treaty, the border between the kingdom of France and the Principality of Catalonia lay further north, along a line of [[citadel]]s ([[Treaty of Corbeil (1258)|Treaty of Corbeil]]).

These territories corresponded to the [[Catalan counties]] of [[County of Roussillon|Roussillon]] and [[County of Conflent|Conflent]], founded in the 9th century, as well as to the northern part of the [[County of Cerdanya]], to which was added the former Vicounty of Castelnou, or [[Vallespir]] (the ''pagus'' of [[County of Besalú]], united with the [[County of Roussillon]] in 1209). The new Province of Roussillon<ref>''Roussillon, Province of (France; 1659- 1790)'' {{BNF|153258873}}.</ref> also known simply as Roussillon,<ref>''Roussillon (Pyrénées-Orientales)'' {{BNF|11940604q}}.</ref> brought together the medieval administrative courts, or [[viguerie]]s, of Roussillon,<ref>''France. Viguerie du Roussillon'' {{BNF | 12494240r}}.</ref> [[County of Conflent|Conflent]],<ref>''France. Viguerie de Conflent et Capcir'' {{BNF|12494275d}}.</ref> and the north of the [[County of Cerdanya]]<ref>''France. Viguerie de Cerdagne'' {{BNF|12494237v}}.</ref> which were part of the government structure of the [[Crown of Aragon]]'s (and attached to the [[Principality of Catalonia]]) counties of Roussillon and Cerdanya (''governació dels comtats de Rosselló i Cerdanya'' in [[Catalan language|Catalan]])<ref>{{cite web  | title = Rosselló i Cerdanya County|language=ca  |url= http://www.enciclopedia.cat/EC-GEC-0056963.xml  | publisher =  [[Gran Enciclopèdia Catalana]] online  }}.</ref> at the time of their attachment to France. Roussillon was subject to direct taxation as a ''pays d'imposition'' (taxing country) and did not have representation through [[the Estates]]<ref>{{ cite book  | language = fr  |author = Vincent Adoumié  | title = Les régions françaises  |place= Paris  | publisher = Hachette supérieur  | date = 23 January 2013|edition=2nd revised and expanded  | isbn = 978-2-01-140018-5  |url = http://www.enseignants.hachette-education.com/post-bac_Classespreparatoires_Classespreparatoireslitteraires_Geographie/pages/catalogue/fiche-livre/les-regions-francaises-1401652.html }}</ref> (a provincial assembly, the provincial assembly of Roussillon,<ref>''France. Assemblée provinciale du Roussillon''{{BNF | 124999479}}.</ref> was created on 15 August 1787).<ref>{{ cite book  | language = en | title = Règlement fait par le Roi [en Conseil] sur la formation et la composition des assemblées qui auront lieu dans la province de Roussillon (Versailles, 15 août 1787)  | place = Paris  | publisher = Imprimerie royale  | date = 1787 }}.</ref> It formed both a government<ref>''France. Gouvernement de Roussillon'' {{BNF|11685874z}}.</ref> and an intendance<ref name = "Bély (2015)">{{ cite book  | language = en  | author = Lucien Bély | title = Dictionnaire Louis XIV  | place = Paris  |publisher = Éditions Robert Laffont  | date = 10 September 2015|edition=first   | isbn= 978-2-221-12482-6 | url = http://www.bouquins.tm.fr/site/dictionnaire_louis_xiv_&100&9782221124826.html }}.</ref> and reported to the [[Secretary of State for War (France)|Secretary of State for War]] as a border province. It had sovereign jurisdiction: the Sovereign Council of Roussillon, independent of the [[Parlement of Toulouse]].

Currently, the name [[Roussillon]] is still the most widely used to designate this territory, being found in the denomination of the former region of [[Languedoc-Roussillon]].

Today, the territory is often subdivided into five unofficial traditional and natural [[Comarca|comarque]]s: [[Rosselló (comarca)|Roussillon]] proper, [[Vallespir]], [[Conflent]], Upper [[Cerdanya]] and [[Capcir]]. A recent addition is [[Fenouillèdes]], the Occitan part of the department of [[Pyrénées-Orientales]].
