The '''Region of Murcia''' ({{IPAc-en|ˈ|m|ʊər|s|i|ə}}, {{IPAc-en|USalso|ˈ|m|ɜːr|ʃ|(|i|)|ə}};<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.collinsdictionary.com/dictionary/english/murcia|title=Murcia|work=[[Collins English Dictionary]]|publisher=[[HarperCollins]]|access-date=29 May 2019}}</ref><ref>[https://web.archive.org/web/20160928091649/https://en.oxforddictionaries.com/definition/us/murcia "Murcia"] (US) and {{Cite dictionary |url=http://www.lexico.com/definition/Murcia |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20200322182214/https://www.lexico.com/definition/murcia |url-status=dead |archive-date=2020-03-22 |title=Murcia |dictionary=[[Lexico]] UK English Dictionary |publisher=[[Oxford University Press]]}}</ref><ref>{{Cite Merriam-Webster|Murcia|access-date=29 May 2019}}</ref> {{langx|es|Región de Murcia}} {{IPA|es-ES|reˈxjon de ˈmuɾθja|}}; {{langx|ca-valencia|Regió de Múrcia}}) is an [[Autonomous communities of Spain|autonomous community]] of Spain located in the southeastern part of the [[Iberian Peninsula]], on the [[Mediterranean Sea|Mediterranean]] coast. The region is {{convert|11,313|km2|sqmi|abbr=on}} in area and had a population of 1,511,251 as at the start of 2020.<ref name="ine.es">{{cite web| url = http://www.laverdad.es/murcia/v/20111217/region/padron-registra-cuarta-mayor-20111217.html| title = El padrón registra la cuarta mayor subida, con 8.000 habitantes más| access-date = December 17, 2011| author = La Verdad de Murcia| date = 17 December 2011}}</ref> About a third of its population lives in the capital, [[Murcia]], and a seventh in the second city, Cartagena. At {{convert|2,014|m|ft|abbr=on}}, the region's highest point is Los Obispos Peak in the {{ill|Massif of Revolcadores|es|Macizo de Revolcadores}}.<ref name=vertice>[ftp://ftp.geodesia.ign.es/Red_Geodesica/Hoja0909/090974.pdf Review Geodesic Vertex, Government of Spain (pdf)]</ref>

A jurisdiction of the [[Crown of Castile]] since the Middle Ages, the [[Kingdom of Murcia]] was replaced in the 19th century by territory primarily belonging to the [[Provinces of Spain|provinces]] of [[province of Albacete|Albacete]] and Murcia (and subsidiarily to those of Jaén and Alicante).<ref>{{Cite journal|url=https://digitum.um.es/digitum/bitstream/10201/7160/1/Los%20origenes%20de%20la%20delimitacion%20territorial%20de%20la%20Comunidad%20de%20Murcia.pdf|page=305|journal=Anales de Historia Contemporánea|volume=14|year=1998|first=María José|last=Vilar|title=Los orígenes de la delimitación territorial de la Comunidad de Murcia}}</ref> The former two were henceforth attached to a 'historical region' also named after Murcia. The province of Murcia constituted as the full-fledged single-province autonomous community of the Region of Murcia in 1982.

The region is bordered by [[Andalusia]] (the provinces of [[Almería (province)|Almería]] and [[province of Granada|Granada]]), [[Castilla-La Mancha|Castile La Mancha]] (the province of Albacete), the [[Valencian Community]] (province of [[province of Alicante|Alicante]]), and the Mediterranean Sea. The autonomous community is a single [[provinces of Spain|province]]. The city of [[Murcia]] is the capital of the region and the seat of the regional government, but the legislature, known as the [[Regional Assembly of Murcia]], is located in [[Cartagena, Spain|Cartagena]]. The region is subdivided into [[List of municipalities in Murcia|municipalities]].<ref>{{cite book|publisher = Editorial Planeta|title = New Larousse Encyclopedia |year = 1981|location = Barcelona| isbn = 84-320-4274-9|page = 6806 |volume = XIV}}</ref>

The region is among Europe's largest producers of fruits, vegetables, and flowers, with important vineyards in the municipalities of [[Jumilla]], [[Bullas]], and [[Yecla]] that produce wines of {{Lang|es|Denominación de origen}}. It also has an important tourism sector concentrated on its Mediterranean coastline, which features the [[Mar Menor]] saltwater lagoon. Industries include the petrochemical and energy sector (centered in Cartagena) and food production. Because of Murcia's warm climate, the region's long growing season is suitable for agriculture; however, rainfall is low. As a result, in addition to the water needed for crops, there are increasing pressures related to the booming tourist industry. Water is supplied by the [[Segura River]] and, since the 1970s, by the [[Tagus-Segura Water Transfer]], a major civil-engineering project that brings water from the [[Tajo|Tagus River]] into the Segura under environmental and sustainability restraints.

Notable features of the region's extensive cultural heritage include 72 [[cave art]] ensembles, which are part of the [[rock art of the Iberian Mediterranean Basin]], a [[World Heritage Site]].<ref>727 individual codes according to the [https://whc.unesco.org/es/list/874/multiple=1&unique_number=1026 list of UNESCO]</ref> Other culturally significant features include the [[Council of Wise Men of the plain of Murcia]] and the {{Lang|es|tamboradas}} (drumming processions) of [[Moratalla, Murcia|Moratalla]] and [[Mula, Spain|Mula]], which were declared [[intangible cultural heritage]] by [[UNESCO]].<ref>Las Provincias. [http://www.lasprovincias.es/valencia/20090930/local/valenciana/tribunal-aguas-valencia-designado-200909301522.html El Tribunal de las Aguas de Valencia es designado Patrimonio Cultural Inmaterial de la Humanidad]. 30 September 2009.</ref><ref>{{cite web|website=Region of Murcia official website|url=http://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=c,369&r=NoP-39106-DETALLE_NOTICIA|title=La tamborada de Mula y Moratalla, Patrimonio Inmaterial de la Humanidad por la Unesco|date=29 November 2018}}</ref> The region is also the home of [[Caravaca de la Cruz]], a holy city in the Catholic Church that celebrates the Perpetual Jubilee every seven years in the [[Basilica of Vera Cruz|Santuario de la Vera Cruz]].<ref>{{cite web

==Toponymy and denomination==
The [[Toponymy|toponym]] (place name) ''Murcia'' is of uncertain origin. According to [[Francisco Cascales]], it could refer to the Roman goddess [[Murcia (deity)|Venus Murcia]], from the [[Myrtus communis|myrtles]] on the banks of the Segura River. Historical studies conclude that, like the deity, Murcia is of Latin origin deriving most likely from {{Lang|la|Myrtea}} or {{Lang|la|Murtea}} ('place of myrtles' or 'place where myrtles grow'). Furthermore, {{Lang|ar-la|Mursiya}} (already documented in the Islamic period as the name of the city of Murcia), was the adaptation in the Arabic of the pre-existing Latin.<ref>{{cite book |author1=Elena Conde Guerri|author2=Rafael González Fernández|author3=Alejandro Egea Vivancos|title=Espacio y tiempo en la percepción de la antigüedad tardía |year=2006 |isbn= 978-84-8371-667-0 |location=Murcia |page=135 |url= https://books.google.com/books?id=nKz6Aov9wYQC&pg=PA135}}</ref> According to Bienvenido Mascaray, it is also possible that the name originates from the [[Iberian language]] in the form {{Lang|xib|m-ur-zia}}, meaning 'the water that empowers or moistens.'<ref>{{cite web|last1= Mascaray |first1= Bienvenido |title= Toponimia Ibérica – Murcia |url= http://iberiasegunmascaray.es/murcia |work=Ibéria según Mascaray |access-date=26 December 2015}}</ref>

[[File:murcia 1795.jpg|thumb|Map of the [[Kingdom of Murcia (Crown of Castile)|Kingdom of Murcia]] in 1795]]

The use of "Murcia" to define the present region has its origin in the [[Taifa of Murcia]], an Arab kingdom that existed at different times in the 11th, 12th, and 13th centuries.<ref>{{cite book|url=https://dialnet.unirioja.es/servlet/libro?codigo=4490|title=Miradas a la historia|author=Miguel Rodríguez Llopis|year=2004|page=59|publisher=University of Murcia|isbn=9788483714478}}</ref> After the Christian [[Conquest of Murcia (1265–66)|conquest of Murcia]]  between 1243 and 1266, the Kingdom of Murcia emerged, a territorial jurisdiction that formed its own institutions until its demise in 1833.<ref>{{cite book |url=https://www.alianzaeditorial.es/libro.php?id=39376&id_col=100508&id_subcol=100513 |title=La Monarquía de España|author=Miguel Artola Gallego|year=1999|isbn=978-84-206-8195-5|edition=First|page=145|publisher=Alianza Editorial }}</ref>

After the provincial administrative reform of 1833, the first [[Region of Murcia (1833-1978)|Region of Murcia]] was formed from the provinces of Albacete and Murcia. In the first attempt at decentralization, during the [[First Spanish Republic|First Republic]], this region was one of the 17 member states that were contemplated by the [[Spanish Draft Constitution of 1873]],<ref>{{cite book|url=https://www.sigloxxieditores.com/libro/la-revolucion-de-1868-y-la-i-republica_16894/|title=La Revolución de 1868 y la I República|author=María Victoria López-Córdon|year=1976|isbn=978-84-323-0238-1|page=59|publisher=Siglo Veintiuno Editores }}</ref> proclaiming during that year the so-called [[Cantón de Cartagena|Cantón Murciano]], as an attempt to form a regional [[Canton (country subdivision)|canton]] in the context of the [[Cantonal rebellion]].<ref>{{cite book|author1=Juan Bautista Vilar Ramírez |title=El sexenio democrático y el cantón murciano|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=70VJAQAAIAAJ |publisher=Academia Alfonso X el Sabio |year=1983 |isbn=9788400054021}}</ref>

In 1978, the [[Regional Council of Murcia]] was created as a pre-autonomous body, in effect until 1982, when the [[Statute of Autonomy of the Region of Murcia]] was approved. The province of Murcia was then granted autonomy under the official name of the Autonomous Community of the Region of Murcia in the framework of the political process in place during the Spanish transition to democracy.

==Symbols==
[[File:"Granata et Murcia regna" (22271038571) (cropped).jpg|thumb|right|Map of the Kingdom of Murcia in ''La Geographia Blaviana'' by [[Joan Blaeu]] (1659). In the upper left quadrant appears the [[coat of arms]] of the kingdom, which was included in the [[Flag of the Region of Murcia|flag]] and [[Coat of arms of the Region of Murcia|coat of arms]] of the Region of Murcia.]]

The flag of the Region of Murcia is rectangular and contains four castle battlements in gold in the upper hoist [[canton (flag)|canton]], distributed two over two (symbolizing the borders of the ancient Kingdom of Murcia and the four [[border]]s that it had at some point in its history), and seven royal [[Crown (heraldry)|crown]]s in the lower fly canton (these being the [[Escutcheon (heraldry)|escutcheon]] of the historical coat of arms of the Kingdom of Murcia), arranged in four rows, with one, three, two and one elements, respectively; all on the [[crimson]] background of Cartagena.<ref>{{cite web |url= https://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=c,373,m,1915&r=ReP-26784-DETALLE_REPORTAJESPADRE|title= La bandera y el escudo de la Región de Murcia. Castillos y coronas|publisher= Region of Murcia website}}</ref>

The flag's origin dates back to the [[Spanish transition to democracy|Spanish transition]], when the president of the [[Regional Council of Murcia]], [[Antonio Pérez Crespo]], established a commission in 1978 to study the future flag of the Region of Murcia. The commission was formed by historians [[Juan Torres Fontes]] and [[José María Jover]] and senators [[Ricardo de la Cierva]] and Antonio López Pina. The project was approved on 26 March 1979 and the flag was first hoisted on 5 May 1979 on a balcony of the Regional Council building, the former [[Provincial Council of Murcia]] (now the Ministry of Finance).<ref>{{cite web |url= https://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=c,373,m,1915&r=ReP-26783-DETALLE_REPORTAJES|title= La bandera y el escudo de la Región de Murcia. La bandera|publisher= Region of Murcia website}}</ref>

The same committee established that the [[coat of arms of the Region of Murcia]] had the same symbols and distribution as the flag, with the royal crown as a crest above. Flag and shield were specified by Article 4 of the [[Statute of Autonomy of the Region of Murcia]], approved by organic law in 1982.

The {{ill|Day of the Region of Murcia|es|Día de la Región de Murcia}} is celebrated on 9 June, commemorating the promulgation of the Statute of Autonomy.

==Geography==

===Location===
[[File:Murcia Sat.jpg|thumb|right|Satellite view of the Region of Murcia.]]

The Region of Murcia is located in the southeast of the [[Iberian Peninsula]], on the coast of the [[Mediterranean Sea]]. It ranges from 38º 45' in the north to 37º 23' in the south, and from 0º 41' in the east to 2º 21' in the west. With an area of {{convert|11313|km2}}, it is the ninth-largest region of Spain by area and constitutes 2.9% of the national area. It extends over the greater part of the hydrographic basin of the [[Segura River]], thus constituting a well-defined geographical unit, except for the ''[[comarca]]s'' of the [[Mancomunidad de Municipios de la Sierra del Segura|Sierra de Segura]] and the [[Campos de Hellín]] which were in the province of Albacete, Los Vélez in Almería and [[El Baix Segura / La Vega Baja|La Vega Baja]] in the province of Alicante, all belonging to the same basin.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.carm.es/econet/sicrem/PU_datosBasicos/sec1.html |title=Posición geográfica de Murcia. Centro Regional de Estadística de Murcia|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160625002416/http://www.carm.es/econet/sicrem/PU_datosBasicos/sec1.html|archive-date=25 June 2016}}</ref>

===Terrain===
[[File:Macizo de Revolcadores, Moratalla, Murcia, España, cara sur, desde poco antes de Cañada de la Cruz.jpg|thumb|The [[Massif of Revolcadores]] is the highest point of the Region of Murcia, its highest peak is Los Obispos Peak at {{convert|2014|m}}.]]

Approximately 27% of the Murcian territory consists of mountainous reliefs, 38% intermountain depressions and corridor valleys, and the remaining 35% of plains and high plateaus. The region is located at the eastern end of the [[Baetic System]], being affected climatologically by an orography that isolates it from the Atlantic influence. These mountain ranges are divided in turn from north to south into:<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=c,365,m,1042&r=ReP-3229-DETALLE_REPORTAJESPADRE|title=Relieve de la Región de Murcia – Región de Murcia Digital|language=es|access-date=2019-12-09}}</ref>

* the [[Prebaetic System|Cordillera Prebética]]: the northernmost, where the Sierra del Carche stands out from the others.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=c,365,m,1042&r=ReP-3230-DETALLE_REPORTAJESPADRE|title=Relieve en la Región de Murcia- Prebético – Región de Murcia Digital|language=es|access-date=2019-12-08}}</ref>
* the [[Subbaetic System|Cordillera Subbética]]: it consists of numerous dipping faults superimposed on each other or on the materials of the Prebaetic. The Massif of Revolcadores, the highest in the region at {{convert|2015|m}}, belongs to this system.
* the [[Penibaetic System|Cordillera Penibética]]: with three distinct lithological complexes from north to south  (Nevado-Filabride, Alpujárride and Maláguide). They are very fractured, although there is a predominance of dipping faults and inverse faults between these complexes. [[Sierra Espuña]] is one of the fundamental penibaetic mountains.

Among the high plateaus are the Campo de San Juan and the Altiplano murciano.<ref name=ATLAS/>

Some of the valleys and plains are the [[Coastal plain|coastal depression]] of the [[Campo de Cartagena]]-Mar Menor; a little farther inland is the Valle del Guadalentín (also called the ''Murcian pre-coastal depression''), which crosses the region from southwest to northeast.<ref name=ATLAS>{{cite book |author=several authors |title=Atlas Global de la Región de Murcia |url=http://www.atlasdemurcia.com/ |year=2007 |publisher=La Verdad Grupo Multimedia, Comunidad Autónoma de la Región de Murcia, Cajamurcia |location=Murcia |chapter=El medio natural: El relieve |chapter-url=http://www.atlasdemurcia.com/index.php/secciones/2/el-relieve/4/}}</ref> The fertile plains lie along the Segura River (among the most famous ones the so-called Valle de Ricote), and tributaries of the Segura, such as the Mula basin.

To explain this complex relief, it is important to highlight the existence of significant [[Fault (geology)|faults]] throughout the area—such as Alhama de Murcia, [[Bullas]]-[[Archena]], or the Cicatriz Nor-Bética—which, along with intersections with other minor faults, generate numerous earth movements, such as the [[2011 Lorca earthquake]].

The most widely present soil types are the calcaric fluvisol, the calcaric regosol, and the calcic xerosol.<ref>{{Cite book|title=Atlas Global de la Región de Murcia|page=187}}</ref> [[Regosol]] soils form about a quarter of the region's surface;<ref>{{Cite book|title=Atlas Global de la Región de Murcia|page=181}}</ref> and calcic horizons (B [[Horizon (geology)|horizons]] [third layers of the soil] being formed by calcium carbonate deposits and 15 cm thick at least, and containing a minimum 15% of CaCO<sub>3</sub> besides more features) occur in almost half of the surface.<ref>{{Cite book|title=Atlas Global de la Región de Murcia|page=183}}</ref>

===Climate===
Murcia predominantly has a [[hot semi-arid climate]] ([[Köppen climate classification]]: ''BSh'') and a [[Semi-arid climate|cold semi-arid climate]] ([[Köppen climate classification|Köppen]]: ''BSk''). However, parts of the southern coast of Murcia have a [[Desert climate|hot desert climate]] ([[Köppen climate classification|Köppen]]: ''BWh''). In addition, there are small areas in the interior that have a [[Desert climate|cold desert climate]] (''BWk''), a [[Mediterranean climate|hot summer Mediterranean climate]] (Csa) and, to a lesser extent, a [[Mediterranean climate|warm summer Mediterranean climate]] (''Csb'').<ref>{{Cite web |title=Evolucion de los climas de Koppen en España en el periodo 1951-2020 |url=https://www.aemet.es/documentos/es/conocermas/recursos_en_linea/publicaciones_y_estudios/publicaciones/NT_37_AEMET/NT_37_AEMET.pdf |access-date=2024-05-06 |website=[[Agencia Estatal de Meteorologia]]}}</ref>

Winters are mild (an average of {{convert|11|°C}} in December and January) and summers are hot to very hot (where the daily maximum regularly exceeds {{convert|40|°C}}). The average annual temperature varies from {{convert|10|°C}} to more than {{convert|19|°C}}.<ref>{{Cite web |last=Meteorología |first=Agencia Estatal de |title=Valores climatológicos normales - Agencia Estatal de Meteorología - AEMET. Gobierno de España |url=https://www.aemet.es/es/serviciosclimaticos/datosclimatologicos/valoresclimatologicos |access-date=2024-05-06 |website=www.aemet.es |language=es}}</ref>

Precipitation varies from more than {{convert|550|mm|in}} to less than {{convert|200|mm|in}} per year, making this autonomous community the driest in [[Spain]].<ref>{{Cite web |last=Meteorología |first=Agencia Estatal de |title=Valores climatológicos normales - Agencia Estatal de Meteorología - AEMET. Gobierno de España |url=https://www.aemet.es/es/serviciosclimaticos/datosclimatologicos/valoresclimatologicos |access-date=2024-05-06 |website=www.aemet.es |language=es}}</ref> The region has between 120 and 150 days in the year where the sky is totally clear. April and October have the most precipitation, with frequent heavy downpours in a single day.

The distance to the sea and the relief causes a thermal difference between the coast and the interior, especially in winter, when the temperature rarely dips below {{convert|10|°C}} on the coast, while in the interior regions the minimum usually does not rise above {{convert|6|°C}} and the precipitation level is higher (up to {{convert|600|mm}}).

The city of Murcia holds the Spanish record high temperature in the 20th century. It reached {{convert|46.1|°C|°F}} on July 4, 1994. The winter of 2005 was the coldest in a long time, with snow falling even on the Murcian coast.<ref>{{cite web|date=2005-12-23|title=La Verdad Digital – Murcia|url=http://www1.laverdad.es/interactivo/imagenes_galerias/nevadacostaene05/imagenes.htm|access-date=2021-08-02|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20051223050436/http://www1.laverdad.es/interactivo/imagenes_galerias/nevadacostaene05/imagenes.htm|archive-date=2005-12-23}}</ref>

* the [[Alhárabe]] River and its tributary the Benamor.<ref>{{cite web|last=|first=|date=|title=Visitando: el río Alhárabe – Región de Murcia Digital|url=https://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=c,365,m,2705&r=ReP-21324-DETALLE_REPORTAJESPADRE|access-date=2021-02-12|website=|language=Spanish}}</ref>
* the [[Mula (Spain)|Mula]] River.<ref>{{cite web|title=Lugar de Importancia Comunitaria "Río Mula y Pliego" (ES6200045)|url=http://www.murcianatural.carm.es/c/document_library/get_file?uuid=b063bdd7-51c0-413e-b85d-bbc5b5d9bbfa&groupId=14|access-date=2020-08-13|language=es}}</ref>
* the [[Guadalentín]], Sangonera or Reguerón  (which originates above [[Lorca, Spain|Lorca]]).<ref>{{cite web|title=Ayuntamiento de Alhama de Murcia – Río Guadalentín|url=https://ayuntamiento.alhamademurcia.es/rio-guadalentin.asp|access-date=2020-08-13|language=es}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|title=Guadalentín: El río más salvaje de Europa I La Verdad|date=31 October 2010|url=https://www.laverdad.es/murcia/v/20101031/lorca/guadalentin-salvaje-europa-20101031.html|access-date=2020-08-13|language=es}}</ref>

Due to the Segura river basin's insufficient water capacity, contributions to this river basin are made from the basin of the [[Tajo]] River by means of the Tajo–Segura Water Transfer.<ref>{{cite web|last=|first=|date=|title=Trasvase Tajo-Segura|url=https://www.miteco.gob.es/es/agua/temas/concesiones-y-autorizaciones/Trasvase-Tajo-Segura/|access-date=2021-02-12|website=Ministerio para la Transición Ecológica y el Reto Demográfico|language=Spanish}}</ref>

====Seas====
[[Image:Cartagena, Mar Menor, i Cap de Pals (foto satèl·lit).jpg|thumb|upright|Satellite view of the Mar Menor|200px]]

The greatest natural lake of [[Spain]] can be found in the region: the ''[[Mar Menor]]'' (Small Sea) lagoon. It is a [[seawater|salt water]] lagoon, adjacent to the [[Mediterranean Sea]]. Its special ecological and natural characteristics make the Mar Menor a unique place and the largest saltwater lake in Europe. With a semicircular shape, it is separated from the Mediterranean Sea by a sand strip {{convert|22|km}} in length and between {{convert|100 and 1200|m}} wide, which is known as ''[[La Manga del Mar Menor]]'' (the Minor Sea's Sandbar).<ref>{{cite web|title=Mar Menor – Región de Murcia Digital|url=https://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=c,365,m,2584|access-date=2020-08-14|language=es}}</ref> The lagoon has been designated by the United Nations as a Specially Protected Zone of Importance for the Mediterranean. Its coastal perimeter accounts for {{convert|73|km}} of coast, along which beaches follow one another beside crystal clear shallow water (the maximum depth does not exceed {{convert|7|m}}). The lake has an area of {{convert|170|sqkm}}.<ref>{{cite web|last=Murcia|first=Instituto de Turismo de la Región de|title=Mar Menor : Official Murcia Region tourist site|url=https://www.murciaturistica.es/en/mar_menor/|access-date=2020-08-14|website=murciaturistica.es|language=en}}</ref>

===Flora and fauna===

====Flora====
There are more than 30 tree species, over 50 species of shrubs, and more than 130 herbaceous plant species in the region. Some species have been introduced but are now part of the landscape.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=c,365,m,1050&r=ReP-26085-DETALLE_REPORTAJESPADRE|title=Árboles – Región de Murcia Digital|language=es|access-date=2019-12-08}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=c,365,m,1050&r=ReP-26087-DETALLE_REPORTAJESPADRE|title=Arbustos – Región de Murcia Digital|language=es|access-date=2019-12-08}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=c,365,m,1050&r=ReP-26092-DETALLE_REPORTAJESPADRE|title=Hierbas y Matas – Región de Murcia Digital|language=es|access-date=2019-12-08}}</ref>

Indigenous tree species in the region are [[Pinus halepensis|Aleppo pines]], [[Rhamnus alaternus|Mediterranean buckthorns]], [[Tamarix|tamarisk trees]], and [[Ulmus minor|field elms]]. There are also some species that have been introduced, such as the [[Cupressus sempervirens|Mediterranean cypress]].

Native shrubs found in several parts of the region are [[Stipa tenacissima|esparto grass]], a species of the genus [[Chamaerops|European fan palm]], ''Salsola genistoides'' (close to the opposite-leaved saltworts), [[rosemary]], [[Pistacia lentiscus|lentisks]], [[Rhamnus lycioides|black hawthorns]], [[Posidonia oceanica|Neptune grass]], [[Thymelaea hirsuta|shaggy sparrow-wort]], and ''Retama sphareocarpa''. There are also species which have been introduced, such as the [[Nicotiana glauca|tree tobacco]] and ''Opuntia maxima''.

In regards to herbaceous plants, some native species are [[Sonchus tenerrimus|slender sowthistles]], [[Reichardia tingitana|false sowthistles]], [[Convolvulus althaeoides|mallow bindweeds]], [[Hordeum murinum|wall barleys]], [[fennel]]s, ''Brachypodium retusum'' (close to false-bromes), ''Thymus hyemalis'' (close to broad-leaved thymes), ''Asphodelus ayardii'' (of the same genus as onionweeds). Non-native species include the [[Oxalis pes-caprae|African wood-sorrel]] and the [[Erigeron bonariensis|flax-leaf fleabane]].

====Fauna====
In the region, there are over 10 species of land mammals (not counting bats), 19 bat species, over 80 bird species, 11 species of amphibians, 21 reptile species, and 9 species of fish.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=c,365,m,1036&r=ReP-7477-DETALLE_REPORTAJESPADRE|title=Mamíferos – Región de Murcia Digital|language=es|access-date=2019-12-08}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=c,365,m,1036&r=ReP-8049-DETALLE_REPORTAJESPADRE|title=Aves – Región de Murcia Digital|language=es|access-date=2019-12-08}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=c,365,m,1036&r=ReP-16774-DETALLE_REPORTAJESPADRE|title=Anfibios – Región de Murcia Digital|language=es|access-date=2019-12-08}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=c,365,m,1036&r=ReP-16773-DETALLE_REPORTAJESPADRE|title=Reptiles – Región de Murcia Digital|language=es|access-date=2019-12-08}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=c,365,m,1036&r=ReP-8046-DETALLE_REPORTAJESPADRE|title=Peces – Región de Murcia Digital|language=es|access-date=2019-12-08}}</ref>

Mammals inhabiting the area include [[barbary sheep]], [[European badger]]s, [[beech marten]]s, [[Eurasian otter]]s, [[red fox]]es, [[wild boar]]s, [[red squirrel]]s, [[European wildcat]]s, [[garden dormouse|garden dormice]], and [[Cabrera's vole|Cabreras vole]] (of the same genus as field voles). In addition, some species of bats are the [[common pipistrelle]], [[Kuhl's pipistrelle|Kukhl's pipistrelle]], the [[common bent-wing bat]], the [[soprano pipistrelle]], the [[greater horseshoe bat]], the [[meridional serotine]] (which only inhabits southern Spain, Morocco, Algeria and Tunisia), the [[lesser horseshoe bat]], and the European [[free-tailed bat]].

In regard to birds, there are some raptor species, such as [[Bonelli's eagle]]s, [[golden eagle]]s, [[peregrine falcon]]s, [[little owl]]s, and [[Eurasian eagle-owl]]s. There are also waterbirds, such as [[yellow-legged gull]]s, [[mallard]]s, [[Himantopus himantopus|black-winged stilts]], [[little grebe]]s, and [[garganey]]s. Other bird species are the [[house sparrow]], [[European greenfinch]], [[European robin]]s, [[common blackbird]]s, and [[European turtle dove]]s.

Some amphibians found in the Region of Murcia are [[Perez's frog]], [[common parsley frog]], [[Common toad|European toads]], and [[Natterjack toad]]s.

Reptile species in the region are [[Malpolon monspessulanus|Montpellier snakes]], [[ladder snake]]s, [[horseshoe whip snake]]s, [[Natrix maura|viperine water snakes]], [[Iberian worm lizard]]s, [[Spanish pond turtle]]s, [[Podarcis hispanicus|Iberian wall lizards]], [[Psammodromus hispanicus|Spanish psammodromus]], ''[[Tarentola mauritanica]]'', [[loggerhead sea turtle]]s, and [[Greek tortoise]]s.

Fish species in the region include the [[Atlantic horse mackerel]], [[Spanish toothcarp]], [[gilt-head bream]], [[greater amberjack]], [[sand steenbras]], and [[flathead grey mullet]].

==History==

===Prehistory and Ancient Era===
Since the [[Lower Paleolithic]] era, the Region of Murcia has been inhabited by humans.<ref>{{cite web|title=Paleolítico Inferior – Región de Murcia Digital|url=https://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=c,373,m,1915&r=ReP-24614-DETALLE_REPORTAJESABUELO|last=Gómez-Guillamón Buendía|first=Antonio|language=es|access-date=2020-05-04}}</ref> In the Torre-Pacheco municipality in the southeast of the region is a noteworthy paleontological site, the [[Sima de las Palomas]], which contains bone remains of [[Neanderthal]]s from the [[Middle Paleolithic]] era.<ref>{{cite web|title=Paleolítico Medio – Región de Murcia Digital|url=https://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=c,373,m,1915&r=ReP-24620-DETALLE_REPORTAJESABUELO|last=Gómez-Guillamón Buendía|first=Antonio|language=es|access-date=2020-05-04}}</ref>

The [[Argaric culture]] flourished in the region from the [[Chalcolithic]] era until the early [[Bronze Age]]. La Bastida is a site in the Totana municipality, in the southwestern quarter of the region, that references the civilization.<ref>{{cite web|title=Turismo Totana – La Bastida|url=http://turismo.totana.es/la_bastida/|language=es|access-date=2020-05-05}}</ref> Later, the Iberians were present in this territory during the Middle and Late Bronze Age and remained until very early in ancient history, before the Romans conquered a large part of the Iberian Peninsula.<ref>{{cite web|title=Los íberos – Región de Murcia Digital|url=https://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=c,373,m,1915&r=ReP-24646-DETALLE_REPORTAJESABUELO|last=Gómez-Guillamón Buendía|first=Antonio|language=es|access-date=2020-05-05}}</ref> A shrine, necropolis, and an ancient settlement for these people can be found at the {{ill|El Cigarralejo|es|Complejo ibérico de El Cigarralejo}} site. Another site that consists of the remains of an Iberian shrine is Santuario Ibérico de la Luz, located in the Murcia municipality.<ref>{{cite web|title=Santuario – Santuario Ibérico de La Luz|url=https://www.um.es/|language=es|access-date=2020-05-05}}</ref>

[[Image:Teatro romano cartagena.jpg|thumb|left|upright=1.14|Roman Theatre, Cartagena]]

In 227 BC, [[Ancient Carthage|Carthaginian]]s settled in what is now [[Cartagena, Spain|Cartagena]] and established a permanent [[trade|trading]] port on its coast that was named Qart-Hadast. For the Carthaginian traders, the mountainous territory was merely the [[Iberian Peninsula|Iberian]] hinterland of their seacoast [[empire]]. In 209 BC, the Romans conquered Qart-Hadast, and the territory belonged to the [[Roman province|province]] of [[Hispania Carthaginensis]].<ref>{{cite web|title=Historia de Cartagena- Antigüedad – Región de Murcia Digital|url=https://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=a,75,c,373,m,1871&r=ReP-7509-DETALLE_REPORTAJESPADRE|language=es|access-date=2020-05-05}}</ref> During the Roman era, Carthago Nova was the most important place in the region, and there are still remains of ancient villas in the Campo de Cartagena.<ref>{{cite web|title=Historia de Torre Pacheco- Antigüedad – Región de Murcia Digital|url=https://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=a,95,c,373,m,1871&r=ReP-4367-DETALLE_REPORTAJESPADRE}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|title=Publicación número 10048 del BORM número 142 de 23/06/2011|url=http://www.borm.es/borm/documento?obj=anu&id=431979|language=es|access-date=2020-05-06}}</ref> The Romans built a salt factory and settled in a little town called Ficaria, in the current municipality of Mazarrón.<ref>{{cite web|title=Historia Antigua de Mazarrón – Región de Murcia Digital|url=https://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=c,373,m,2910&r=ReP-25307-DETALLE_REPORTAJESPADRE|language=es|access-date=2020-05-06}}</ref> [[Comarcas of Region of Murcia#Altiplano|Altiplano]] and Noroeste ''[[comarca]]s'' (a kind of region) both contain surviving dwellings of the Romans.<ref>{{cite web|title=Publicación número 10279 del BORM número 145 de 27/06/2011|url=http://www.borm.es/borm/documento?obj=anu&id=432220|language=es|access-date=2020-05-06}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|title=Publicación número 8631 del BORM número 128 de 05/06/2013|url=http://www.borm.es/borm/documento?obj=anu&id=567115|language=es|access-date=2020-05-06}}</ref>

In the early 5th century, the [[Vandals]], [[Suebi]], and [[Alans]] began to invade the Iberian Peninsula, settling in different provinces. The Vandals acquired Lusitania and Carthaginensis, the Suebi took the Gallaecia, and the Alans settled in Baetica. The Romans wanted to recover their land and requested assistance from the [[Visigoths]], to which they would provide goods and territory in return. With that, the Alans and Vandals were defeated by the Visigoths and fled to North Africa. Consequently, the Visigoths became federated to the Roman Empire in a kingdom that stretched from Gibraltar to the [[Loire|Loire River]]. The Visigothic kingdom became independent of the Roman Empire in 476.<ref>{{cite web|title=La invasión de los pueblos germánicos – Región de Murcia Digital|url=https://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=c,373,m,1915&r=ReP-24774-DETALLE_REPORTAJESABUELO|last=Gómez-Guillamón Buendía|first=Antonio|language=es|access-date=2020-05-07}}</ref>

In 555 AD, the Byzantines, under the emperor [[Justinian the Great]], conquered the southeastern coast of the Iberian Peninsula and established the province of [[Spania]]. Part of the current Region of Murcia belonged to the province and therefore to the Eastern Roman or [[Byzantine Empire]]. The current of [[Campo de Cartagena|Campo Cartagena-Mar Menor]] (Cartagena, La Unión, Fuente Álamo, Torre-Pacheco, Los Alcázares, Mazarrón) and [[Comarcas of Region of Murcia#Alto Guadalentín of Comarca de Lorca|Alto Guadalentín]] (Lorca, Águilas, San Javier and Santiago de la Ribera, and Puerto Lumbreras) also belonged to the province.<ref>{{cite web|title=La provincia de Spania: Carthago Spartaria – Región de Murcia Digital|url=https://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=c,373,m,1915&r=ReP-24797-DETALLE_REPORTAJESABUELO|last=Gómez-Guillamón Buendía|first=Antonio|language=es|access-date=2020-05-07}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|title=Bizancio en Spania – Revista de Historia|date=7 March 2015|url=https://revistadehistoria.es/bizancio-en-spania/|language=es|access-date=2020-05-07}}</ref>

===Moorish Middle Ages===
In the early 8th century there was a disputed succession to the Visigothic throne. The king [[Wittiza]] wanted his son Agila to be his successor, but the nobles of the court elected [[Roderic]], duke of [[Hispania Baetica|Baetica]], as king. The people in favour of Agila conspired to overthrow Roderic. They asked the [[Moors]] for help and promised spoils of war in return.<ref name=":0">{{cite web|title=El Emirato Dependiente: el pacto de Tudmir|url=https://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=c,373,m,1915&r=ReP-24811-DETALLE_REPORTAJESABUELO|last=Gómez-Guillamón Buendía|first=Antonio|language=es|access-date=2020-05-08}}</ref>

The Moors began conquering the Iberian Peninsula in 711. Roderic was killed, and the Visigothic kingdom disappeared. Consequently, the Moors quickly conquered much of the peninsula.<ref name=":0" />

[[Theodemir (Visigoth)|Theodemir]] led a nucleus of resistance in almost all the current region and the south of Alicante province. In 713, he signed the [[Treaty of Orihuela]], because the resistance could no longer endure. The territory came under Muslim rule, but the conquerors granted it political autonomy.<ref name=":0" />

Under the Moors, who introduced the large-scale [[irrigation]] upon which Murcian agriculture relies, the province was known as ''Todmir''. According to [[Muhammad al-Idrisi|Idrisi]], the 12th century Arab cartographer based in [[Sicily]], it included the cities of [[Orihuela]], [[Lorca, Spain|Lorca]], [[Mula city, Spain|Mula]], and [[Chinchilla de Montearagón|Chinchilla]].

[[File:Ibn Hud, Cantigas de Santa Maria 169.jpg|thumb|upright|[[Ibn Hud]] as depicted in the ''[[Cantigas de Santa Maria]]'']]

In the early 11th century, after the fall of the Umayyad [[Caliphate of Córdoba]], a territory centered on the city of Murcia became an independent principality, or ''[[taifa]]''. At one point, the taifa included parts of the present-day provinces of [[Province of Albacete|Albacete]] and [[Province of Almería|Almería]], as well.

After the 1086 [[Battle of Sagrajas]], the [[Almoravid|Almoravid emirate]] swallowed up the ''taifas''. When Almoravid rule ultimately declined, [[Abu ʿAbd Allāh Muḥammad ibn Saʿd ibn Mardanīš]] established a taifa—including the cities of Murcia, Valencia, and Dénia—that opposed for a time the spread of the [[Almohad]]s, but ultimately succumbed to the latter's advance in the 1170s. Conversely, when the Almohads receded after their defeat in the 1212 [[Battle of Las Navas de Tolosa]], another taifa-prince based in Murcia, [[Ibn Hud]], rebelled against Almohad rule and briefly controlled most of [[Al-Andalus]].

===Christian Middle Ages and early modern period===
[[Ferdinand III of Castile]] received the submission of the Moorish king of Murcia under the terms of the 1243 {{ill|Treaty of Alcaraz|es|Tratado de Alcaraz (1243)}} and made the territory a protectorate of the Crown of Castile. There were towns that rejected compliance with the treaty, such as Qartayanna-Al halfa (Cartagena), Lurqa (Lorca) and Mula. There were also towns where governors accepted the treaty but the inhabitants did not, such as Aledo, Ricote, Uruyla (Orihuela), and Medina La-Quant (Alicante), (although the two last do not belong to the present-day Region of Murcia; they were part of the Taifa of Murcia). In 1245, a Castilian army and a fleet from the [[Cantabrian Sea]] conquered Qartayanna. Consequently, the rest of the rebellious towns were also taken by the Castilians.<ref>{{Cite book|title=La Región de Murcia y su historia|year=1989|isbn=84-85-856-60-0|volume=I|page=187|last1=Pérez|first1=Fulgencio Pedreño}}</ref> Following the support of local Muslims for the [[Mudéjar revolt of 1264–1266]], in 1266 [[Alfonso X of Castile]] [[Conquest of Murcia (1265–66)|annexed the territory outright]] with critical military support from his uncle [[Jaime I of Aragon]].<ref>{{cite web|title=Historia de Cartagena- Edad Media Cristiana – Región de Murcia Digital|url=https://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=a,75,c,373,m,1871&r=ReP-7513-DETALLE_REPORTAJESPADRE|language=es|access-date=2020-05-08}}</ref>

The Castilian conquest of Murcia marked the end of the [[Crown of Aragon|Aragon's]] southward expansion along the Iberian Mediterranean coast. The kingdom of Murcia was repopulated with people from Christian territories by giving them land.<ref>{{Cite book|last=González Castaño|first=Juan|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=ShcFjDaPimQC|title=Breve historia de la Región de Murcia|year=2009|isbn=978-84-7564-527-8|page=94|publisher=Ediciones Tres Fronteras }}</ref>

[[James II of Aragon]] broke an agreement between the Castile and Aragon regarding the division of territory between the two kingdoms and, from 1296 to 1302, conquered Alicante, Elche, Orihuela, Murcia, Cartagena, and Lorca. In consequence of those victories, James II and [[Ferdinand IV of Castile]] agreed to the [[Treaty of Torrellas]], which stipulated the return of the conquered territory to Castile, save for the towns of Cartagena, Orihuela, Elche, and Alicante. In 1305, Cartagena was returned to Castile. The kingdom of Murcia lost the territory of the current province of Alicante.<ref>{{cite web|title=Las intervenciones militares de la Corona de Aragón – Región de Murcia Digital|url=http://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=c,373,m,1915&r=ReP-24921-DETALLE_REPORTAJESABUELO|language=es|access-date=2020-05-09}}</ref>

The Castilian monarchs proceeded to delegate power over the whole Kingdom of Murcia (then a borderland of the Crown of Castile, near Granada and Aragon) to a senior officer called the [[Adelantado]]. The kingdom of Murcia was divided into religious manors, nobility manors, and {{Lang|es|señoríos de}} ''[[realengo]]'' (a type of [[manorialism]] in which the noble had the property, but the king had the authority to administer justice). There were two noble lineages during the [[Late Middle Ages]] and the modern period: Los Manueles and Los Fajardos.<ref>{{cite web|title=Las luchas nobiliarias por el poder: Manueles vs Fajardos – Región de Murcia Digital|url=https://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=c,373,m,1915&r=ReP-24938-DETALLE_REPORTAJESABUELO|last=Gómez-Guillamón Buendía|first=Antonio|language=es|access-date=2020-05-10}}</ref>

The Kingdom of Murcia was adjacent to the [[Emirate of Granada]], which provoked several Muslim raids and wars that occurred mainly during the 15th century.<ref>{{Cite book|title=La Región de Murcia y su historia|year=1989|isbn=84-85-856-60-0|volume=II|pages=197, 198|language=es|last1=Pérez|first1=Fulgencio Pedreño}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|title=Territorio de fronteras: las guerras con Granada|url=https://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=c,373,m,1915&r=ReP-24966-DETALLE_REPORTAJESABUELO|language=es|access-date=2020-05-12}}</ref>

[[File:Reino de Murcia loc 1590.svg|thumb|upright|Map of the Kingdom of Murcia in 1590]]

In the early 16th century, the population increased in the Kingdom of Murcia. There were three plague epidemics during the century, but they did not severely affect the region. In the first third of the century, the [[Revolt of the Comuneros]] occurred. Some places that supported the revolt were towns in the present-day Castile and León and Castilla-La Mancha regions. In the Kingdom of Murcia, the revolutionary towns were Murcia, Cartagena, Lorca, Caravaca, Cehegín, and Totana. The castle of [[Aledo, Spain|Aledo]] defended the monarchy. In 1521, the Revolt of the Comuneros was defeated.<ref>{{cite web|title=El emperador Carlos V: la revuelta comunera – Región de Murcia Digital|url=https://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=c,373,m,1915&r=ReP-24997-DETALLE_REPORTAJESABUELO|last=Gómez-Guillamón Buendía|first=Antonio|language=es|access-date=2020-05-14}}</ref>

In the early 17th century, King [[Philip III of Spain]] [[Expulsion of the Moriscos|expelled all the ''Moriscos'']] (descendants of Muslims) from Valencia, Aragon, and Castille. During this century, two [[Bubonic plague|plague]] epidemics also occurred.<ref>{{Cite book|last=González Castaño|first=Juan|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=ShcFjDaPimQC|title=Breve historia de la Región de Murcia|year=2009|isbn=978-84-7564-527-8|pages=184, 185, 189|publisher=Ediciones Tres Fronteras }}</ref>

During the 18th century, [[Francisco Salzillo]] was a notable Baroque artist in the Kingdom of Murcia. He made carvings with religious imagery.<ref>{{cite web|title=Felipe V: el esplendor del Barroco murciano – Región de Murcia Digital|url=https://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=c,373,m,1915&r=ReP-25113-DETALLE_REPORTAJESABUELO|last=Gómez-Guillamón Buendía|first=Antonio|language=es|access-date=2020-05-16}}</ref>

===Napoleonic wars===
In 1807, Napoleon signed the [[Treaty of Fontainebleau (October 1807)|Treaty of Fontainebleau]] with Spain, in order for French armies to cross the peninsula to conquer Portugal. In early 1808, Napoleon betrayed Spain and invaded Pamplona, San Sebastián, Barcelona, Burgos, and Salamanca. In 1808, the people of Madrid rebelled, and all of Spain was summoned to fight the French invaders. The people of the country established for each province political organisations, or ''[[Junta (Peninsular War)|junta]]s'', as alternatives to the official administrations. Since the French were not much present in the Kingdom of Murcia, battles were rare in the region. Nevertheless, Spaniards from the region battled the French in other areas of Spain. In addition, the region became a staging area for the movement of troops, guns, and supplies destined for the eastern Iberian Peninsula, or Andalucía. In 1810, French troops did attack the Kingdom of Murcia. Most local officials escaped. The French, coming from Lorca, invaded the town of Murcia on 23 April, and looted it on the 26th. The troops returned to the town in August, but defensive measures had been taken and the French attack was repelled. The French army occupied Murcia again in January 1812, looting Águilas, Lorca, Caravaca, Cehegín, Jumilla, Yecla, Mula, Alhama de Murcia, and the Ricote Valley. Cartagena withstood a French siege, owing to its rampart and the help of an English fleet. In 1813, the French were decisively defeated in the north at the [[Battle of Vitoria]].<ref>{{cite web|title=La Guerra de Independencia y Floridablanca – Región de Murcia Digital|url=https://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=c,373,m,1915&r=ReP-25170-DETALLE_REPORTAJES|language=es|access-date=2020-05-19}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|title=Murcia en los dos primeros años de la guerra por la independencia|url=https://digitum.um.es/digitum/bitstream/10201/6373/1/N%205%20%20Murcia%20en%20los%20dos%20primeros%20anos%20de%20la%20guerra%20por%20la%20independencia.pdf|language=es|access-date=2020-05-19}}</ref>

===20th century===
In 1936, under the [[Second Spanish Republic]], there was an uprising. The North African territories of Spain were taken on 17 July. The uprising was successful in [[Spanish Civil War#Beginning of the coup|some areas]] of Spain. The partial success of the uprising brought on the [[Spanish Civil War]]. The province of Murcia supported the [[Popular Front (Spain)|Popular Front]] (the governing party in that era). The port of Cartagena became the main base of the Republican navy and was home to destroyer, cruiser, and submarine fleets. Thus, the Region of Murcia was of geostrategic importance during the war. To defend Cartagena, there were anti-aircraft bases throughout the region. The region was not near the frontlines and overall it was not attacked, except from the air against Cartagena and Águilas. Large factories, basic services, and some other properties were seized by trade unions. There was an impoverishment among the inhabitants and a lack of food supplies. Consequently, rationing was established in the region.<ref>{{cite web|title=La Guerra Civil-El Alzamiento Nacional – Región de Murcia Digital|url=https://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=c,373,m,1915&r=ReP-27045-DETALLE_REPORTAJES|last=Gómez-Guillamón Buendía|first=Antonio|language=es|access-date=2020-05-20}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|title=El plan de defensa de la provincia de Murcia – Región de Murcia Digital|url=https://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=c,373,m,1915&r=ReP-27299-DETALLE_REPORTAJES|language=es|access-date=2020-05-20}}</ref>

Under [[Francoist Spain]], wine agriculture and economic activities increased in the Altiplano {{Lang|es|comarca}} (north of the region).<ref>{{cite web|title=El franquismo-La autarquía (años 40): la creación de la Academia General del Aire|url=https://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=c,373,m,1915&r=ReP-27404-DETALLE_REPORTAJES|last=Gómez-Guillamón Buendía|first=Antonio|language=es|access-date=2020-05-29}}</ref> An oil refinery infrastructure was established in Cartagena in 1942, and power refineries, supply refineries, and factories were constructed in the same area during the 1950s and 1960s.<ref>{{cite web|title=Barrios y Diputaciones I Historia I Tu Ciudad I Ayuntamiento de Cartagena|url=https://www.cartagena.es/barrios_diputaciones.asp|last=Gómez Vizcaíno|first=Juan Antonio|language=es|access-date=2020-05-29}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|title=La ruptura del aislamiento internacional (años 50): la Refineria de Petróleos de Escombreras|url=https://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=c,373,m,1915&r=ReP-27460-DETALLE_REPORTAJES|last=Gómez-Guillamón Buendía|first=Antonio|language=es|access-date=2020-05-29}}</ref>

Murcia became an autonomous region in 1982.

Massive riots erupted in Cartagena in 1992 protesting against the closing down of shipbuilding, mining and chemical companies and the regional legislature building was set on fire.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.publico.es/culturas/revuelta-obrera-hizo-arder-parlamento-murciano.html|website=[[Público (Spain)|Público]]|date=18 February 2022|title=La revuelta obrera que hizo arder el Parlamento murciano|first=Juan|last=Losa}}</ref>

The Region of Murcia had a population of 1,511,251 inhabitants at the start of 2020,(INE 2021, [[Instituto Nacional de Estadística (Spain)|National Statistic Institute of Spain]]) of which almost a third (30.4%) live in the municipality of Murcia, and nearly another sixth (14.3%) live in the municipality of [[Cartagena, Spain|Cartagena]] along the south coast. It makes up 3.0% of the Spanish population. In addition, after [[Ceuta]] and [[Melilla]], Murcia has the highest population growth (5.52 per thousand inhabitants) and the highest birth rate in the country.

* Birth rate (2004): 13.00 per 1,000
* Mortality rate (2004): 7.48 per 1,000
* Life expectancy (2002):
** Men: 76.01 years
** Women: 82.00 years

In the 1991–2005 period, the Murcian population grew by 26.06%, as opposed to the national average of 11.85%. 12.35% of the inhabitants are of foreign origin, according to the INE 2005 census, which is 4% more than the Spanish average. The most notable groups of immigrants are Ecuadorians (33.71% of all foreigners), Moroccans (27.13%), Britons (5.95%), Bolivians (4.57%), and Colombians (3.95%).

[[Roman Catholicism]] is, by far, the largest religion in the Region of Murcia. In 2019, 80.1% of Murcians identified themselves as Roman Catholic.<ref name=CIS2019Murcia />

===Language===
The Spanish spoken in the region has its own accent and local vocabulary. The [[Murcian Spanish|Murcian]] dialect is one of the southern dialects of Spanish and tends to eliminate many syllable-final consonants and emphasizes regional vocabulary, much of which is derived from [[Aragonese language|Aragonese]], [[Catalan language|Catalan]], and [[Arabic language|Arabic]] words. The general intonation and some of the distinctive vocabulary of the Murcian dialect share several traits with the dialects spoken in the neighboring [[province of Almería]], north of [[province of Granada|Granada]], and the [[Vega Baja del Segura]], in the [[province of Alicante|Alicante province]].<ref>{{cite web |last=Gómez Ortín |first=Francisco |title=El Dialecto Murciano y Sus Variedades |url=https://www.um.es/tonosdigital/znum8/portada/monotonos/03-GORTIN.pdf |language=es |access-date=8 May 2021}}</ref>

The [[Valencian language]] is spoken in a small area of the region known as El [[Carche]].<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.lavanguardia.com/local/valencia/20190826/463814427218/carche-murcia-valenciano.html|title=La Murcia donde se habla valenciano|date=26 August 2019|language=es|access-date=2019-12-08}}</ref>

==Transport==

===Road===
The region's highway network provides connectivity along the coast, with three highway links with Andalusia ([[Autovía A-91]], [[Autovía A-7]], and the tolled [[Autopista AP-7]]) and another three with the Valencian Community (A-7 and the tolled AP-7 and Autopista AP-37), but only the [[Autovía A-30]] connects Murcia with inland Spain. It is thus the goal of the regional government to provide alternative highway corridors that connect the interior to the coastal zones.

The autonomous government is investing heavily in its highway network, both for trips along the coast and inland–coast connectivity. Due to the expansion of the regional network that this effort is expected to produce, Murcia has recently implemented a new naming scheme for its regional highways, more in accordance with that of the national network. When the renaming is complete, all highways will be identified by white-on-blue names that start with RM (for {{Lang|es|Región de Murcia}}).

===Rail===
The [[Chinchilla–Cartagena railway]] provides the only rail route to Madrid from the region. The [[Cercanías Murcia/Alicante]] [[commuter rail]] network connects Murcia to Alicante, via [[Orihuela]] and [[Elche]], along with a branch to [[Águilas]].

The [[Madrid–Levante high-speed rail network]] is due to reach Murcia in 2021, and the [[Murcia–Almería high-speed rail line]] will connect the region to Almería by 2023.

===Air===
The [[Región de Murcia International Airport]] opened in 2019, replacing the [[Murcia San Javier Airport]] for passenger flights. It was used by a million passengers in its first year of operation. [[Alicante Airport]], although outside Murcia, is also used by air travellers from the region.

===Sea===
The [[Port of Cartagena]] is the region's only port. 60% of the region's exports and 80% of its imports go through the port.<ref>{{cite news |url=http://www.apc.es/actualidad.php?reg=18&not=279 |date=3 March 2015 |title=El puerto de Cartagena como infraestructura logística de apoyo a las empresas exportadoras |work=Autoridad Portuaria de Cartagena |access-date=21 March 2019 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20150615001305/http://www.apc.es/actualidad.php?reg=18&not=279 |archive-date=15 June 2015 |publisher=[[Ministerio de Fomento]] |agency=[[Gobierno de España]] |language=es}}</ref>

==Economy==
The [[Gross domestic product]] (GDP) of the autonomous region was 31.5 billion [[euro]]s in 2018, accounting for 2.6% of Spanish economic output. GDP per capita, adjusted for purchasing power, was 22,800 euros, or 76% of the [[2007 enlargement of the European Union|EU27]] average in the same year. The GDP per employee was 84% of the EU average.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://ec.europa.eu/eurostat/documents/2995521/10474907/1-05032020-AP-EN.pdf/81807e19-e4c8-2e53-c98a-933f5bf30f58|title=Regional GDP per capita ranged from 30% to 263% of the EU average in 2018|website=Eurostat}}</ref>

Agriculture, ranching, and fishing contributed 5.99% of Region of Murcia's [[Gross value added|Gross Value Added]] (GVA). Extraction industries, manufacturing industries, and several power supply activities constituted 18.32% of the GVA.<ref>{{cite web |title=CREM – Datos Básicos de la Región de Murcia – 2. Evolución del Producto Interior Bruto a precios de mercado y sus componentes (precios corrientes) |url=http://econet.carm.es/inicio/-/crem/sicrem/PU_datosBasicos/sec41.html |language=es |access-date=8 May 2021}}</ref> The tourism sector provided 11.4% of regional GDP in 2018.<ref>{{cite web|title=Plan estratégico turístico 2015–2019. Balance turístico de la Región de Murcia 2018.|url=https://www.murciaturistica.es/webs/murciaturistica/documentos/1/DOCUMENTOS_1_2272.pdf|page=12|language=es|access-date=2020-05-04}}</ref>

35.9% of the land in the region is given to arable farming. Major crops grown are oat, barley, lettuce, citrus fruits, peaches, almonds, apricots, olives, and grapes.<ref>{{cite web |title=CREM – Datos Básicos de la Región de Murcia – 1. Distribución general de la tierra.|url=http://econet.carm.es/inicio/-/crem/sicrem/PU_datosBasicos/sec48.html|language=es|access-date=2020-05-04}}</ref><ref>{{cite web |title=CREM – Datos Básicos de la Región de Murcia – 2. Evolución de la superficie y producción según tipo de cultivo.|url=http://econet.carm.es/inicio/-/crem/sicrem/PU_datosBasicos/sec49.html}}</ref> It is common to find Murcia's tomatoes and lettuce, lemons, and oranges in European supermarkets.<ref>{{cite web|title=Murcia is the leading Spanish fruit and vegetable exporter|url=https://www.freshplaza.com/article/2117481/murcia-is-the-leading-spanish-fruit-and-vegetable-exporter/|access-date=2021-01-08|website=Fresh Plaza|date=31 January 2014 }}</ref><ref>{{cite web|title=Murcian fruit and veg exports exceeded 2.5 million tons in 2019|url=https://www.freshplaza.com/article/9221717/murcian-fruit-and-veg-exports-exceeded-2-5-million-tons-in-2019/|access-date=2021-01-08|website=Fresh Plaza|date=2 June 2020 }}</ref> Murcia is a producer of wines, with about {{convert|29000|ha}} devoted to grape vineyards.<ref>{{cite web |title=CREM – Datos Básicos de la Región de Murcia – 2. Evolución de la superficie y producción según grupo de cultivo |url=https://econet.carm.es/web/crem/inicio/-/crem/sicrem/PU_datosBasicos/sec49.html |language=es}}</ref> Most of the vineyards are located in [[Jumilla]] and [[Yecla]].<ref>{{cite web|title=CREM – Datos Municipales Jumilla – 3. Evolución de la superficie dedicada a cada grupo de cultivo|url=https://econet.carm.es/web/crem/inicio/-/crem/sicrem/PU_JumillaCifras/P8004/sec4.html|website=Portal Estadístico de la Región de Murcia – CREM|language=es}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|title=CREM – Datos Municipales Yecla – 3. [[Human rights in Spain#Migrant agricultural workers in Southern Spain|Migrant workers are used]] in the agriculture industry.<ref>{{Cite news|last=|first=|date=2010-12-16|title=El 75% de trabajadores en la agricultura murciana son extranjeros|language=Spanish|work=La Opinión de Murcia|url=https://www.laopiniondemurcia.es/comunidad/2010/12/16/75-trabajadores-agricultura-murciana-son-extranjeros/290367.html|access-date=2021-01-15}}</ref> In regards to fishing sector, the most caught species are anchovies, round sardinellas, sardines, chub mackerels, gilt-head breams, and pompanoes. Aquaculture breeds Atlantic bluefin tuna, gilt-head breams, and sea bass.<ref>{{cite web|title=CREM – Datos Básicos de la Región de Murcia – 6. Murcia has some industry, with foreign companies choosing it as a location for factories, such as [[Henry Milward & Sons]] (which manufactures surgical and knitting needles) and American firms such as General Electric and Paramount Park Studios.

==Tourism==
Despite the famous seaside resorts, the Region of Murcia is relatively unknown even within Spain, so it continues to be relatively unspoilt compared to other, more overcrowded areas of the Spanish Mediterranean coast. Nevertheless, its more than 300 sunny days a year with an average temperature of {{convert|21|C}}, and the {{convert|250|km}} of beaches of the so-called [[Costa Cálida]] (Warm Coast) have attracted tourists for decades.

The region is also being promoted as a cultural destination with many highlights for visitors: monuments, gastronomy, cultural events, museums, historic remains, festivals, etc. The region is one of the Spanish autonomous communities that have grown the most in the last few years, and this has conferred on it the character of an ideal destination for services, shopping, cultural events, and conventions.

===Cultural tourism===
[[Image:Catedral de Santa María I.JPG|thumb|upright|Murcia Cathedral.]]
[[Image:Castillo_de_Lorca1.jpg|thumb|upright|Castle of Lorca]]

====Major tourist destinations====
The most visited towns are:<ref>{{cite web|title=BALANCE TURÍSTICO DE LA REGIÓN DE MURCIA 2017|url=https://www.murciaturistica.es/webs/murciaturistica/documentos/1/DOCUMENTOS_1_2132.pdf|page=73|language=es|access-date=2020-05-04}}</ref>
* [[Murcia]], the capital city, offers the facilities of a large city. It is the seventh-largest Spanish city by population with approximately 440,000 inhabitants in 2009. Murcia's sights include its famous [[Cathedral of Murcia|cathedral]] with its {{convert|90|m|ft|adj=on}} tall bell tower. Murcia is also a large university town with more than 30,000 students per year. It has more than 2 million m<sup>2</sup> of parks and gardens. Murcia has a rich history tied to the Jewish community.
* [[Cartagena (Spain)|Cartagena]] is the region's second-largest city and one of the main Spanish naval bases. Sights include its recently restored Roman Theatre (among numerous Roman remains), a number of modernist buildings and many military fortifications.
* [[Lorca, Spain|Lorca]] is a large medieval town at the foothills upon which its famous castle stands. It is the second-largest municipality of Spain in area.
* [[Caravaca de la Cruz]], or simply Caravaca, is one of the five official Holy cities for Catholicism since it is claimed to house part of the [[Lignum Crucis]], the Holy Cross.

====The castles itinerary====
The interior of the Region of Murcia has plenty of castles and fortifications that show the importance of these frontier lands between the Christian [[Castile (historical region)|Castile]] and the Muslim [[Al-Andalus|Andalusia]]. They include:
* Castle of [[Jumilla]], a former Roman fortification turned by the Moors into an [[Alcazaba]]. The Castilian kings and the [[Marquess of Villena|marquesses of Villena]] gave it its appearance of a Gothic royal residence.
* Castle of [[Moratalla]], one of the largest castles of the province, built to defend the town of Moratalla from invaders from the nearby Muslim [[Kingdom of Granada]].
* Castle of [[Mula, Spain|Mula]], of Muslim origin, but as with many castles, eventually restored and renovated.
* Royal Alcázar of [[Caravaca de la Cruz]], where the Holy sanctuary was built, also of Moorish origin, conquered by the Christians and finally home to several noble families.
* Concepción Castle, in [[Cartagena (Spain)|Cartagena]], built on one of the five hills of the old Cartagena, following the Roman taste. Now it is home of the Centre for the Interpretation of the History of Cartagena.
* [[Lorca Castle]], also known as the Fortress of the Sun.

====Festivals====
Cartagena's and Lorca's [[Holy Week|Holy Week processions]] have been declared of International Tourist Interest,<ref>{{cite web|title=Semana Santa de Cartagena: Web oficial turismo Región de Murcia|url=https://www.murciaturistica.es/es/evento/semana-santa-de-cartagena-M424152/|language=es|access-date=2020-05-30}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|title=Semana Santa de Lorca : Web oficial turismo Región de Murcia|url=https://www.murciaturistica.es/es/evento/semana-santa-de-lorca-M424163/|language=es|access-date=2020-05-30}}</ref> together with Murcia's {{Lang|es|Bando de la Huerta}} and "[[The Burial of the Sardine in Murcia]]", included in its spring festivities.<ref>{{cite web|title=Entierro de la Sardina : Web oficial turismo Región de Murcia|url=https://www.murciaturistica.es/es/evento/entierro-de-la-sardina-M424175/|language=es|access-date=2020-05-30}}</ref> Murcia's Holy Week is also interesting since its processions include statues by Murcian sculptor [[Francisco Salzillo]].

Cartagena's main festivities are the {{Lang|es|Carthagineses y Romanos}}, re-enacting the Punic Wars. They have been declared of National Tourist Interest.<ref>{{cite web|title=Fiestas de Carthagineses y Romanos: Web oficial de turismo de la Región de Murcia|url=https://www.murciaturistica.es/es/evento/fiestas-de-carthagineses-y-romanos--M425245/|language=es|access-date=2020-05-29}}</ref>

Águila's [[Carnival]] is one of the most important and colourful in Spain.<ref>{{cite web|title=Carnaval de Águilas : Official Murcia Region tourist site|url=https://www.murciaturistica.es/en/event/carnaval-de-aguilas-M424133/|last=Murcia|first=Instituto de Turismo de la Región de|website=murciaturistica.es|language=en|access-date=2020-05-29}}</ref>

===Beaches and golf===
[[Image:La Manga del Mar Menor2.jpg|thumb|upright|La Manga del Mar Menor]]

The {{Lang|es|Costa Cálida}} has {{convert|250|km}} of beaches, from El Mojón, in the north near [[Province of Alicante|Alicante]], to [[Águilas]], in southwest Murcia near [[Province of Almería|Almería]].

One of the major destinations of Murcia is the ''[[Mar Menor]]'' or Small Sea, located on the [[Mediterranean]]. It is the largest natural lake in Spain and the largest salty lagoon in [[Europe]].<ref>{{cite web|title=Mar Menor – Región de Murcia Digital|url=https://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=c,365,m,2584|language=es|access-date=2020-05-30}}</ref> It is separated from the Mediterranean by a {{convert|22|km|adj=on}} long narrow sandy strip known as ''[[La Manga del Mar Menor]]'' or simply {{Lang|es|La Manga}}.<ref>{{cite web|title=La Manga – Región de Murcia Digital|url=https://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=a,681|language=es|access-date=2020-05-30}}</ref> It is probably the most developed and overcrowded holiday area of Murcia, despite being declared one of the [[Specially Protected Areas of Mediterranean Importance]] (SPAMI) by the [[United Nations]].

Mar Menor's muds are famous for their therapeutic properties.<ref>{{cite web|title=Mar Menor Muds : Official Murcia Region tourist site|url=https://www.murciaturistica.es/en/mar_menor_muds/|last=Murcia|first=Instituto de Turismo de la Región de|website=murciaturistica.es|language=en|access-date=2020-05-30}}</ref> Apart from Mar Menor, the Murcian coast from Cartagena to the frontier with [[Andalusia]] alternates between wild and unspoilt rocky areas, large sandy beaches, and the towns of [[Mazarrón]] and [[Águilas]].<ref>{{cite web|title=Playas en Mazarrón : Web oficial turismo Región de Murcia|url=https://www.murciaturistica.es/es/playas/mazarron/|language=es|access-date=2020-05-30}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|title=Águilas : Official Murcia Region tourist site|url=https://www.murciaturistica.es/en/aguilas/|last=Murcia|first=Instituto de Turismo de la Región de|website=murciaturistica.es|language=en|access-date=2020-05-30}}</ref>

The needs of tourism have forced the area to add all kinds of facilities and services. A construction boom resulted in a huge number of estates, including the controversial holiday resorts of [[Polaris World]], second residences, and numerous malls. 
Thanks to the [[orography]] and climate of the region, these lands are suitable for golf courses, a fact that has been very controversial because of the need for water, which Murcia lacks, being a very dry region.

Other services include adventure tourism companies, tourist routes, guided visits, yacht facilities, nautical excursions, and sports federations.

Golf, and in particular golf tourism, has become important to the economy and draws visitors from around the world, particularly the United States, [[United Kingdom]], [[Scandinavia]], and Germany.<ref>{{Cite journal|year=2015|title=Estudio de productos. Costa Cálida – Verano 2014|url=https://www.murciaturistica.es/webs/murciaturistica/planes/doc_2_6_3_1_1_5.pdf|journal=Turismo en la Región de Murcia. Estudios y estadísticas|language=es|publisher=Instituto de turismo Región de Murcia|page=7}}</ref> Unlike other parts of Europe, especially northern Europe, the weather in high season can almost be guaranteed to be dry and sunny. This has led to the creation of specialist golf holidays<ref>{{Cite web |url=http://www.golfstash-holidays.com/ |title=GolfStash Holidays &#124; Golf Breaks Spain |access-date=2021-01-10 |archive-date=2021-01-25 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20210125232043/https://golfstash-holidays.com/ |url-status=dead }}</ref> to bring in visitors from April to June and September to November, especially. Unlike in other parts of the country, [[golf course]]s are quieter in July and August due to the extreme heat.

===Natural resources and rural tourism===
The Region of Murcia has 19 areas under different statutes of environmental protection, representing 6% of its territory.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=c,365,m,1039&r=ReP-3355-DETALLE_REPORTAJESPADRE|title=Espacios naturales – Región de Murcia Digital|language=es|access-date=2019-12-09}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=c,365,m,1039&r=ReP-3410-DETALLE_REPORTAJESPADRE|title=Reservas y Espacios Naturales – Región de Murcia Digital|language=es}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.regmurcia.com/servlet/s.Sl?sit=c,365,m,1039&r=ReP-3365-DETALLE_REPORTAJESPADRE|title=Paisajes Protegidos – Región de Murcia Digital|language=es|access-date=2019-12-09}}</ref>

[[Image:EZEQUIELA001.jpg|thumb|upright|right|San Pedro's marsh]]
[[Image:Cañon almadenes fsalmeron.jpg|thumb|upright|right|Almadenes Canyon]]

* The [[Sierra Espuña]] – a protected natural space of {{convert|17804|ha}} in area. It is located on the [[Cordillera Bética|Baetic Cordillera]] within the basin of the [[Segura River]]. This regional park is centred around the {{convert|1583|m|adj=on}} [[Penibaetic System|Sierra Espuña]] mountain. It has been declared a [[Special Protection Area]] for birds.<ref>{{cite web |title=Zonas de Especial Protección para las Aves (ZEPA) |trans-title=Special Protection Areas for Birds |url=https://www.miteco.gob.es/es/biodiversidad/temas/espacios-protegidos/red-natura-2000/zepa.aspx |website=[[Ministry of Environment (Spain)|Ministry of Environment]] |language=es |access-date=30 March 2021}}</ref> 
* {{ill|Salinas y Arenales de San Pedro del Pinatar|es}} – a salt marsh by the Mar Menor.
* {{ill|Parque regional de Cabo Cope y Puntas de Calnegre|es}}, between [[Águilas]] and [[Lorca, Spain|Lorca]], by the Mediterranean sea. The regional government attempted to amend Law 1/2001 of 24 April on Land in the Region of Murcia, to declassify a total of {{convert|1600|ha}} of the land protected by the regional park, but the attempt was annulled by the [[Constitutional Court of Spain]]. Rare species of animals (Bonelli's eagle, Greek tortoise, martingale) and plants are threatened.
* [[Calblanque Regional Park]] – between La Manga and [[Cartagena (Spain)|Cartagena]], has beaches that are favoured by Murcians, although it is an undeveloped area.
* {{ill|Carrascoy y el Valle|es}} – a Special Protection Area and [[Site of Community Importance]] (SCI).
* {{ill|Sierra de la Pila (Región de Murcia)|lt=Sierra de la Pila|es}} – is also a Special Protection Area.
* {{ill|Sierra del Carche|es}} – also part of the Baetic Cordillera. It is in the north of the region, in the Yecla and Jumilla municipalities.<ref>{{cite web|title=Presidencia – Murcia Natural|url=http://www.borm.es/borm/documento?obj=anu&id=343011|language=es|access-date=2020-05-04}}</ref>
* {{ill|Cañón de Almadenes|es}} – a Special Protection Area on the Segura River.
* {{ill|Humedal del Ajuaque y Rambla Salada|es}} – another wetland and Special Protection Area.
* {{ill|Cabezo Gordo|lt=Cerro de Cabezo Gordo|es}} – contains the [[Sima de las Palomas]] archaeological site, a cave where the second oldest human remains in the Iberian Peninsula were found.
* {{ill|Sierra de la Muela, Cabo Tiñoso y Roldán|es}} –  coastal mountains in the south of the region and in Cartagena municipality.<ref>{{cite web|title=OISMA – Sierra de la Muela-Cabo Tiñoso y Roldán|url=http://www.murcianatural.carm.es/web/guest/sierra-de-la-muela-cabo-tinoso-y-roldan1|language=es|access-date=2020-05-04}}</ref>
* A group of islands and islets on the Murcian Mediterranean that are of ecological importance.
* {{ill|Espacios abiertos e islas del Mar Menor|es}} – including five volcanic islands in the Mar Menor.
* {{ill|Sierra de las Moreras|es}} – is a mountain range that occupies part of [[Mazarrón]] municipality in the south of the region. It is a Site of Community Importance.<ref>{{cite web|title=OISMA – LIC ES6200011 Sierra de las Moreras – Visor Contenidos|url=http://www.murcianatural.carm.es/web/guest/visor-contenidos-dinamicos?artId=81781|language=es|access-date=2020-05-03}}</ref>
* {{ill|Sotos y Bosque de la ribera de Cañaverosa|es}}
* {{ill|Sierra de Salinas (Murcia)|lt=Sierra de Salinas|es|Sierra de Salinas}}
* {{ill|Barrancos de Gebas|es}}
* {{ill|Saladares del Guadalentín|es}}
* {{ill|Cuatro Calas|es}}

The interior of the region, near the historical towns of [[Caravaca de la Cruz]] and [[Moratalla]], offers a number of rural accommodations and facilities, including cottages, farmhouses, country houses, and campsites. Visitors can engage in sports, day trips, and sightseeing excursions.
