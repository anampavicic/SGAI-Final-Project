'''Al-Qurnah''' ('''Kurnah''' or '''Qurna''', meaning connection/joint in [[Arabic]]) is a town in southern [[Iraq]] about 74 km northwest of [[Basra]], that lies within the conglomeration of Nahairat.  Qurna is located at the [[confluence]] point of the [[Tigris]] and [[Euphrates]] rivers to form the [[Shatt al-Arab]] waterway.<ref name="atlastours.net">{{Cite web |url=http://www.atlastours.net/iraq/qurna.html |title=Qurna, Iraq |access-date=2007-12-12 |archive-date=2007-11-12 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20071112044150/http://www.atlastours.net/iraq/qurna.html |url-status=live }}</ref> Local folklore holds Qurnah to have been the original site of biblical paradise, the [[Garden of Eden]], and location of the [[Tree of the knowledge of good and evil|Tree of Knowledge]].  

==History==
[[File:Confluence of the Tigris and Euphrates near Al-Qurnah.tif|left|thumb|View of Al-Qurnah in 1885 (Ridpath) ]]
Local folklore holds Qurnah to have been the site of the [[Garden of Eden]] and the location of a city built by general [[Seleucus I Nicator|Seleucus Nicator]] I.<ref>{{Cite web|title=Qurna, Iraq|url=http://www.atlastours.net/iraq/qurna.html|access-date=2021-04-15|website=www.atlastours.net|archive-date=2020-06-26|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20200626023741/http://www.atlastours.net/iraq/qurna.html|url-status=live}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|title=Seleucus I Nicator {{!}} Seleucid ruler|url=https://www.britannica.com/biography/Seleucus-I-Nicator|access-date=2021-05-21|website=Encyclopedia Britannica|language=en}}</ref>  An ancient tree is celebrated locally and shown to the tourists as the actual [[Tree of the knowledge of good and evil|Tree of Knowledge]] of the [[Bible]].<ref>{{Cite web|title=The Tree of Knowledge|url=http://www.atlasobscura.com/places/tree-knowledge|access-date=2021-04-11|website=Atlas Obscura|language=en|archive-date=2021-06-02|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20210602025544/https://www.atlasobscura.com/places/tree-knowledge|url-status=live}}</ref>  The tree died some time ago and replacement trees were planted. The [[Ezra's Tomb|tomb]] of [[Ezra]] is also described to be nearby and found further upstream on the river Tigris.<ref>{{Cite web|last=Yigal Schleifer|title=History|url=http://www.dangoor.com/issue76/articles/76056.htm|url-status=live|access-date=2021-04-11|website=www.dangoor.com|archive-date=2016-03-03|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160303212636/http://www.dangoor.com/issue76/articles/76056.htm}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|date=2014-08-13|title=TOMB OF EZRA|url=https://thecompletepilgrim.com/tomb-ezra/|access-date=2021-04-11|website=The Complete Pilgrim - Religious Travel Sites|language=en-US|archive-date=2021-04-11|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20210411152057/https://thecompletepilgrim.com/tomb-ezra/|url-status=live}}</ref>

In 1855, Al Qurnah was the site of the [[Qurnah Disaster]], in which local tribes attacked and sank a convoy of a ship and rafts carrying 240 cases of antiquities discovered by Victor Place's mission to [[Dur-Sharrukin|Khorsabad]], [[Sir Henry Rawlinson, 1st Baronet|Rawlinson]]'s to [[Nineveh|Kuyunjik]] and [[Fulgence Fresnel|Fresnel]]'s to [[Babylon]].<ref name=":0">Larsen, M.T., ''The Conquest of Assyria: Excavations in an Antique Land,'' Routledge, 2014, pp 344-9</ref><ref>{{Cite journal|last=Potts|first=D. T.|title=Potts 2020. 'Un coup terrible de la fortune:' A. Clément and the Qurna disaster of 1855. Pp. 235-244 in Finkel, I.L. and Simpson, St J., eds. In Context: The Reade Festschrift. Oxford: Archaeopress.|url=https://www.academia.edu/44717694|language=en|journal=|access-date=2021-04-11|archive-date=2021-04-11|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20210411152055/https://www.academia.edu/44717694/Potts_2020_Un_coup_terrible_de_la_fortune_A_Cl%C3%A9ment_and_the_Qurna_disaster_of_1855_Pp_235_244_in_Finkel_I_L_and_Simpson_St_J_eds_In_Context_The_Reade_Festschrift_Oxford_Archaeopress|url-status=live}}</ref>   The loss of priceless antiquities was a notable disaster for those researching the antiquities of the region.<ref name=":0" /><ref>{{Cite journal |last=Genç |first=Bülent |date=October 2021 |title=Memory of destroyed Khorsabad, Victor Place, and the story of a shipwreck |url=https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/journal-of-the-royal-asiatic-society/article/abs/memory-of-destroyed-khorsabad-victor-place-and-the-story-of-a-shipwreck/372290C7774F15E7E9D4D04331EAEFDE |journal=Journal of the Royal Asiatic Society |language=en |volume=31 |issue=4 |pages=759–774 |doi=10.1017/S135618632100016X |issn=1356-1863}}</ref>  Subsequent efforts to recover antiquities lost in the [[Dur-Sharrukin#The Qurnah Disaster|Qurnah Disaster]], including a Japanese expedition in 1971-2, were largely unsuccessful.<ref>Namio Egami, "The Report of The Japan Mission For The Survey of Under-Water Antiquities At Qurnah: The First Season," (1971–72), 1-45, https://www.jstage.jst.go.jp/article/orient1960/8/0/8_0_1/_pdf {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20181031234121/https://www.jstage.jst.go.jp/article/orient1960/8/0/8_0_1/_pdf |date=2018-10-31 }}.</ref>
[[File:Place V 1867 III Plate 43 6 (extract2).jpg|left|thumb|River rafts loaded with antiquities floating down River Tigris (V Place, 1867)]]
The town experienced the [[Battle of Qurna]] during the [[Mesopotamian Campaign]] of [[World War I]], when the British defeated Ottoman troops who had retreated from Basra in 1914.<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Reynolds|first=B. T.|date=1937|title=The Battle of Qurna|url=https://www.jstor.org/stable/44563856|journal=The Military Engineer|volume=29|issue=164|pages=101–106|jstor=44563856 |issn=0026-3982|access-date=2021-04-09|archive-date=2021-06-06|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20210606163603/https://www.jstor.org/stable/44563856|url-status=live}}</ref> The Battle of Qurna secured the British [[front line]] in Southern [[Mesopotamia]], thereby protecting [[Basra]] and the oil refineries at [[Abadan, Iran|Abadan]] in Persia (now Iran).<ref>A.J. Barker, ''The First Iraq War, 1914–1918, [https://books.google.com/books?id=EMrxLPQS5M4C Britain's Mesopotamian Campaign] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20210606163602/https://www.google.co.uk/books/edition/The_First_Iraq_War_1914_1918/EMrxLPQS5M4C?hl=en&gbpv=1 |date=2021-06-06 }},''(Enigma, New York, 2009; originally published in 1967 as The Bastard War(US)/The Neglected War(UK)), 36</ref>

==Recent developments==
[[File:Adam Tree.jpg|left|thumb|Al Qurnah is said to be location of the [[Tree of the knowledge of good and evil|Tree of Knowledge]].]]
The river front Qurnah Tourist Hotel was built during the [[Ba'athist Iraq|Ba'athist period]] to encourage tourism for the region.<ref>{{Cite web |url=http://www.commondreams.org/headlines03/0528-04.htm |title=War Takes Its Toll on the Garden of Eden |access-date=2007-12-12 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20071129234349/http://www.commondreams.org/headlines03/0528-04.htm |archive-date=2007-11-29 |url-status=dead }}</ref>  

[[Majnoon Island]] near Al-Qurnah is a center for oil production of the giant [[Majnoon oil field|Majnoon Oilfield]]. The area was built out of sand dunes and mud to create pathways for oil pipelines.{{cn|date=January 2023}}  The island was held by [[Islamic Republic of Iran Army|Iranian army]] during the [[Iran–Iraq War|Iran-Iraq war]] before Iraqi [[Chemical weapon|chemical weapons]] were deployed.<ref>{{Cite web|last=Regencia|first=Ted|title=Chemical attacks on Iran in the 1980s that US ignored|url=https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2018/4/19/chemical-attacks-on-iran-when-the-us-looked-the-other-way|access-date=2021-04-16|website=www.aljazeera.com|language=en|archive-date=2021-04-16|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20210416023814/https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2018/4/19/chemical-attacks-on-iran-when-the-us-looked-the-other-way|url-status=live}}</ref>
