'''Bodrum''' ({{IPA|tr|ËˆbodÉ¾um}}) is a small city and [[Districts of Turkey|district]] of [[MuÄŸla Province]], [[Turkey]].<ref>[https://www.e-icisleri.gov.tr/Anasayfa/MulkiIdariBolumleri.aspx BÃ¼yÃ¼kÅŸehir Ä°lÃ§e Belediyesi], Turkey Civil Administration Departments Inventory. Retrieved 19 September 2023.</ref> About 200 thousand people live in the district,<ref name="tuik" /> which covers 650&nbsp;km<sup>2</sup><ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.harita.gov.tr/uploads/files-folder/il_ilce_alanlari.xlsx|title=Ä°l ve Ä°lÃ§e YÃ¼z Ã¶lÃ§Ã¼mleri|publisher=General Directorate of Mapping|access-date=19 September 2023}}</ref> and includes the city. It is a port city at the entrance to the [[Gulf of GÃ¶kova]]. Known in ancient times as [[Halicarnassus]], the city was once home to the [[Mausoleum at Halicarnassus]], also known as the tomb of Mausolus, one of the [[Seven Wonders of the Ancient World]].

The city was founded by [[Dorians|Dorian Greeks]]. It later fell under [[Achaemenid Empire|Persian]] rule and became the capital city of the [[satrapy]] of [[Caria]]. [[Mausolus]] ruled Caria from here, and after his death in 353 BC, his wife [[Artemisia II of Caria|Artemisia]] built a tomb, called the Mausoleum, for him. [[Macedonia (ancient kingdom)|Macedonian]] forces laid [[Siege of Halicarnassus|siege to the city]] and captured it in 334 BC. After [[Alexander the Great|Alexander]]'s death, the city passed to successive [[Hellenistic period|Hellenistic]] rulers and was briefly an independent kingdom until 129 BC, when it came under [[Asia (Roman province)|Roman rule]]. A series of natural disasters and repeated pirate attacks wreaked havoc on the area, and the city lost its importance by the time of the [[Byzantine Empire|Byzantine era]]. The [[Knights Hospitaller]] arrived in 1402 and used the remains of the Mausoleum as a quarry to build [[Bodrum Castle]]. After the [[Siege of Rhodes (1522)|conquest of Rhodes]] by [[Suleiman the Magnificent]] in 1522, the town fell under [[Ottoman Empire|Ottoman control]] as the Knights Hospitaller relocated to Europe.

By the 20th century, the city's economy was mainly based on fishing and [[sponge diving]], but tourism has become the main industry in Bodrum since the late 20th century. The abundance of visitors has also contributed to Bodrum's retail and service industry. [[Milasâ€“Bodrum Airport]] and [[Kos International Airport]] are the main airports that serve the city. The port has ferries to other nearby Turkish and Greek ports and islands, [[Kos]] being the most important. Most of the public transportation in the city is based on local [[share taxi]]s and buses.

==History==

=== Ancient era ===
{{See also|Halicarnassus}}
Halicarnassus ({{langx|grc|á¼‰Î»Î¹ÎºÎ±ÏÎ½á¾±ÏƒÏƒÏŒÏ‚|HalikarnassÃ³s}}, or {{lang|grc|á¼ˆÎ»Î¹ÎºÎ±ÏÎ½Î±ÏƒÏƒÏŒÏ‚}} ''AlikarnassÃ³s''; {{langx|tr|Halikarnas}}), was an [[ancient Greece|ancient Greek]] city at the site of modern Bodrum in [[Turkey]]. Halicarnassus was founded by [[Dorians|Dorian]] Greeks, and the figures on its coins, such as the head of [[Medusa (mythology)|Medusa]], [[Athena]], [[Poseidon]], and the [[trident]], support the statement that the mother cities were [[Troezen]] and [[Argos, Peloponnese|Argos]].<ref>{{cite EB1911 |wstitle=Halicarnassus |volume=12 |page=837 |first=David George |last=Hogarth}}</ref> The inhabitants appear to have accepted [[Anthas|Anthes]], a son of Poseidon, as their legendary founder, as mentioned by [[Strabo]], and were proud of the title ''Antheadae''. The Carian name for Halicarnassus has been tentatively identified with AlosÎ´karnosÎ´ in inscriptions.

In an early period, Halicarnassus was a member of the [[Doric Hexapolis]], which included [[Kos]], [[Cnidus]], [[Lindos]], [[Kameiros]], and [[Ialysus]]; but it was expelled from the league when one of its citizens, Agasicles, took home the prize tripod that he had won in the Triopian games instead of dedicating it according to custom to the Triopian [[Apollo]]. In the early 5th century, Halicarnassus was under the sway of [[Artemisia I of Caria]] (also known as Artemesia of Halicarnassus<ref>{{cite EB1911 |wstitle=Artemisia (daughter of Lygdamis) |volume=2 |page=665 }}</ref>), who made herself famous as a naval commander at the [[battle of Salamis]]. Little is known of Pisindalis, her son and successor; but [[Lygdamis II of Halicarnassus|Lygdamis]], the [[tyrant]] of Halicarnassus, who next attained power, is notorious for having the poet [[Panyasis]] put to death and forcing [[Herodotus]], possibly the most well-known Halicarnassian, to leave his native city ({{Circa|457 BC}}).<ref>{{cite book|title=Suda|chapter=Herodotus|chapter-url=http://www.stoa.org/sol-bin/search.pl?db=REAL&search_method=QUERY&login=guest&enlogin=guest&user_list=LIST&page_num=1&searchstr=herodotus&field=hw_eng|title-link=Suda}} At the Suda On Line Project.</ref>

Under the Persians, it was the capital city of the [[satrapy]] of [[Caria]], the region that long afterward constituted its hinterland and of which it was the principal port. Its strategic location ensured that the city enjoyed considerable autonomy. Archaeological evidence from the period, such as the recently discovered [[Salmacis (Caria)|Salmakis]] (''Kaplankalesi'') Inscription, now in [[Bodrum Museum of Underwater Archaeology]], attests to the particular pride its inhabitants had developed.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.uni-koeln.de/phil-fak/ifa/zpe/downloads/1998/123pdf/123001.pdf |title = The Pride of Halicarnassus|author=Signe Isager|publisher=Zeitschrift fÃ¼r Papyrologie und Epigraphik, 123 p. 1-23|year=1998}}</ref>

[[Mausolus]] ruled Caria from here, nominally on behalf of the Persians but practically independently, for much of his reign from 377 to 353 BC. When he died in 353 BC, [[Artemisia II of Caria]], who was both his sister and his widow, employed the [[Ancient Greece|ancient Greek]] [[Architecture|architects]] [[Satyros]], [[Pythius of Priene|Pythis]], and the [[Sculpture|sculptors]] [[Bryaxis]], [[Scopas]], [[Leochares]], and [[Timotheus (sculptor)|Timotheus]] to build a monument and a tomb for him. The word "[[mausoleum]]" derives from the structure of this tomb. It was a temple-like structure decorated with reliefs and statuary on a massive base. Today only the foundations and a few pieces of sculpture remain.

=== Hellenistic and Roman periods ===
[[Alexander the Great]] laid [[Siege of Halicarnassus|siege to the city]] after his arrival in the Carian lands and, together with his ally, Queen [[Ada of Caria]], captured it after fighting in 334 BC. After Alexander's death, the rule of the city passed to [[Antigonus I Monophthalmus|Antigonus I]] (311 BC), [[Lysimachus]] (after 301 BC), and the [[Ptolemaic dynasty|Ptolemies]] (281â€“197 BC) and was briefly an independent kingdom until 129 BC, when it came under Roman rule. A series of earthquakes destroyed much of the city, as well as the great Mausoleum, while repeated pirate attacks from the Mediterranean wreaked further havoc on the area. By the time of the early Christian [[Byzantine Empire|Byzantine]] era, when Halicarnassus was an important bishopric, there was little left of the shining city of Mausoluos.<ref>[https://www.worldhistory.org/halicarnassus/ worldhistory.org]</ref>

=== Medieval era ===
[[File:Bodrum_-_panoramio_(10).jpg|thumb|[[Bodrum Castle]] coat of arms|alt=|left]]Crusader Knights arrived in 1402 and used the remains of the Mausoleum as a quarry to build the still impressively standing [[Bodrum Castle]] (''Castle of Saint Peter''), a well-preserved example of late Crusader architecture in the eastern Mediterranean. The [[Knights Hospitaller]] (''Knights of St. John'') were given permission to build it by the Ottoman sultan [[Mehmed I]] after [[Tamerlane]] destroyed their previous fortress in [[Ä°zmir]]'s inner bay. {{Citation needed|date=April 2016}}

In 1522, [[Suleiman the Magnificent]] [[Siege of Rhodes (1522)|conquered the base of the Crusader knights]] on the island of [[Rhodes]], who then relocated first briefly to [[Sicily]] and later permanently to [[Malta]], leaving the Castle of Saint Peter and Bodrum to the [[Ottoman Empire]].

===Modern era===
Bodrum was a quiet town of fishermen and [[sponge diving|sponge divers]] until the early 20th century. From 1867 until 1922, it was part of the [[Aidin Vilayet]] of the [[Ottoman Empire]]. In the 1923 [[Population exchange between Greece and Turkey|population exchange]], the [[Anatolian Greeks|Greeks]] of Bodrum settled in [[Nea Alikarnassos]], Crete in exchange for Muslims of [[Crete]]. In her book ''Bodrum'', Fatma Mansur points out that the presence of a large community of bilingual [[Cretan Turks]], coupled with the conditions of free trade and access to the southern [[Dodecanese]] islands until 1935, made the town less provincial.<ref>{{cite book | title = Bodrum |isbn=90-04-03424-2|author= Fatma Mansur|publisher=[[Brill Publishers]]| year= 1972}}</ref> The fact that traditional agriculture was not a very rewarding activity in the rather dry peninsula also prevented the formation of a class of large landowners. Bodrum has no notable history of political or religious extremism. A first nucleus of intellectuals started to form after the 1950s around the writer [[Cevat Åakir KabaaÄŸaÃ§lÄ±]], who first came here in exile two decades previously and was charmed by the town to the point of adopting the [[pen name]] ''Halikarnas BalÄ±kÃ§Ä±sÄ±'' ('The Fisherman of Halicarnassus').<ref>''Bodream'', [[Jean-Pierre Thiollet]], Anagramme Ed., 2010, pp.62â€“66</ref>

== Main sights ==
[[File:Bodrum castle 3.JPG|thumb|350px|The [[Bodrum Castle|Castle of St. Peter]] was built by the [[Knights Hospitaller]].]]
The [[Bodrum Castle|Castle of St. Peter]], also known as Bodrum Castle, is one of the main attractions of the peninsula. The castle was built by the [[Knights Hospitaller]] during the 15th century, and the walls of the fortification contain pieces of the ruins of the Mausoleum, which was used as a source of construction materials. The Castle of Bodrum retains its original design and character of the Knights' period and reflects [[Gothic architecture]].<ref name=":7">{{cite web|url=https://whc.unesco.org/en/tentativelists/6121/|title=The Bodrum Castle|website=UNESCO World Heritage Centre|publisher=UNESCO|access-date=12 June 2018}}</ref> It also contains the [[Bodrum Museum of Underwater Archaeology]], a museum established by the Turkish government in 1962 to host the underwater discoveries of ancient shipwrecks in the [[Aegean Sea]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.bodrum-museum.com|title=Bodrum Museum|date=2010-03-23|publisher=Bodrum Museum|access-date=2012-06-26}}</ref> In 2016, the castle was included in the [[List of World Heritage Sites in Turkey (Tentative list)|Tentative list of World Heritage Sites in Turkey]].<ref name=":7" /> The castle has been under renovation since 2017, and only some parts of it are accessible to visitors.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.kenttv.net/bodrum-kalesi-ziyarete-acildi|title=Bodrum Kalesi Ziyarete AÃ§Ä±ldÄ±...|last=Codingest|date=2019-05-18|website=Kent TV|language=tr|access-date=2019-12-01}}</ref>

==Government==
The district of Bodrum is one of 957 in Turkey. It is in [[MuÄŸla Province]], which is part of the [[AydÄ±n Subregion|Aydin Subregion]], which, in turn, is part of the [[Aegean Region]]. Bodrum became a sub-district{{Clarify|date=September 2024}} of the Ottoman Empire in 1871 and a district of MuÄŸla Province in 1872. Bodrum Municipality operates with its 18 directorates and subsidiary units in the entire Bodrum Peninsula, which covers an area of 689&nbsp;km<sup>2</sup> and a coastline of 215&nbsp;km. The organizational structure of Bodrum Municipality is composed of the mayor, four deputy mayors, and 18 directorates.<ref name=":6">{{Cite web|url=https://bodrum.bel.tr/about-us.html|title=About us â€“ Bodrum Belediyesi|website=Bodrum Municipality|language=en|access-date=2019-09-01}}</ref>

Bodrum Municipality served as the sole district municipality in the Bodrum region for many years. With the increase in the population of the peninsula, a town municipality called Karatoprak ([[Turgutreis]]) was established in 1967. The increase in the population also led to the establishment of the [[Mumcular, Bodrum|Mumcular]] (1972), [[YalÄ±kavak]] (1989), and [[GÃ¼ndoÄŸan, Bodrum|GÃ¼ndoÄŸan]] Municipalities (1992).<ref name=":6" />

Following the new municipality law of 1999, many villages in Bodrum were turned into towns: [[Ortakent YahÅŸi, Bodrum|Ortakent-YahÅŸi]] with the integration of Ortakent and YahÅŸi villages, [[GÃ¶ltÃ¼rkbÃ¼kÃ¼]] with the integration of GÃ¶lkÃ¶y, and [[TÃ¼rkbÃ¼kÃ¼]] and [[YalÄ±, Bodrum|YalÄ±]] with the integration of YalÄ± and KÄ±zÄ±laÄŸaÃ§ villages. The same year, the municipalities of [[GÃ¼mÃ¼ÅŸlÃ¼k]], [[KonacÄ±k]], and [[Bitez]] were also founded, raising the number of municipalities in the Bodrum Peninsula to 11.<ref name=":6" />

After MuÄŸla Province received [[metropolitan municipality]] status, these town municipalities were abolished, and all towns across the province were integrated into the city of Bodrum. Since March 30, 2014, the peninsula has been governed as a sole municipality.<ref name=":6" />

==Economy==
[[File:YalikavakPalmarina.jpg|thumb|YalÄ±kavak Marina is a luxury port and shopping mall in YalÄ±kavak, Bodrum]]
[[File:Bodrummarina.jpg|thumb|Milta Bodrum Marina]]During the 20th century, the city's economy was mainly based on fishing and sponge diving. Even though naked sponge diving in the Aegean region can be traced back at least 3,000 years, modern sponge diving became prevalent in Bodrum after [[Kos#Turkish population|Koan]] and [[Cretan Turks|Cretan immigrants]] settled in the city in the early 1920s, after the [[population exchange between Greece and Turkey]].<ref name=":3">{{Cite web|url=https://bodrumdenizmuzesi.org/en/sponge-fishing/|title=Sponge-fishing â€“ Bodrum Maritime Museum|language=en-US|access-date=2019-08-31}}</ref> During its golden age between 1945 and 1965, about 150 boats engaged in sponge diving activities in Bodrum. However, sponge diseases, artificial sponge production, and a ban on sponge diving eventually ended this lucrative industry.<ref name=":3" />[[File:Turkey.Bodrum023.jpg|thumb|''[[Gulet]]'' type [[schooner]]s near Bodrum|alt=|left]]

[[File:Bodrum - 32010650707.jpg|thumb|White-washed stone houses in Bodrum]]
Over the years, tourism became a major activity and income source for local communities.<ref>{{Cite web |url=http://veryturkey.com/destination-info/bodrum/everything-about-bodrum |title=Bodrum |access-date=2014-03-14 |archive-date=2018-08-06 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20180806110554/http://veryturkey.com/destination-info/bodrum/everything-about-bodrum |url-status=dead }}</ref> The abundance of visitors has also enlivened Bodrum's retail and service industries. Leather goods, particularly for traditional woven sandals, are well-known products. Other traditional goods, such as tangerine-flavored [[Turkish delight]], [[nazar (amulet)|nazar amulets]], and handicrafts are popular souvenirs.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.bodrumlife.com.tr/detay.php?bolum=319#.XWfhWOgzbIU|title=Bodrum'da Hediyelikler Harika Bodrumlife SayÄ± 24 AÄŸu-2012|website=www.bodrumlife.com.tr|language=en|access-date=2019-08-29|archive-date=2020-07-27|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20200727034138/http://www.bodrumlife.com.tr/detay.php?bolum=319#.XWfhWOgzbIU|url-status=dead}}</ref>

Apart from small shopping facilities, the city hosts a few shopping malls, such as Midtown and Oasis. There are also marinas for yachts and small ships, such as Milta Bodrum Marina,<ref name=":2">{{Cite web|url=https://www.denizticaretodasi.org.tr/tr/sayfalar/turkiyedeki-marinalar|title=TÃ¼rkiye'deki Marinalar|website=www.denizticaretodasi.org.tr|access-date=2019-08-29}}</ref> D-Marin Turgutreis,<ref name=":2" /> and the award-winning YalÄ±kavak Marina.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://britishmarine.co.uk/News/2019/February/TYHA-awards-worlds-best-marinas-for-201819|title=2018/19 TYHA awards for the world's best marinas|website=britishmarine.co.uk|access-date=2019-08-29|archive-date=2019-08-29|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20190829144716/https://britishmarine.co.uk/News/2019/February/TYHA-awards-worlds-best-marinas-for-201819|url-status=dead}}</ref>

The [[Carian Trail]], which passes by Bodrum and the surrounding [[KÄ±zÄ±laÄŸaÃ§, Bodrum|KÄ±zÄ±laÄŸaÃ§]] and [[Pedasa]] ruins, attracts hikers from both inside and outside Turkey.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.hurriyet.com.tr/karya-yolu-nda-baharla-kucaklasma-22620379|title=Karya Yolu'nda baharla kucaklaÅŸma|last=Yedig|first=Serhan|website=www.hurriyet.com.tr|date=18 February 2013 |language=tr|access-date=2019-09-24}}</ref>

=== Architecture ===
[[File:Bodrum_AkÃ§abÃ¼k_Evleri_-_panoramio.jpg|thumb|left|Traditional white houses in Bodrum.]]Traditional Bodrum houses are characterized by their prismatic shapes, simple designs and locally sourced building materials like stone, wood, clay and cane.<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Aysel|first=Nezih|date=May 2003|title=Bodrum-Ortakent (MÃ¼sgebi)'de Geleneksel Ev Tipleri Ãœzerine Bir Ä°nceleme|url=https://dergipark.org.tr/en/download/article-file/209278|journal=Dergipark|volume=3|pages=17â€“18}}</ref> They also tend to have white dominated exterior walls with some blue parts (doors, windows).<ref name=":0">{{Cite web|url=https://www.aa.com.tr/tr/yasam/bodrumun-beyazi-hasereye-mavisi-nazara/894454|title=Bodrum'un beyazÄ± 'haÅŸereye' mavisi 'nazara'|website=www.aa.com.tr|access-date=2019-08-29}}</ref> The tradition of white-washed walls is associated with the bug and scorpion repellent properties of lime, which is found in white paint. It is also traditionally applied in most hot regions for heat-reflecting properties of white color. Blue is also believed by locals to protect against the malicious effects of envy (similar to the Anatolian belief [[Nazar (amulet)|Nazar]], originated in [[Mesopotamia]]).<ref name=":0" />

According to [[MuÄŸla]] Municipality, in order to acquire a building permit one has to agree to paint the walls of the new building white. Use of any paint other than white on the exterior walls of a building was officially banned by MuÄŸla Governor Temel KoÃ§aklar in 2006.<ref name=":1">{{Cite web|url=http://www.yapi.com.tr/haberler/bodrum-evlerinin-rengi-beyaz-olacak_46642.html|title=Bodrum Evlerinin Rengi Beyaz Olacak|website=www.yapi.com.tr|language=tr|access-date=2019-08-29}}</ref> This was implemented to protect the historical fabric and cultural identity of the city.<ref name=":1" />

==Transportation==
===Airports===
There are no civilian airports located in the district's borders and [[Milasâ€“Bodrum Airport]] and [[Kos Island International Airport]] are the main airports that serve the city. Milasâ€“Bodrum Airport is located {{convert|36|km}} northeast of Bodrum, with both domestic and international flights.<ref name="veryturkey1">{{cite web |url=http://veryturkey.com/destination-info/bodrum/everything-about-bodrum |title=BODRUM &#124; Place to Visit &#124; Things to Do &#124; Famous For |publisher=Very Turkey |access-date=2014-03-20 |archive-date=2018-08-06 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20180806110554/http://veryturkey.com/destination-info/bodrum/everything-about-bodrum |url-status=dead }}</ref> Kos Island International Airport, {{convert|70|km}} to the SW, located in [[Andimachia]], [[Greece]], accessible by boats from Bodrum across a {{convert|20|km}} stretch of the [[Aegean Sea]]. Luxury marina Yalikavak Marina near Bodrum was long in the hands of [[MÃ¼bariz Mansimov]], a Turkish-Azerbaijani oligarch and shipping magnate until Erdogan handed management over to [[Mehmet AÄŸar]], his former interior minister.<ref name="correctiv">{{Cite web |last=Can DÃ¼ndar |date=2024-08-13 |title=Wie ich ins Fadenkreuz eines Mafiabosses geriet |url=https://correctiv.org/aktuelles/tuerkei/2024/08/13/wie-ich-ins-fadenkreuz-eines-mafiabosses-geriet/ |access-date=2024-09-05 |website=correctiv.org |language=de-DE}}</ref> After 2022, the marina became a safe haven for fleeing Russian oligarchs, like [[Roman Abramovich]].<ref>{{Cite web |last=Ben Anthony Horton  |date=2022-03-22 |title=Turkey welcomes Abramovich's â‚¬900m superyacht into Bodrum harbour |url=https://www.euronews.com/travel/2022/03/22/turkey-welcomes-abramovich-s-900m-superyacht-into-bodrum-harbour |access-date=2024-09-05 |website=euronews |language=en}}</ref>

== Wildlife ==
[[Maquis shrubland|Maquis shrubland biome]], which is the typical vegetation of the Mediterranean climate, is widespread in Bodrum, especially near the coast. [[Forests in Turkey|Forests]] cover 61.3% of the district.<ref name=":4">{{Cite web|url=https://www.bodto.org.tr/content.php?lang=en&id=00038|title=Bodrum'un CoÄŸrafi Konumu â€“ Bodrum Chamber of Commerce, BODTO Web Portal|website=www.bodto.org.tr|access-date=2019-08-31}}</ref> Conifers such as pines, larches, stone pines, cedars and junipers are the dominant trees in the region.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.bodrum.net.tr/iklim-ve-bitki-ortusu|title=Bodrum â€“ Ä°klim ve Bitki Ã–rtÃ¼sÃ¼|website=bodrum.net.tr â€“ Bodrum|language=en-US|access-date=2019-08-31}}</ref> Forested areas are prone to [[Wildfires in Turkey|wildfires]], which are common in the district's history.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.hurriyetdailynews.com/forest-fires-in-bodrum-manavgat-contained-145619|title=Forest fires in Bodrum, Manavgat contained â€“ Turkey News|website=HÃ¼rriyet Daily News|date=8 August 2019 |language=en|access-date=2019-08-31}}</ref> 95% of forest fires in Turkey are believed to be caused by human activities and there are concerns that forests are deliberately being set on fire to enlarge the city. The ruling party [[AK Parti|AKP]] has been criticized in the media for giving building permits to construct new hotels on burnt and [[deforestation|deforested]] areas.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://teyit.org/fotograflarin-mugla-guvercinlikte-yangin-sonrasi-yapilasmaya-acilan-yerleri-gosterdigi-iddiasi/|title=FotoÄŸraflarÄ±n MuÄŸla GÃ¼vercinlik'te yangÄ±n sonrasÄ± yapÄ±laÅŸmaya aÃ§Ä±lan yerleri gÃ¶sterdiÄŸi iddiasÄ±|date=2019-04-16|website=teyit.org|language=tr|access-date=2019-08-31}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.ntv.com.tr/turkiye/bodrumda-cevre-cinayeti,vDkVhV8l0UG_dbD9h1nlkQ|title=Bodrum'da Ã§evre cinayeti|date=2011-11-24|website=NTV|language=tr|access-date=2019-08-31}}</ref>

Wild boars and foxes are prevalent in the area, as are other animals such as [[pygmy cormorant]]s, [[Dalmatian pelican]]s and [[lesser kestrel]]s. The region is also home to the endangered and internationally protected [[Mediterranean monk seal]].<ref name=":4" />
